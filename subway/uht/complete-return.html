Complete the return

If you are an affected owner of a residential property in Canada on December 31, you must file an Underused Housing Tax return for the calendar year.

If you own more than one residential property in Canada, you must file a separate return for each property. If you share ownership of a residential property, each of you must file a separate return for the property.

You must still file a return even if your ownership of the property qualifies for an exemption and you do not owe any tax.

Filling out the return

Follow the steps below to help you fill out the parts of your return.

What return form to use

  • UHT-2900, Underused Housing Tax Return and Election Form

  1. Part 1 - Information about the owner

    You must have a valid CRA tax identifier number to file your return.

    Tax identifier numbers

    The following tax identifier numbers may be used depending on the situation:

    • A social insurance number (SIN)
    • An individual tax number (ITN)
    • A temporary tax number (TTN)
    • A Canadian business number (BN) with an Underused Housing Tax (RU) program account identifier code

    Note: A trust account number (TAN) cannot be used to file Underused Housing Tax returns.

    Tax identifier numbers for individuals

    Depending on your citizenship, if you are an individual, you must file your return using either a SIN, an ITN, or a TTN.

    Canadian citizens or permanent residents

    You must use your SIN to file your return.

    If you do not already have a SIN, you must apply for one.

    Apply for a SIN

    Non-residents or temporary residents to Canada

    If you already have a SIN, you must use it to file your return.

    If you do not have a SIN, you must use your ITN or TTN to file your return.

    Non-residents (use an ITN)

    If you do not have an ITN, you must apply for one.

    Apply for an ITN

    If you do not have a valid ITN, and are concerned with meeting the filing deadline, you may be able to apply for one by filling out the application form (Form T1261) and attaching it with your completed return.

    Make sure to file your ITN application form, all required supporting documentation, and your Underused Housing Tax return together.

    If you are a corporation, you must file your return using a BN with an RU program account identifier code.

  2. Part 2 - Information about your residential property in Canada

    You must determine your ownership percentage, as well as the value of the residential property.

    Determine your ownership percentage

    If you are the only owner of the residential property, your ownership percentage is 100%.

    If you are one of several owners of the residential property, your ownership percentage is either of the following:

    • The percentage of your ownership as indicated in the land registration system
    • 100% divided by the number of owners, if no percentage is indicated in the land registration system
    Determine the value of the residential property

    There are two ways to determine the value of a residential property.

    The general rule is to use its taxable value.

    What is taxable value

    The taxable value of the residential property for a calendar year is the greater of the following amounts:

    • The value of the residential property determined by an authority who establishes the assessed value of property for property tax purposes
    • The residential property's most recent sale price, on or before December 31 of the calendar year

    For more detailed information, refer to: Calculating the tax payable using the taxable value

    If you want to use the residential property's fair market value instead, you must file a Fair market value (FMV) election.

  3. Part 3 - Multiple residential properties

    This part only applies to individuals who are not Canadian citizens or permanent residents, and who own more than one residential property.

    Section 1 - Multiple properties

    If you own more than one residential property in Canada on December 31, you must file a separate return for each property.

    Section 2 – Election to designate a residential property

    Complete this section if you answered yes for line 300, and no for line 310.

    Line 315: By answering yes, you are electing that you as the owner, designate the residential property (completed in part 2) as either a primary place of residence or for qualifying occupancy.

    You must fill out this section to be eligible for one of these 2 exemptions.

    Section 3 – Joint election to designate a residential property

    Complete this section if you answered yes for line 310.

    Line 320: By answering yes, you are electing that both you and your spouse or common-law partner, as the owners, designate the residential property (completed in part 2) as either a primary place of residence or for qualifying occupancy.

    You must fill out this section to be eligible for one of these 2 exemptions.

    To constitute a valid joint election, your spouse or common-law partner must elect to designate the same residential property for the calendar year in an UHT return for a residential property they own.

    The election is incomplete until the CRA receives your spouse or common-law partner's portion of the election on their UHT return designating the same residential property.

    If you choose to make an election, you may only designate one residential property.

    You cannot elect to designate different residential properties to claim multiple exemptions based on your, or your spouse's or common-law partner's, personal occupancy of the properties.

  4. Part 4 - Exemption for primary place of residence

    Only owners who are individuals may qualify for this exemption.

    To be eligible for this exemption, you must file the election in Part 3, Section 2 or 3.

    Primary place of residence

    This part applies to situations where a dwelling unit that is part of the residential property is occupied by an owner or certain members of the owner's family.

    For more detailed information, refer to: What is a primary place of residence.

  5. Part 5 - Exemption for qualifying occupancy

    To be eligible for this exemption, you must file the election in Part 3, Section 2 or 3.

    Qualifying occupancy

    This part applies to situations where your ownership of a residential property has one or more qualifying occupancy periods totalling at least 180 days in the calendar year.

    A qualifying occupancy period is at least 1 month in a calendar year where certain individuals (qualifying occupants) have continuous occupancy. Qualifying occupancy periods can be longer than 1 month, but not shorter than 1 month.

    For more detailed information, refer to: What is a qualifying occupancy period.

  6. Part 6 - Other exemptions

    Complete this part if your ownership of the residential property is exempt for reasons other than primary place of residence or qualifying occupancy.

  7. Part 7 - Fair market value (FMV) election

    If you choose to make a FMV election for a particular residential property, you are electing to use the FMV of your property, instead of its taxable value.

    Fair market value (FMV)

    To calculate the tax you owe using the fair market value of a residential property, you must file an election by completing the section.

    An owner electing to use fair market value has to get an appraisal of the property. The fair market value of your residential property must be determined by an accredited, professional, real estate appraiser operating at arm’s length to the owner. The intended use of the appraisal report must be to help in the administration of the Underused Housing Tax Act.

    Acceptable Professional Associations and Designations
    • Appraisal Institue of Canada (AIC)
      • Accredited Appraiser Canadian Institute (AACI)
      • Canadian Residential Appraiser (CRA)
    • Canadian National Association of Real Estate Appraisers
      • Designated Appraiser Residential (DAR)
      • Designated Appraiser Commercial (DAC)
    • Ordre des évaluateurs agréés du Québec (OEAQ)
      • Chartered Appraiser (C.App./É.A.)

    If you have an appraisal report from an appraiser that does not have a designation from one of these associations and need further clarification, please contact the CRA

    For more detailed information, refer to: Calculating the tax payable using the FMV]

  8. Part 8 - Calculation of tax payable

    If your ownership of a residential property does not qualify for an exemption from the tax for a calendar year, you must calculate what you owe.

    Calculation

    The Underused Housing Tax rate is 1%.

    To calculate what you owe, multiply the value of the residential property by the tax rate. Then multiply that result by your ownership percentage of the property.

    • Value of the property
    • times Underused Housing Tax rate (1%)
    • times Your ownership percentage
    • equals Underused Housing Tax amount owed
    Calculation example

    In 2023, the value of the residental property is $500,000. You are 1 of 2 owners, who equally share ownership as indicated in the land registration system.

    • $500,000 (Value of the property)
    • × times 1% (Underused Housing Tax rate)
    • × times 50% (Your ownership percentage)
    • = equals $2,000 (Underused Housing Tax amount owed for 2023)
