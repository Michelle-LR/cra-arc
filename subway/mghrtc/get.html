Determining the amount to claim

An eligible individual can claim up to $50,000 in qualifying expenditures for a qualifying renovation ending in the tax year

Only one qualifying renovation is permitted during the lifetime of a qualifying individual.

The MHRTC is 15% of whichever amount is less:

  • $50,000

  • The amount of qualifying expenditures incurred by the eligible individual

    Refer to: Expenses you can claim

  • Nil if the individual is not resident in Canada throughout the taxation year

    Residency status if there is a death

    The deceased individual is deemed to be:

    • a resident of Canada from the time of death until the end of the year if, immediately before death, the deceased individual was resident in Canada;

Since this is a refundable tax credit, if the credit you can claim is higher than the taxes you owe, you'll receive the difference as a refund.

Tax credits reduce the taxable income, which means less tax is paid to CRA. If the taxable income was originally $60,000, it would then be reduced to a taxable income of $52,500 ($60,000 - $7,500).

If taxes had already been paid on the original $60,000 (often in the form of deductions on paycheques), then CRA may have been overpaid, which can result in a refund.

To clarify, it is not the $7,500 that is refunded, it is the taxes paid on $7,500 of income that are refunded. The specific refund amount amount depends on which tax rate (tax bracket) is being applied and whether enough taxes were paid in the first place.

How tax rates work

Reducing your taxable income may also change your tax rate, by putting you in a lower 'tax bracket'.

Canada’s tax system uses 'marginal' tax rates, which means you pay more tax as your income increases. The tax rates are split up into 'brackets', and the taxes you pay are based on which of the tax brackets your income falls into, and where you live in Canada.

Many people think that once they move up or down in rates, all of their taxable income falls into that bracket. This is wrong. Your income divided into different portions that are taxed at the rates that they fall within. The more you reduce your income, like with a tax credit, the fewer tax brackets may apply to you, each with a progressively lower tax rate.
