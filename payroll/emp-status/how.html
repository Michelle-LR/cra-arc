Determine if a contract is valid

On this page

There must be a contract in place to see whether the worker is considered an employee or self-employed. This guidance on employment status assumes there is, or will be, a contract in place.

Contracts can be formed either orally or in writing. An oral contract is as valid and enforceable as a written contract.

The CRA may request a copy of the written contract or testimony by the parties to review the parties's actions. This review is to identify the intentions of both parties in the context of the employment. While this agreement is important, the facts of the working relationship as a whole will determine the employment status and not the wording of the contract alone.

Generally, all of the following elements apply when a contract is valid:

Examples of a valid contract
Description Element

There must be an offer and an acceptance of the terms and conditions of the agreement from the parties.

In a valid contract, one party makes an offer and the other party accepts the offer.

Offer and acceptance

There must be compensation or a reward for a party to carry out its obligations under the contract.

After an offer is accepted, something of value is exchanged between the two parties.

 Consideration

The parties involved must be legally capable of entering into the contract. Both parties must have the ability or capacity to understand the terms and nature of the contract. For example, as per the respective provincial Employment Standards, one of the following applies:

  • The worker has the legal age to work
  • The parents or legal guardians provides written consent for the minor child to work
  • Both parties does have a mental disability that prevents them from understanding the nature of the contract
 Capacity

The agreement must be mutual.

Each party must freely consent to the terms of the contract. There can be no undue influence or pressure to force a party to make or accept an offer.

 Consent/intent to be legally bound

The object of the contract must be legitimate.

A contract must be lawful in intent and cannot be made to execute illegal business activities.

 Obligation/lawful purpose
Who decides when a contract is null and void

When addressing the issue of statutory illegality, the courts can decide that a contract is null and void from the beginning (void ab initio) if the formation of the contract was prohibited by statute and if, in the court's assessment, it would not be contrary to public policy to declare the contract void ab initio.

Examples of termination of a contract

The following are examples of when a contract is terminated:

  • The death of one of the parties
  • The expiry of the time stipulated for the duration of the contract by the parties or by law (in the absence of any by the parties)
  • Confusion arising as a result of both parties to the contract becoming one in the same (for example, an individual buys a business (sole proprietorship) that they have an existing contract with and becomes both parties to the same contract)
  • Misrepresentation
  • Fraud
  • Duress
  • The effect of a resolutory clause in the contract (this would be a clause in the contract that serves to release the parties to the contract from an obligation in the event of a stipulated occurrence or series of events)
  • The disrespect of the main clauses of the contract, or significant breach of the conditions outlined in the contract
  • A judgment from a competent court
  • When the performance required by the contract becomes impossible for one or more of the parties to the contract to honour (reasons such as a change in law making it illegal for one of the parties to meet their obligations, or the physical incapacity (injury or mental illness) of one or more of the parties can cause a contract to become impossible to honour)
  • When both the obligations and the consideration set out in the contract have been satisfied (once a contract is completed, it is at its cessation)

Types of contracts under the law

The main types of employment contracts are:

  • Contracts of service (COS) - Employer/employee relationship
  • Contracts for service (CFS) - Worker is self-employed or a contractor
  • Contracts sui generis (of their own kind) - Elements found in both a COS and in a CFS
  • Contracts of apprenticeship - Contractual relationship with an employer for the purpose of learning a trade or occupation
What is not considered an employer/employee relationship

The following are not considered an employer/employee relationship because they are designed for other purposes than to earn the worker’s livelihood:

  • Volunteering arrangements
  • Rehabilitation and reintegration programs

If your situation is unique and different from regular employment or self-employment, you can ask for a CPP/EI ruling.

Factors to consider based on where the contract was made

You need to review the contract to determine in which province it was formed in because the multiple factors to consider are different.

The factors to consider are based on where the contract was made, unless the contract says otherwise.

What if the parties are not located in the same province

When the parties involved in a contract are not in the same province or territory, multiple factors determine which jurisdiction's laws and regulations apply to the contract.

The most common factors include the following:

  • The province or territory where the contract is considered to have been formed is significant
  • Where the parties have explicitly chosen the legal framework (the contract itself may specify which jurisdiction's laws should govern the agreement)
  • The provincial and territorial laws often have rules for determining which jurisdiction's laws apply in the absence of a specific choice of law in the contract
  • The jurisdiction where the offeror (the party making the initial offer) receives acceptance of the contract may be considered the jurisdiction where the contract was formed (where the parties negotiated the contract through methods like telephone, fax, or email)
