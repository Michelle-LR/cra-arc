Factors: Employee or self-employed worker for CPP/EI purposes

You need to review the contract to determine which province it was formed in because the factors to consider are different.

Depending on where the employment contract is formed in or where the contract requires the terms to be reviewed according to the laws of the province or territory, continue to:

  • Contract formed in a province or territory other than Quebec
  • Contract formed in the province of Quebec
    How is the Revenu Québec ruling different from the CRA

    Revenu Québec (RQ) has a separate and independent rulings process for determining whether a worker is an employee or self-employed for purposes of the QPP and QPIP.

    The determinations made by RQ do not replace a CPP/EI ruling for CRA’s purposes.

Steps

  1. Determine the intent of the parties in forming the agreement

    You need to review the contract to determine the intent of the parties (both the worker and the payer), when they entered into the working arrangement. Even with a contract, the parties may not have a common understanding about their intention in forming that contract.

    Workers and payers can choose how they set up their affairs. However, the working status they choose must reflect their working relationship. When reviewing the employment status, you need to consider all the facts, not just their intention in making the contract. This includes the actual terms and conditions of their employment.

    Review the question, and make a selection to help you gather the facts.

    At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

    Employee-employer

    Indicator that the worker is an
    Employee

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

    If the contract of service is in writing, it normally contains information such as:

    • Scale or rate of pay
    • Hours of work
    • Location of work
    • Period of employment
    Example
    Description

    Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed over the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $20 an hour

    Result

    Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

    Self-employed

    Indicator that the worker is
    Self-employed

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

    Example
    Description

    Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

    IT Computers Ltd. hires Paul’s services under a written agreement for a period of 3 months. Paul’s Beautiful Gardens will maintain the lawns and shrubs for a monthly fee of $2,000.

    Result

    In this case, Paul has an agreement to perform specific work for IT Computers Ltd. in return for payment. Paul has entered into a valid contract for services with IT Computers Ltd. and is considered a self-employed worker.

  2. Gather all facts to determine the status of the worker

    Once you completed your review of the contract to determine the intent of the parties, you need to gather all the facts to help you determine the status of the worker.

    This will help you determine if the person who is engaged to perform the services was performing them as:

    • An employee
    • A self-employed worker (a person in business on their own account)

    The following indicators for each factor need to be viewed as a whole in order to help you decide. This also needs to be done on a case-by-case basis. This can change when the terms and conditions of the relationship changes.

    Review the questions for each factor, and make a selection to help you gather the facts based on your situation.

    The questions and indicators cover the most common situations. It does not take into account all exceptions and situations and is only provided as a guideline. They do not replace the law and do not replace a formal request for a CPP/EI ruling.

    1. Control over the worker

      Control is the ability, authority, or right of a payer to exercise control over a worker concerning the manner in which the work is done and what work will be done.

      Consider the degree of control held by the payer or the degree of independence held by the worker. The actual degree of control will vary with the type of work and the skills of the worker.

      Reviewing the degree of control for professionals

      Generally, it is difficult to review the degree of control for professionals like engineers, doctors, and IT consultants. These experts, due to their training, might not need much specific direction in their work. When reviewing the factor of control, we should look at both the payer's control over the worker's daily tasks and their influence over the worker.

      The right of the payer to exercise control is very important when reviewing the degree of control for professionals and not necessarily whether they actively use that right.

      Duties

      Q1. Does the worker have defined duties?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer decides what duties the worker will do. The payer controls the worker with respect to both the results of the work and the method used to do the work.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker usually works independently of supervision. The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities. The worker has the flexibility to make arrangements as to how the tasks are performed.

      Q2. Can the payer assign or change the worker's duties at their discretion including nature and volume of workload?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The relationship is one of subordination. The payer will often direct, scrutinize, and effectively control many elements of how and when the work is carried out.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker usually works independently. The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities.

      The worker is usually free to work when and for whom they choose and may provide their services to different payers at the same time.

      Q3. Can the worker refuse work from the payer without ramifications?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can accept or refuse work from the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer decides what jobs the worker will do. The worker doesn’t have the right to refuse work from the payer.

      Work schedule

      Q4. Does the worker have set days and hours of work?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer has the final word as to when the worker performs their services. In other words, the worker requires permission from the payer to make any changes.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker has the final word as to when they perform their services. In other words, the worker doesn't require permission from the payer to make any changes.

      Q5. How long has the worker been working for the payer (or is expected to work)?

      Ongoing

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Working for a long or an indefinite period could be an indication of an employer-employee relationship.

      The working relationship between the payer and the worker has a degree of continuity, loyalty, security, subordination, or integration in the business of the payer, all of which are generally associated with an employer-employee relationship.

      For a specific job or project

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      A short term contract could be an indication of self-employment relationship.

      The working relationship between the payer and the worker does not present a degree of continuity, loyalty, security, or integration, all of which are generally associated with an employer-employee relationship.

      Q6. If the worker's days and hours of work are flexible, are they subject to the approval of the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      All decisions made by the worker concerning the days and hours of work are subject to the approval of the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can decide what days and hours they want to work and they are not subject to the approval of the payer.

      Q7. Is the worker required to record their days and hours of work? For example: By email, timesheets, timecards

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker's days and hours of work performed are recorded by the payer. The worker is required to keep a record of the starting and finishing time of each day of work.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is not required to record their days and hours of work.

      However, the worker will maintain books and records for the services provided, revenues generated and expenses, for example purchase receipts, business bank statements, and more.

      Q8. Can the worker leave at any time during the day without the approval of the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker doesn't need the payer's permission to leave.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker must ask for the payer's permission to leave the work during the hours that the payer has established that must be worked.

      Q9. If the worker is unable to work due to illness or family emergencies, is the worker required to notify the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      If the worker was unavailable due to sickness, family emergencies, etc., the payer must be notified as per the employment standards requirements. The worker must request permission to be absent from work.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is not generally required to notify the payer if is not able to work due being sick or having personal emergencies.

      Q10. If the worker wishes to take time off, is the worker required to ask for the payer's approval?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker must ask for the payer's permission for any leave of absence. The payer has the final say as to whether the worker can take a leave from work at a particular time.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker doesn't need the payer's permission to take time off.

      They may need to notify the payer depending on the requirements of the job or project.

      Degree of autonomy

      Q11. Is the worker regularly monitored or supervised (day-to-day activities) by the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The overall relationship is one of subordination. The payer will often direct, scrutinize, and effectively control many elements of how and when the work is carried out.

      The payer controls the worker with respect to both the results of the work and the method used to do the work.

      Where the work schedule is irregular, priority on the worker's time is an indication of control over the worker.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker usually works independently.

      The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities.

      Q12. Did the worker work under the supervision of the payer or the staff of the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker works under the direct or indirect supervision of the payer or someone in the payer’s business.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker generally works independently and unsupervised. The worker does not necessarily have contact with the payer until the job is complete.

      Q13. Is the worker's work subject to review by the payer or the supervisor?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The services performed by the worker are subject to the payer’s review as frequently as the payer determines.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker works independently and is ultimately responsible for meeting the payer’s expectations.

      Q14. If the payer directs the worker to redo the work, would the worker be compensated for the time to redo the work?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Employees are generally paid by the hour rather than by unit of work, so they would be compensated for the time taken to correct any errors in the work.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Self-employed people are generally be paid by the project or job, rather than an hourly or weekly rate. If errors must be corrected, these will not generally give a right to further pay.

      Q15. Is the worker required to attend meetings with the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is required to attend meetings at the payer's request.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is not generally required to attend meetings at the payer’s invitation, except if their contract requires it.

      Remuneration

      Q16. Does the payer determine the worker's rate of pay (negotiated between the worker and the payer)?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer determines and controls the method and amount of pay, subject to applicable employment standards. Salary negotiations may still take place in an employer-employee relationship.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker generally either charges a predetermined rate by invoice to the payer or negotiates with the payer the amount to be paid for the work performed.

      Q17. Is the worker paid regardless of volume of work performed?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is paid based on the time of work performed (generally an hourly rate) and is entitled to overtime pay.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is typically paid a fixed amount for a specific project or job.

      Q18. Does the payer determine the frequency and method of the worker's remuneration?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer determines the schedule and method according to which the worker is to be paid (weekly/biweekly/semi-monthly or monthly), subject to employment standards.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker negotiates with the payer to determine when they are to be paid and what the method of payment should be.

      Location of services

      Q19. Is the worker required to work at the payer's office?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer has the final word as to when and where the worker performs their services.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker decides where they perform their services, depending on the nature of the contract they have with the payer.

      The worker can perform the service at another location of choosing without necessarily having the payer’s approval.

      Right to exercise punitive action and or terminate the relationship

      Q20. Can the payer exercise disciplinary actions against the worker?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer can take disciplinary actions in response to the worker's poor performance.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The payer cannot take disciplinary actions in response to the worker’s poor performance, but could terminate the services required under the contract.

      Q21. Did the payer have a right to dismiss the worker with or without cause and notice?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer can terminate the worker’s employment in accordance with employment laws and any required compensation.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The payer can’t dismiss the worker with or without cause and notice as these are terms from employment law, but could terminate the services required under the contract.

      Q22. Can the worker look for work with other payers?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is usually free to work when and for whom they choose and may provide their services to different payers at the same time.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      When the worker is an employee, the payer generally has first call on the worker’s time. The worker may require permission from the payer to do work for others in addition to their duties to the payer.

      How the work is done

      Q23. Where their schedule is irregular, does the payer have first call or priority of the worker's time?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Where the schedule is irregular, the payer has priority over the worker’s time.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is usually free to provide their services to different payers at the same time and decide on any priority.

      Q24. Does the payer set the worker's deadlines and priorities?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer sets the worker’s priorities and deadlines. The payer decides the priority of the worker’s workload and can change the priorities at their discretion.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker decides which workloads take priority and can set their own priorities on how to complete it.

      Q25. Does the worker have the option to choose whatever method they feel appropriate to finish the task?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer determines the method of their choosing that must be used by the worker in completing work. The worker can have input but the payer has the final say in how the work is to be done.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker determines how best to carry out the workload with minimal interference from the payer.

      Training

      Q26. Did the payer provide the worker with training?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer provides and pays for any training to the worker.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is responsible to identify and make arrangements for their own training that are needed to complete the task.

      Q27. Was the worker required to pay for the training?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker generally pays for their own training.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Training is generally paid by the payer and there is no requirement for the worker to take training on their own.

      Q28. Was the worker paid for the time they spent in training?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The arrangement between the worker and the payer is that the payer will pay or reimburse for training that the worker is required to take for completing their employment duties.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Training is the worker’s responsibility and taken at their own cost. The payer is not required to pay or reimburse the worker’s training expenses.

      Q29. If the training was off site, did the payer provide the worker with transportation, accommodation and or meal allowances?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer is responsible through the employment contract to pay for the worker’s training and associated costs.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is responsible through their contract to pay for their own training and associated costs.

    2. Ownership of the tools and equipment

      Consider if the worker owns and provides tools and equipment to accomplish the work. Contractual control of, and responsibility for, an asset in a rental or lease situation is also considered under this factor.

      What is considered a significant investment

      What is relevant is the significant investment in the tools and equipment along with the cost of replacement, repair, and insurance. A worker who has made a significant investment is likely to retain a right over the use of these assets, diminishing the payer's control over how the work is carried out. In addition, such a significant investment may place the worker at a risk of a financial loss.

      Tools and equipment can vary widely in terms of value and can include everything from wrenches and hammers, to specialized clothing, appliances, stethoscopes, musical instruments, computers, and vehicles such as trucks and tractors.

      Self-employed workers often supply the tools and equipment required for a contract. As a result, the ownership of tools and equipment by a worker is more commonly associated with a business relationship.

      However, employees sometimes also have to provide their own tools. The courts have acknowledged that because a worker is required to provide tools of the trade, this does not in itself mean that the worker is a self-employed worker. For example, many skilled tradespeople such as auto mechanics have to supply their own tools, even if they are full-time employees.

      Q30. Who owns the most important tools that the worker uses while performing services for the payer?

      Worker

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Generally, the self-employed worker would provide all of their own tools and equipment required to complete the work.

      Payer

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Depending on the industry, the employer generally provides an employee with all the tools and equipment to carry out their duties.

      Both

      Indicator that the
      Facts are neutral

      This fact is neutral. You can disregard this question because it will not impact your analysis of the employment relationship.

      Q31. If the payer provides the tools, did the payer charge the worker for the use of the tools and equipment provided?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Generally, the self-employed worker would be responsible for providing or renting all tools necessary to complete a task, including renting equipment from the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Generally, the payer would allow an employee to use their tools and equipment on a particular job without charge.

      Q32. Is it a requirement (not a preference) of the worker to provide these tools and equipment?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Generally, the self-employed worker would be required to provide all of their own tools and equipment to complete the job they are hired for.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      An employer generally provides an employee with all the tools and equipment to carry out their duties or will reimburse their employees for the costs of purchasing tools/equipment to carry out their duties.

      Q33. Who is responsible for the repair, replacement, maintenance and insurance of the tools and equipment?

      Worker

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is responsible to purchase, maintain, repair and provide insurance coverage for the tools and equipment needed.

      Payer

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer is responsible to purchase, maintain, repair and provide insurance coverage for the tools and equipment provided.

      Both

      Indicator that the
      Facts are neutral

      This fact is neutral. You can disregard this question because it will not impact your analysis of the employment relationship.

    3. Subcontracting of work or hiring assistants

      Consider if the worker can subcontract work or hire assistants. This factor can help decide a worker's business presence because subcontracting work or hiring assistants can affect their chance of profit and risk of loss.

      Q34. Can the worker subcontract the work without the approval of the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can subcontract the job or task. They do not have to carry out the services personally.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker cannot subcontract the job or task. They have to do the work personally.

      Q35. Can the worker hire someone to assist them to do and or finish the work without the approval of the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can hire helpers or assistants without the approval of the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker cannot hire helpers or assistants without the approval of the payer.

    4. Financial risk taken on by the worker

      Consider the degree of financial risk taken by the worker. Consider if there are any fixed ongoing costs incurred by the worker or any expenses that are not reimbursed.

      Employees will usually not have financial risks

      Usually, employees will not have any financial risk as any expenses will be reimbursed, and they will not have fixed ongoing costs.

      Self-employed workers, on the other hand, can have financial risk and incur losses because they usually pay fixed monthly costs even if work is not currently being done.

      Employees and self-employed workers may be reimbursed for business or travel expenses. Therefore, focus on the expenses that are not reimbursed by the payer.

      Q36. Does the payer reimburse the worker for any expenses?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker will not have any financial risk as any expenses required to perform their employment duties will be reimbursed.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker bears financial risk and responsibility for their own expenses related to performing the work.

      Q37. Does the payer provide benefits to the worker on account of the working relationship?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Employers frequently provide benefits as part of their compensation package to their employees.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker does not typically receive any benefits from the payer.

      Q38. Does the worker have any expenses that could be so substantial as to incur a business loss?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker’s income is not guaranteed. It is based on the fulfilment of the contract agreed between the parties. The worker will still be responsible for their fixed outgoing costs.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is guaranteed their hourly ( or weekly) rate of pay for the time worked. Employees usually bear no expenses related to their employment (other than travel to and from work). They are not financially liable if they do not fulfil the obligations of the contract.

      Q39. Does the worker have any expenses pertaining to the running of a business? For example: rent, advertising, business telephone, overhead

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker usually pays fixed monthly costs even if work is not currently being done.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee generally would not have any fixed ongoing costs associated with their job. They are an employee and not running a business of their own in performing the work.

      Q40. Is the worker financially liable if they cannot perform the work?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer is not financially liable if they do not fulfill the obligations of the contract. Disciplinary actions for poor performance are available to the employer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is financially liable if they do not fulfill the obligations of the contract.

    5. Responsibility for investment and management held by the worker

      Consider the degree of responsibility for investment and management held by the worker. A significant investment is evidence that a business relationship may exist. You should also consider if the worker is free to make business decisions that affect their profit or loss.

      Q41. Does the worker have a business with an operating name through which they provide the services?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker has an operating name through which they provide the services. The worker performs their services independently, for their own business, rather than as an employee.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker provides their services under the name of the payer.

      However, if the worker providing the services is doing so through a corporation, they may be operating as an incorporated employee.

      Learn more: Factors to consider: Employee or PSB for income tax purposes

      Q42. Does the worker maintain a separate business office either at their home or a commercial location?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker has their own office that is not part of the premises of the payer indicates a separate business exists.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker does not have a separate business office other than one to which the employer allows the employee to report for their employment duties.

      Q43. Does the worker have a business presence for providing services separate from the payer’s business?For example: business bank accounts, pre-printed invoices, business cards or letterheads

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker has a business presence that is separate from the payer’s business.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker does not have a business presence that is separate from the payer’s business.

      Q44. Does the worker advertise their services for hire? For example: In periodicals, websites, magazines, newspapers

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker advertises the type of services they provide to the payer for other clients.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker does not advertise the type of services they provide to the payer for other clients, as all of their work comes from the payer.

      Q45. Does the worker carry their own liability insurance for errors and omissions?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Liability insurance is usually taken for protection of the worker’s liability as an independent contractor.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker would be covered under the payer’s liability insurance for errors and omissions.

      Q46. Does the worker maintain books and records for the services provided? For example: Record of revenues generated and expenses paid for the services performed such as invoices, purchase receipts, business bank statements)

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker requires their own books and records to track the activities of their business.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer maintains the books and records for the work performed by the worker.

      Q47. Does the worker manage their own staff?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can supervise and manage their own staff.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The employee is only able to hire or use their own staff with the consent of the payer.

      Q48. Does the worker hire individuals to help them to perform the work and the worker pays these individuals?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can decide whether to hire workers to help complete the work and it is the worker’s responsibility to pay them.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer is responsible to hire and pay more workers when additional support is required to perform a task.

      Q49. Does the worker issue invoices to the payer for payments?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      A separate business would be responsible for collecting and remitting the GST/HST payable on the property and services it provides.

      Charging, collecting and remitting GST/HST would demonstrate a separate business from the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Employees are typically paid by their negotiated rate (per hour, week, etc) and invoices would not be required.

      Q50. Does the worker report income earned as business income and also claim any business expenses?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker reports their income from the project or job exclusively as business income and does not receive a T4 or report their income on their personal income tax return as employment income.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker receives payment as an employee performing employment duties for the payer and is not eligible to report this income as business income or claim the associated expenses.

      The income will be reported on a T4 and they will report their income on their personal income tax return as employment income.

    6. Opportunity for profit

      Consider whether the worker can realize a profit or incur a loss, as this indicates that a worker controls the business aspects of services rendered and that a business relationship likely exists. To have a chance of a profit and a risk of a loss, a worker has to have potential proceeds and expenses, and one could exceed the other.

      Consider this factor from the worker's perspective

      This factor has to be considered from the worker's perspective, not the payer's. It is for the most part an assessment of the degree to which the worker can control their proceeds and expenses.

      Employees normally do not have the chance of a profit and risk of a loss even though their remuneration can vary depending on the terms of their employment contracts. For example, employees working on a commission or piece-rate basis, or employees with a productivity bonus clause in their contract can increase their earnings based on their productivity. This increase in income is not normally viewed as a profit, as it is not the excess of proceeds over expenses.

      Employees may have expenses directly related to their employment, such as automobile expenses, and board and lodging costs. Normally, expenses would not place employees at risk of incurring a loss because it is unlikely that the expenses would be greater than their remuneration.

      Self-employed workers normally have the chance of profit or risk of loss, because they have the ability to pursue and accept contracts as they see fit. They can negotiate the price (or unilaterally set their prices) for their services and have the right to offer those services to more than one payer. Self-employed workers will normally incur expenses to carry out the terms and conditions of their contracts, and to manage those expenses to maximize net earnings. Self-employed workers can increase their proceeds and/or decrease their expenses in an effort to increase profit.

      Employees generally do not share in profits or suffer losses incurred by the payer's business.

      The method of payment may help to decide if the worker has the opportunity to make a profit or incur a loss. In an employer-employee relationship, the worker is normally guaranteed a return for the work done and is usually paid on an hourly, daily, weekly, or similar basis.

      Similarly, some self-employed workers are paid on an hourly basis. However, when a worker is paid a flat rate for the work done, it generally indicates a business relationship, especially if the worker incurs expenses in doing the work.

      Q51. Do the terms of the work relationship anticipate continuous work?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      An employee is generally hired on a continuous basis.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker must find contracts by themselves and the payer is not responsible if there is no work (or no further work) available.

      Q52. Is the worker paid a fixed amount for a specific end result?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker is generally compensated by a flat fee to perform a specific project or job.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is guaranteed a fixed remuneration based on the time worked, regardless of the volume of work performed.

      The worker is often entitled to benefit plans that are normally offered only to employees. These include registered pension plans, and group accident, health, and dental insurance plans.

      Q53. Does the worker incur expenses related to completion of the contract with the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is financially responsible for expenses related to completion of the contract.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer is financially responsible for expenses related to performance of their employment duties.

      Q54. If the worker can hire assistant or subcontract the work, can the worker determine the rate of pay of the assistant hired or the subcontractor?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker sets the rates for any assistants or subcontractors they hire, and is responsible for paying them.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker would not be able to hire assistants or helpers without the consent of the payer.

      The payer would hire additional workers and assign additional resources if needed and the payer would also determine the helpers’ rates of pay.

  3. Do a final analysis based on all factors reviewed

    Analyze the facts

    You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will show if the facts of the working relationship match the status the parties have chosen and are consistent with the common law understanding of employment and self-employment. In brief, it will clarify if there is an employment relationship, or if the worker is in business on their own account.

    You must analyze the answer to each question from both the payer’s and the worker’s perspective to help you decide on the worker’s employment status.

    Weighing the facts

    This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support an employee-employer relationship and other facts indicate self-employment.

    To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the working relationship of the parties, comparing the intentions of the parties to the facts of the working relationship. You must review the indicators in the factors of control, ownership of tools and equipment, opportunity for profit and financial risk, and responsibility for investment and management. As you review each question and response, you should be able to decide what weight to assign each indicator based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, control may favour an employee relationship, but ownership of tools may be assigned more weight due to how the total relationship is structured. This may be an indicator because of the increased weight given to this factor, that the worker is self-employed.

    Review each situation on a case-by-case basis

    Whether a worker is an employee or self-employed needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to decide the employment status, you must examine the total working relationship. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

    Employee

    Example
    Description

    Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed over the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $20 an hour

    Result

    Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

    Self-employed

    Indicator that the worker is
    Self-employed

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

    Example
    Description

    Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

    4. Relationship of subordination

      Under the C.C.Q., a relationship of subordination is the main characteristic of a contract of employment. Without this relationship, there cannot be a contract of employment. If there is no subordination, it is a contract for services.

      A relationship of subordination is the capacity, the authority, or the right of a payer to exercise control over the worker's activities and the manner in which the work is done.

      It is the right of the payer to exercise control which is important in this review and not necessarily whether they actively use that right. It is the control of the payer over the worker that is relevant and not the control of the payer over the end result of a product or service that they have purchased.
