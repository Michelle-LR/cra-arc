Determine if the worker is operating a PSB

Eligibility criteria

Under the Income Tax Act (ITA), the corporation is considered a personal services business (PSB) if it meets all 5 conditions below.

Select all of the conditions that apply to your situation:

  • What is a related person and a specified shareholder

    Related person

    Where the person is an individual, a related person is someone connected to the person by blood relationship, marriage, common-law partnership or adoption.

    A person is considered related to a corporation where:

    1. They control that corporation
    2. They are a member of a related group that controls the corporation
    3. They are related to someone described in a. or b.

    Learn more: Income Tax Folio S1-F5-C1, Related Persons and Dealing at Arm's Length

    Specified shareholder

    A specified shareholder of the corporation is an individual who directly or indirectly owns at least 10% of the issued shares of any class of a capital stock of the corporation or a related corporation at any time in the year.

  • What is a full-time employee

    An employee who works a full business day on each working day of the year, subject to normal absences due to illness or vacation.

  • What is an associated corporation

    Generally, a corporation is associated with another corporation if it meets any of the 6 conditions at any time in the tax year.

    Learn more: T2 Corporation – Income Tax Guide – Chapter 2: Page 2 of the T2 return

  • How to determine if the worker would be considered an employee

    One of the conditions for a corporation to be a PSB is that the worker that provides services to the person on behalf of the corporation would reasonably be considered an employee of that person if the corporation did not exist.

    You cannot ask for a CPP/EI ruling to determine if the corporation meets the requirements to be considered a PSB

    The analysis to determine if a worker is an employee or self-employed is also used to determine whether the corporation of an employee who provides services through a corporation meets this condition for being considered a PSB for corporate income tax purposes.

    It is important to recognize that employment status and whether a corporation meets the conditions to be considered a PSB are distinct determinations and are made under different legislation. The factors used to determine whether an employee-employer relationship exists are the same factors used to determine whether a worker would have been an employee if the corporation did not exist.

    While the analysis of whether an incorporated employee would have been an employee but for the existence of the corporation is fundamentally the same as whether a worker is an employee or self-employed, a CPP/EI ruling cannot be requested to determine if a corporation meets this requirement. However, a worker who provides services for a PSB can request a ruling as to whether they are an employee of the PSB or are self-employed.

    If you are not sure about what the worker’s employment status would be, continue to: Determine if the worker is an employee or a self-employed worker

    If you determine that the worker would be considered an employee and meets the other conditions above, the worker’s corporation is also considered a PSB.

The corporation must meet all conditions above to be considered a PSB

Your result
The corporation must meet all 5 conditions above to be considered a PSB

If the worker’s corporation does not meet all 5 conditions above, the worker is not an incorporated employee and is not operating a PSB.

  • Condition 1 met

    If the worker performs their services through a corporation (condition 1) but does not meet all other 4 conditions above, the worker is not an incorporated employee and is not operating a PSB. The same tax obligations apply as any other corporations.

    Example
    Description

    Faye is looking to create her own coding and consulting business. She creates a business plan with a couple of friends, and incorporates as 123 Programming Inc. Faye and her two friends become shareholders of the business.

    To perform work, Faye and her business partners use the corporation’s computer equipment. Throughout the first fiscal year, the corporation worked for five clients, billed the clients, and disbursed a portion of the payment to the shareholders, while also holding a small portion to pay off corporation expenses.  The work they performed ranged from coding an appointment registration application for a hair salon, to consulting with a mall on how to update their networking infrastructure.

    Result

    In this example, 123 Programming Inc. does not meet the conditions to be considered a PSB, because:

    • Faye and her business partners perform work for clients using 123 Programming Inc.’s equipment, but they do not perform work that an employee of their clients would normally perform
    • 123 Programming’s income is from services performed by more than one shareholder for more than one client

    Since 123 Programming Inc. is not considered a PSB, the same tax obligations apply as any other corporations.

    Continue to: Corporation income tax return

  • Condition 1 not met

    If the worker does not perform their services through a corporation (condition 1), the worker is not an incorporated employee and is not operating a PSB.

    If you are not sure about the worker’s employment status, continue to: Determine if the worker is an employee or a self-employed worker

    You cannot ask for a CPP/EI ruling to determine if the corporation meets the requirements to be considered a PSB

    The analysis to determine if a worker is an employee or self-employed is also relevant to determine whether the corporation of an employee who provides services through a corporation meets the conditions for being considered a PSB for corporate income tax purposes.

    It is important to recognize that employment status and whether a corporation meets the conditions to be considered a PSB are distinct determinations and are made under different legislation.

    While the analysis of whether an incorporated employee would have been an employee but for the existence of the corporation is fundamentally the same as whether a worker is an employee or self-employed, a CPP/EI Ruling cannot be requested to determine if a corporation meets this requirement. However, a worker who provides services for a PSB can request a ruling as to whether they are an employee of the PSB or are self-employed.

    Even though these are distinct, the information has been provided together to help workers in various working arrangements understand their employment status or the nature of the contract they are providing services under and the withholding and reporting obligations that result from these arrangements.

Your result
The corporation meets all 5 conditions to be considered a PSB

The worker is likely an incorporated employee and their corporation is likely considered a PSB because all 5 conditions above apply.

Examples

Example 1

Description

Tam is looking for a job. An Ontario-based trucking company (ABC Trucking) offers him a 12-month position with full-time hours. The contract comes with the stipulation that Tam perform his services through a corporation.

Tam incorporates his business (858689 Ontario Inc.), for which he is the only shareholder and the only employee. 858689 Ontario Inc.’s only client is ABC Trucking.

858689 Ontario Inc. bills ABC Trucking for the services it performs, and it receives payment from ABC Trucking for those services. 858689 Ontario Inc. either keeps the funds in the corporation or disburses them to Tam.

Result

In this example, 858689 Ontario Inc. meets the conditions to be considered a PSB because:

  • Tam is a specified shareholder of 858689 Ontario Inc.
  • Tam performs the work of an ABC Trucking employee using their trucks, and were it not for the existence of 858689 Ontario Inc., Tam would be considered an employee of ABC Trucking
  • 858689 Ontario Inc.’s sole income is from services performed by Tam (incorporated employee) for ABC Trucking

Example 2

Description

Mia is a nail technician and is looking for a job. A nail salon, 789 Salon, offers her a job but requires Mia to incorporate in order to offer her services at their salon. Mia incorporates as XYZ Nails Inc. and is the only shareholder and employee. She pays a chair rental fee to 789 Salon.

The hiring business, 789 Salon, supplies all the instruments and products required to perform her job as a nail technician.

Result

In this example, XYZ Nails Inc. meets the conditions to be considered a PSB because:

  • Mia is a specified shareholder of XYZ Nails Inc. and its only employee
  • Mia performs the work of a 789 Salon employee using their instruments and products, and were it not for the existence of XYZ Nails Inc., Mia would be considered an employee of 789 Salon
  • XYZ Nails Inc.’s sole income is from services performed by Mia (incorporated employee) for 789 Salon

Should the conditions of Mia’s business change to include:

  • Using her own equipment and products
  • Making her own appointments
  • Advertising XYZ Nails Inc. business
  • Hiring other manicurists to sit in her chair at 789 Salon

It would be more likely that she would not be considered a PSB for income tax purposes.
