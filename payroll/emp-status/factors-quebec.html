Contract formed in the province of Quebec

You need to review the contract to determine which province it was formed in because the factors to consider are different.

Depending on where the employment contract is formed in or where the contract requires the terms to be reviewed according to the laws of the province or territory, continue to:

  • Contract formed in a province or territory other than Quebec
  • Contract formed in the province of Quebec
    How is the Revenu Québec ruling different from the CRA

    Revenu Québec (RQ) has a separate and independent rulings process for determining whether a worker is an employee or self-employed for purposes of the QPP and QPIP.

    The determinations made by RQ do not replace a CPP/EI ruling for CRA’s purposes.

Steps

  1. Determine the intent of the parties in forming the agreement

    At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

    Employee-employer

    Indicator that the worker is an
    Employee

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

    If the contract of service is in writing, it normally contains information such as:

    • Scale or rate of pay
    • Hours of work
    • Location of work
    • Period of employment
    Example
    Description

    Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed over the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $20 an hour

    Result

    Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

    Self-employed

    Indicator that the worker is
    Self-employed

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

    Example
    Description

    Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

    IT Computers Ltd. hires Paul’s services under a written agreement for a period of 3 months. Paul’s Beautiful Gardens will maintain the lawns and shrubs for a monthly fee of $2,000.

    Result

    In this case, Paul has an agreement to perform specific work for IT Computers Ltd. in return for payment. Paul has entered into a valid contract for services with IT Computers Ltd. and is considered a self-employed worker.

  2. Review the meaning of a contract of employment under the Civil Code of Québec

    Once you completed your review of the contract to determine the intent of the parties, you need to review the definition of a contract of employment under the Civil Code of Québec (C.C.Q.).

    • Factors to consider: Employee

      For the worker to be considered an employee, the facts of their working relationship with the payer must match the following 3 elements of that C.C.Q. definition:

      • Carrying out the work
      • Remuneration 
      • Relationship of subordination 

    • Factors to consider: Self-employed worker

      For the worker to be considered self-employed (contract of enterprise or for services), the key elements are different. The most important difference is that it does not have a relationship of subordination 

  3. Gather all facts to determine the status of the worker

    Once you completed your review of the meaning of employment under the C.C.Q., you need to gather all the facts to help you determine the status of the worker.

    1. Documents to review

      When reviewing the worker’s and payer’s documents, the type of documents and the manner in which the information is documented can provide important clues into the relationship between the 2 parties.

      Q1. Is the worker listed in the payer’s payroll and other records as an employee?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is more likely to be an employee as the payer is showing they understand them to be an employee in how they are treating them internally.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is more likely to be self-employed as the payer is showing they understand the worker to be self-employed in how they are treating them internally.

      Q2. Was the worker listed as part of the staff in the payer’s staff phone directory or payer’s website?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer’s phone directory generally only contains workers who are employees.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The payer’s phone directory generally does not contain workers who are self-employed.

      Q3. Does the worker have a position within the payer’s organization?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee will generally have a job title or position in the payer’s organization.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed will not generally have a job title or position in the payer’s organization.

      Q4. Were there any written communications such as emails or written correspondence between the payer and the worker that acknowledged that the worker was performing duties as an employee?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is likely an employee as the payer and the worker appear to understand the agreement as an employment relationship.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is likely an independent contractor as the payer and the worker do not appear to understand the relationship as an employment relationship.

    2. Carrying out the work

      Control over where, when and how the work is done needs to be considered when determining the degree of control held by the payer or the degree of independence held by the worker. The actual degree of control will vary with the type of work and the skills of the worker.

      Q5. Did the worker’s position have a job description in the payer’s internal policies?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee would have a job description provided by the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed would not have a job description provided by the payer.

      Q6. Where did the worker perform the agreed services?

      Payer’s location

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee generally reports to and carries out their tasks at an establishment of the payer or a location where they were assigned to work by the payer.

      Worker’s location

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed generally has more control over where they complete their work.

      Client’s location

      Indicator that the
      Facts are neutral

      The work being performed at the client’s location is not a clear sign of the worker being an employee or self-employed.

      Q7. Who determined where the worker performed the agreed service?

      Payer

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer generally determines where the work of an employee is to be completed.

      Worker

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed can exercise more control over where they carry out their work, however, depending on the task, the payer may require the work to be performed at a particular location.

      Q8. Who determined when the worker performed the agreed service?

      Payer

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer generally determines when employment duties should be completed.

      Worker

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker has greater control over when contracted services are to be completed.

      Client

      Indicator that the
      Facts are neutral

      The client determining when the worker performed the agreed services is not indicative of the worker being an employee or self-employed.

    3. Remuneration

      In both types of arrangements (employee or self-employed) remuneration must be paid or due to the worker. However, what the remuneration was based on, the frequency of payments, whether there were deductions withheld, and how the worker requests payments can be key indicators of either a work who is an employee or one who is self-employed.

      Q9. Were QPP, CPP, EI or income tax deductions withheld from the worker’s pay?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer is required to withhold source deductions from the pay of an employee.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is responsible for their own income tax deductions and QPP or CPP contributions.

      Q10. Was a pay stub given to the worker when they were paid?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee is usually given a pay stub showing their gross income, source deductions and net pay.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker generally invoices the payer for the services they have rendered.

      Q11. Was the worker issued a T4 information slip?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      A T4 information slip is required to be given to a worker who is paid employment income.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      A T4 information slip is only given to a worker who is self-employed in certain occupations. For example, barbers, hairdressers and fishers. Depending on the services performed, a self-employed worker may receive a T4A, T1204 or a T5018 information slip.

      Q12. On what schedule was the agreed remuneration paid?

      Hourly/monthly

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee is generally paid for their time while working on a task.

      Percentage of job completion/piecework

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed is generally paid based on completion of a task or job.

      Q13. If the worker is paid by the hour, is the worker entitled to overtime pay?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Overtime pay is required by employment standards legislation for an employee and is generally included as part of their contract.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker is generally not entitled to overtime pay.

      Q14. In addition to remuneration, did the worker receive vacation pay, statutory holiday pay or payments during a sick leave?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Additional benefits such as vacation and sick leave are often provided by the employer to the employee.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Additional benefits such as vacation and sick leave are generally not provided to the worker who is self-employed.

      Q15. Were there any benefits included in the compensation package offered to the worker?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Benefits such as medical and dental insurance plans are often provided by the employer to the employee.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The self-employed worker is generally not offered benefits such as medical and dental insurance plans by the payer.

      Q16. Did the worker participate in the payer’s private pension plan?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      Participation in the payer’s pension plan is usually limited to the worker who is an employee.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      Participation in the payer’s pension plan usually does not include a worker who is self-employed.

      Q17. Does the worker have a right to file a complaint with Employment Standards/Labour Standards for unpaid remuneration?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker is an employee when they have a right to make a complaint under the Employment Standards/Labour Standards for unpaid wages they are owed.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is in business on their own account and not entitled to make a complaint under Employment Standards.

      Q18. How did the worker request payments?

      Filing a timesheet

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker’s hours of work performed are recorded by the payer. The worker is required to complete a record of the starting and finishing time of each day of work (for example, a timesheet).

      Sending an invoice

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker sends an invoice to the payer to request payment for the services provided.

      Q19. Is the worker entitled to any reimbursement of expenses incurred?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker will generally be reimbursed for any expenses they are required to pay associated with their employment duties.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is responsible for their own expenses associated with the services performed.

    4. Relationship of subordination

      Under the C.C.Q., a relationship of subordination is the main characteristic of a contract of employment. Without this relationship, there cannot be a contract of employment. If there is no subordination, it is a contract for services.

      A relationship of subordination is the capacity, the authority, or the right of a payer to exercise control over the worker's activities and the manner in which the work is done.

      It is the right of the payer to exercise control which is important in this review and not necessarily whether they actively use that right. It is the control of the payer over the worker that is relevant and not the control of the payer over the end result of a product or service that they have purchased.

      Q20. Did the worker work at a designated location?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee generally reports to and carries out their tasks at an establishment of the payer or a location where they were assigned to work by the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed generally has more control than the employee over where they complete their work. Depending on the nature of the job or project, the payer may require the work be done at a particular location.

      Q21. Did the payer have a right to determine where the worker performs their service?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker who is an employee generally reports to and carries out their tasks at an establishment of the payer or a location where they were assigned to work by the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker who is self-employed generally has more control than the employee over where they complete their work. Depending on the nature of the job or project, the payer may require the work be done at a particular location.

      Q22. Did the payer set or have the right to set the worker’s work schedule?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer will generally set the work schedule of the worker who is an employee, subject to employment standards requirements. All requests for changes to the schedule made by the worker are subject to the approval of the payer.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can generally decide what days and hours they want to work without needing approval of the payer.

      Q23. If the worker wishes to take time off, is the worker required to ask for the payer’s approval?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker must ask for the payer’s permission for any leave of absence. The payer has the final say as to whether the worker can take a leave from work at a particular time.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker doesn't need the payer’s permission to take time off. They may need to notify the payer depending on the requirements of the job or project.

      Q24. Does the worker have to do the work personally or can they subcontract the work without the approval of the payer?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker can subcontract the job or task. They do not have to carry out the services personally.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker has to do the work personally. They cannot subcontract the job or task.

      Q25. Was training provided to the worker at no charge?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The training is paid by the payer and there is no requirement for the worker to take training at their own expense.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The training is the worker’s responsibility and at their own cost. The payer is not required to pay or reimburse the worker’s training expenses.

      Q26. Were there established procedures and or a code of conduct policy in place governing the worker’s conduct and how work was to be performed?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker must adhere to the procedures set by the payer in all aspects of their performance. The payer has the final say in how the work is to be done.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker is responsible for satisfying the payer’s needs, and is not necessarily subject to any procedures or codes set by the payer for their employees.

      Q27. Did the worker have a supervisor?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The worker works under the direct or indirect supervision of the payer or someone in the payer’s business.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker generally works independently and unsupervised. The worker does not necessarily have contact with the payer until the job is complete.

      Q28. Did the payer regularly review the work performed by the worker?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The services performed by the worker are subject to the payer’s review as frequently as the payer determines.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The worker works independently and is ultimately responsible for meeting the payer’s expectations.

      Q29. Can the payer exercise disciplinary actions against the worker?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer can take disciplinary actions in response to Worker’s poor performance.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The payer cannot take disciplinary action in response to the worker’s poor performance, but could terminate the services required under the contract.

      Q30. Does the payer have the right to dismiss the worker with or without cause and notice?

      Yes

      Indicator that the worker is an
      Employee

      The payer can terminate the worker’s employment in accordance with employment laws and any required compensation.

      No

      Indicator that the worker is
      Self-employed

      The payer can’t dismiss the worker with cause and notice as these are terms from employment law, but could terminate the services required under the contract.

  4. Do a final analysis based on all factors reviewed

    Analyze the facts

    You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will show if the facts of the working relationship match the status the parties have chosen and are consistent with the definitions of the C.C.Q.. Generally, it will clarify if there is an employment relationship, or if the worker is in business on their own account.

    You must analyze the answer to each question from both the payer’s and the worker’s perspective to help you decide on the worker’s employment status.

    Weighing the facts

    This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support an employee-employer relationship and other facts indicate self-employment.

    To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the working relationship of the parties, comparing the intentions of the parties to the facts of their working relationship. You must review the indicators in the factors of carrying out the work, remuneration and relationship of subordination As you review each question and response, you should be able to decide what weight to assign each indicator based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, one fact may favour an employee relationship, but another may be assigned more weight due to how the relationship is structured. This may be an indicator, because of the increased weight given to this factor, that the worker is self-employed.

    Review each situation on a case-by-case basis

    Whether a worker is an employee or self-employed needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to decide the employment status, you must review the understanding a contract of employment and contract for services of the C.C.Q..
