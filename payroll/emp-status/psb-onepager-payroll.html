Determine if the worker is operating a personal services business

Depending if a worker performs the services on behalf of their own corporation, they may be considered a personal services business (PSB) for income tax purposes.

  1. Review the PSB criteria

    Generally, a personal services business (PSB) exists where the individual performing the work would be considered to be an employee of the payer if it were not for the existence of the corporation.

    Refer to: Criteria required to deemed a PSB

    •   If you are not sure about what the worker’s employment status would be, refer to: Factors to consider: Employee or self-employed for CPP/EI purposes

      You cannot ask for a CPP/EI ruling to determine if the corporation meets the requirements to be considered a PSB

      The analysis to determine if a worker is an employee or self-employed is also relevant to determine whether the corporation of an employee who provides services through a corporation meets the conditions for being considered a PSB for corporate income tax purposes.

      It is important to recognize that employment status and whether a corporation meets the conditions to be considered a PSB are distinct determinations and are made under different legislation.

      While the analysis of whether an incorporated employee would have been an employee but for the existence of the corporation is fundamentally the same as whether a worker is an employee or self-employed, a CPP/EI Ruling cannot be requested to determine if a corporation meets this requirement. However, a worker who provides services for a PSB can request a ruling as to whether they are an employee of the PSB or are self-employed.

      Even though these are distinct, the information has been provided together to help workers in various working arrangements understand their employment status or the nature of the contract they are providing services under and the withholding and reporting obligations that result from these arrangements.

    • Continue to next step if you know what the worker’s employment status.

  2. Understand payroll obligations when paying a PSB

    If you know what the worker’s employment status would be, continue to: Your tax obligations: Employer or payer

    Generally, any person must open a payroll account, calculate and remit (pay) any deductions withheld, and file information returns if they make payments to a PSB.

    Generally, if the payer makes a payment for a service outside of an employment relationship or tenure of office, the payer must fill out a T4A slip. This includes payments of fees for services between businesses, including sole proprietors and corporations. Depending on the situation, the payer may need to fill out a T4A-NR, T5018 or T1204 instead.

    Learn more: Payments of fees for services

  3. Understand other tax obligations when paying a PSB

    Income tax obligations

    The payer must report and pay tax on all income earned from taxable activities. The income is the net earnings from employment, property, investments and benefits. To report and pay tax, the payer must calculate the business income and expenses using one of the following form when filing their income tax return:

    • T1 return (business income)

      Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities

      Learn more: Lines 13499 to 14300 – Self-employment income

    • T2 return (corporate income)

      Schedule 125, Income Statement Information

      Learn more: Completing your corporation income tax return

    • T5013 return (partnership income)

      Schedule 125, Income Statement Information

      Learn more: T5013 Partnership Information Return filing requirements

    Learn more about other income tax obligations:

    • Corporation income tax
    GST/HST obligations

    Generally, any person must open a GST/HST account, collect, file and remit (pay) the GST/HST if they provide goods or services and earn more than $30,000 over 4 calendar quarters.
