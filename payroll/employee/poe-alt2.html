Determine the province of employment (POE)

Content has been updated to include the new CRA administrative policy. This change is effective January 1, 2024 and is identified with the tag New CRA administrative policy.

When you pay employment income such as salaries, wages, or commissions, the employee's province or territory of employment (POE) must be determined so that the proper deductions are withheld. This depends on whether your employee “reports for work” at any of your establishments.

The POE is determined by:

  • the type of income
  • the residency status
  • how the employee reports for work

For income tax, CPP and EI withholding purposes, an establishment of the employer is any place or premises in Canada that is owned, leased or rented by an employer where employees report to work or from which employees are paid. For purposes of the POE, this does not need to be a permanent physical location.

POE for employment income: Employee is a resident of Canada

Under the new CRA administrative policy, the employee is considered to be reporting for work at an establishment of the employer if one of the following applies:

  • New CRA administrative policy

    Where a full-time remote work agreement  was entered into, the employee can be reasonably regarded as "attached to an establishment" of the employer

  • The employee reports for work physically at the establishment, there is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place

This administrative policy only applies for determining POE for the purpose of CPP/QPP, EI and income tax deductions. It does not impact any provincial or federal law deeming you to have a permanent establishment in a province.

How to determine if the employee is considered "attached to an establishment" of the employer

To determine if the employee is reasonably considered "attached to an establishment" of the employer, all of the relevant facts in your case must be considered.

You cannot use the factors to attach the employee to an establishment of the employer to avoid source deductions or payroll contributions in any province. 

  1. Determine if a full-time remote work agreement was entered into

    Generally, the CRA considers a full-time remote work agreement to include both temporary and permanent arrangements where an employer directs or allows employees to perform their usual employment duties at an approved location away from the regular workplace.

    Examples of full-time remote workers by types of industries

    • Worker may be a full-time remote worker

      The following sectors have high rates of employee's working remotely:

      • Professional and technical services - For example: accounting, bookkeeping, consulting, legal, advertising, information technology (IT) or computer related services
      • Public administration - For example: federal, provincial, territorial, municipal, or regional administration
      • Information, culture and recreation - For example: Publishing industries, telecommunication, broadcasting or data processing
      • Financial and insurance - For example: Central bank, insurance, securities, funds or other financial activities
      • Employees working on the road - For example: representatives or vendors

      This is because employees may still perform their jobs reasonably well outside the workplace.

    • Worker may not be a full-time remote worker

      The following sectors have lower rates of employee's working remotely:

      • Accommodation and food services - For example: hotels, resorts, bed and breakfast, recreational vehicle parks or coupgrounds
      • Construction services - For example: construction of buildings
      • Retail trade - For example: motor vehicle dealers, furniture stores, electronic and appliance stores or food stores
      • Health care and social assistance - For example: hospitals, nursing and residential care facilities or social assistance

      This is because employees may require more person-to-person contact, working with large machinery or have a limited scope for remote work because of different factors.

    • If the employee is considered to work remotely full-time, the new CRA administrative policy may apply.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine where the employee is considered to be "attached to an establishment" of the employer

    • If the employee is not considered to work remotely full-time, the new CRA administrative policy does not apply.
  2. Determine where the employee is considered to be "attached to an establishment" of the employer

    Once you have determine the employee is considered to have a full-time remote work agreement , you need to determine where the employee is considered to be "attached to an establishment" of the employer.

    This determination must be done based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific case.

    Significant factor

    The establishment where the employee would physically come to work in person to carry out the functions related to their employment duties.

    For employees who physically reported to an establishment of the employer immediately before entering a full-time remote work agreement, that establishment is the one to which they would be considered to be attached.

    Secondary factors

    You may also need to consider the following secondary factors when determining where the employee is considered to be "attached to an establishment" of the employer:

    1. The establishment where the employee would physically attend meetings, through any type of communication
    2. The establishment where the employee would physically receive work-related material or equipment or associated instructions and assistance
    3. The establishment where the employee would physically come in person to receive instructions from their employer regarding their duties, through any type of communication
    4. The establishment that is responsible for the employee, as indicated in the contractual agreements between the employer and the employee
    5. The establishment of the employer to which they would report based on the nature of the duties performed by the employee

    Generally, each factor needs to be viewed as a whole in order to help you determine where the employee is considered to be "attached to an establishment". You will normally have more than 1 factor when determining where the employee is considered "attached" to a specific establishment of the employer.

    More than 1 establishment of the employer

    If your employee can be reasonably considered attached to more than 1 establishment of the employer, the same factors should be used. The establishment of the employer would be either:

    • where they would be spending most time
    • where they would be working last, if the employee would be reporting to each establishment for the same amount of time

Determine the POE based on the situation

Situation: Employee reports for work (physically or is considered attached) to only 1 establishment of the employer in Canada New CRA administrative policy

The province of employment is where the employee reports for work.

There is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Reminder: If the employee's province of employment is not the same as their province of residence, they may have not enough or too much tax deducted. Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source.

Situation: Employee reports for work (physically) to more than 1 establishment of the employer in Canada during a pay period

The province of employment is the province in which the establishment of the employer is located where the employee had either:

  • Spent the most time
  • Worked last, if the employee reported to each establishment for the same amount of time

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Reminder: If the employee's province of employment is not the same as their province of residence, they may have not enough or too much tax deducted. Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source.

Situation: Employee works in Canada but does not report for work (never physically and not considered attached) at an establishment of the employer

The province of employment is the province or territory where the employer's establishment is located from which the employee's salary is paid. This is normally the location of the employer’s payroll department or payroll records.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Reminder: If the employee's province of employment is not the same as their province of residence, they may have not enough or too much tax deducted. Learn more: Increase or reduce income tax deducted at source.

Situation: Employee works in Canada but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • Payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada

Do not use:

  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
POE for employment income: Employee is not a resident of Canada for the full year

Where the employee is not a Canadian resident for the whole year, the province of employment will change depending on their specific residency status.

Determine the POE based on the situation

Situation: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter

The province of employment is where employment duties are performed if one of the following situations applies:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada

Situation: Employee is a part-year resident

For the part of the year that the employee is either:

  • A resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a resident of Canada
  • Not a resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter
Situation: Employee is a deemed resident or sojourner

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory if one of the following situations applies:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • Payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada

Do not use:

  • TD1 provincial and territorial form

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada

POE for non-employment income
for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP

The province of employment is the recipient's province or territory of residence if you pay amounts other than employment income, such as pension income, retiring allowance, or RRSP.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables
