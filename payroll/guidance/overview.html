Types of employment status for tax purposes

The main types of employment status are:

  • Employee
  • Self-employed and contractor

An individual may be an employee in employment law but have a different status under the tax law. As an employer or a payer, you must work out each worker’s status in both employment law and tax law.

Tax law

The CRA determines the employment status on the basis of common law and the Canada Pension Plan, Employment Insurance Act and the ITA, as well as the Civil Code of Quebec in the case of Quebec workers.

As a contract of employment is not defined in the Canada Pension Plan, Employment Insurance Act and the ITA, the meaning of this term is determined from the relevant legislation for the province in which the contract takes place, either the Civil Code of Quebec or the common law.

Employment law

Provinciale or Territorial Employment Standards Code

If the employee is not working in a federally regulated industry, the employment standards legislation of the province or territory where the employment takes place will apply.

Canada Labour Code

Employment and Social Development Canada is responsible for enforcing the Canada Labour Code (CLC) for workers in federally regulated industries. The federal labour standards set out the employment conditions for hours of work, payment of wages, leaves, vacation, holidays, and more. These standards apply only to employees working in federally regulated businesses.

Learn more: Federal labour standards.

ESDC also makes decisions about employment status for the purposes of the CLC and provide a guide regarding how they make these determinations.

Learn more: Determining the Employer/Employee Relationship - IPG-069 .

Review the contract

There must be a contract in place to see whether the worker is considered an employee or self-employed. The guidance assumes there is, or will be, a contract in place.

Contracts can be formed either orally or in writing. An oral contract is as valid and enforceable as a written contract.

The CRA may request a copy of the contract or testimony by the parties to review the intention of the parties in forming the agreement. While this agreement is important, the facts of the working relationship as a whole will determine the employment status and not the wording of the contract alone.

Types of contracts under the law

The main types of employment contracts are:

  • Contracts of service
  • Contracts for service
  • Contracts sui generis (of their own kind)

If you made an error in the determining the employment status

Individuals and employers may have to pay unpaid tax and penalties, or lose entitlement to benefits, if their employment status is wrong.

If you are not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed

Request a ruling from CRA

You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an employee or is self-employed. You can use the CRA guidance to help you gather the facts based on your situation.

If you are still not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed after following the CRA guidance, you can ask for a CPP/EI ruling.
