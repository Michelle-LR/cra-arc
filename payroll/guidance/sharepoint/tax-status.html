  • Type of supply: Residential Sales - Sale of used owner-occupied home (Resale of an individual's personal residence)

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Taxable situation

    The resale of an individual's personal residence is taxable at 5%, 13% or 15% when any of the following applies:

    • It was substantially renovated (link to pop-up to definition below) for the purpose of selling it
    • It was renovated(link to pop-up see below) and resold in a business venture
    • The property was used in a business or commercial activity, including use as short-term accommodation.

    Exempt situations

    The resale by an individual of their personal residence is genrally eempt. See "taxable situation" above for rexceptions.

    DEFINITION OF SUBSTANTIAL RENO "Substantial renovation is generally when all of the interior of the existing building has been virtually removed or replaced (in other words, gutted) and, upon completion, the renovated or altered building is, or forms part of, a residential complex", RENO "In this siutuation - renovation includes all renovations, whether substantial or otherwise"

    Example

    An individual purchased a unit in a condominium building. After residing in the condominium unit, the individual sold it and purchased a larger home. The sale of the condominium unit is exempt because it was the individual's residence.

    Tax status if all requirements are met: Exempt

    ETA: Subsections 123(1) and 190(1), Schedule V, Part I, sections 2, 4 and 5

  • Type of supply: Residential Sales - Sale of new or substantially renovated housing

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Taxable situation

    The sale of a new or substantially renovated housing by a vendor who is a builder (link to pop-up) for GST/HST purposes is taxable at 5%, 13% or 15%.

    Substantial renovation is generally when all of the interior of the existing building has been removed or replaced (in other words, gutted) and, upon completion, the renovated or altered building is, or forms part of, a residential complex.

    Note: The purchaser of new housing is required to remit the GST/HST directly to the Receiver General of Canada when one of the following apply:

    • The supplier is a non-resident
    • The purchaser is not an individual and is registered for the GST/HST.
    • Residential real property is sold to a GST/HST registrant that is not an individual

    Exempt situation

    Generally, the sale of new or sbstanially renovated housing is taxable at 5%, 13% or 15%. In rare circumstances, the sale may be exempt where the sale is not made in the course of a business or in the course of an adveture or concern in the nature of trade.

    Example

    An individual purchases or builds a new home with the intention of living in it. Circumstances changed and the individual decides to sell the home without having live in it. Even though the individual is selling new housing, the sale is exempt because the sale is not made in the course of a business or in the course of an adventure or concern in the nautre of trade.

    Example 2: A married couple purchased an older home. After residing in the house, they noticed several issues with it and decided to gut it and renovate it. They sold the house when they finished renovating the home. The sale is exempt since the house was previously occupied by the owners as their place of residence and the renovations were not made for the purpose of selling it.

    Example 3: A builder and his family reside in their custom-built home. The builder claimed input tax credits (ICs) on the building supplies when constructing this home. When the time comes to sell the home, the builder will charge GST/HST on the sale of it.

    To make an enquiry on these and other complex situations, please call the CRA GST/HST Rulings Centres toll-free at 1-800-959-8287.

    Tax status if all requirements are met: Taxable at 5%, 13% or 15% depending on the location

    ETA: subsections 123(1), 190(1), 228(4), Section 192 and Schedule V, Part I, section 2

  • Type of supply: Residential Sales - Mixed-use real property (home and business)

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Taxable situation

    For mixed-use real property, the commercial portion being sold is deemed to be a separate supply than the residential portion. For the tax status of each portion, if it would have been exempt when sold on its own, that portion will be exempt when sold together.

    The tax status for the commercial portion is taxable in the most common situations. See "Commercial Sales" for details.

    Exempt situation

    The sale of the residential portion is deemed to be a separate supply than the remaining portion of the real property (for example, commercial real property). If the residential portion would have been exempt when sold on its own, it will be exempt when sold together with taxable real property

    Learn more: GST/HST Memorandum 19.2.1 - Residential real property - Sales

    Note: The recipient of a taxable sale of real property is required to remit the GST/HST directly to the Receiver General for Canada when:

    • the supplier is a non-resident,
    • non-residential real property is sold to a GST/HST registrant, or
    • residential real property is sold to a GST/HST registrant that is not an individual

        Example

        A building consists of a convenience store on the ground floor, an apartment and additional space above the store that is used for storage, which is where the store deliveries are made. The sale of only that portion of the property that is commercial real property is taxable. The residential portion (apartment) is exempt if it would have been exempt when sold on its own.

        Tax status if all requirements are met: Taxable at 5%, 13% or 15% depending on the location

        ETA: Subsection 136(2) and Schedule V, Part I, section 2

  • Type of supply: Accommodation in a non-residential complex (such as a hotel or AirBnb)

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    The rent charged for the use of a residential unit that is not in a residential complex is usually taxable, regardless of the period of occupancy, unless the unit is rented for $20 or less per day. For further information, refer to the topic, “Application of Residential Complex to Hotels” in Section 19.2, Residential Real Property.

    Example

    A hotel must charge GST/HST on the rental of a unit for a period of continuous occupancy by an individual, if the accommodation is provided at more than $20 per day.

    Note: A hotel or motel does not qualify as a residential complex if 90% or more of the supplies of the residential units by way of lease, licence, or similar arrangement are, or are expected to be, for periods of less than 60 days.

  • Type of supply: Residental rentals - Accommodation for 30 days or less of continuous occupancy (short term) and rented at $20 or less for each day of occupancy

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Short-term accommodation (the period of occupancy is less than 1 consecutive month) the supply is taxable if provided by a registrant, unless the consideration for the supply is $20 or less per day of occupancy.

    Taxable situation

    Short-term accommodation (such as in a rooming or boarding house) is taxable at 5%, 13% or 15% unless the rent payable for occupancy is $20 per day or less.

    Exempt situation

    Charges for accommodation in a non-residential building are exempt if the amount payable for occupancy is $20 per day or less, regardless of the period of occupancy.

    Learn more: GST/HST Memorandum 19-2-2 Residential Real Property - Rentals

    Example

    A “bed and breakfast” establishment charges $20 per day for its rooms and separately charges $5 per day for breakfast if breakfast is requested. Rooms are rented on a short-term basis. In this example, there are two supplies:
  • the supply of the room is exempt (whether or not the establishment is a residential complex) while the supply of the breakfast is taxable.

    If the establishment did not charge separately for the breakfast and charged a total of $25 per day for a room and breakfast, the full charge would be taxable because the total cost is more than $20 per day.

    If the individual renting a room does not request the breakfast and pays $20 per day for the room, they would not pay GST/HST on the amount they paid to rent the room.

    Example 2 Rents charged for the use of a residential unit that is not within a residential complex are usually taxable, regardless of the period of occupancy, unless the unit is rented for $20 or less per day.

    Example 2 An individual rents a room at a hotel for $15 per day for a period of continuous occupancy, the supply may be exempt.

    Tax status if all requirements are met: Exempt

    ETA: Schedule V, Part I, section 7 and paragraph 6 (b)

  • Type of supply: Residential rentals - Accommodation for more than 30 days of continuous occupancy (long term)

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Taxable situation

    The supply is taxable if the residential unit is:

    • used by a person other than an individual using the property as a place of residence
    • The period of occupany is less than 1 month
    • The amount payable for ocupany is more than $20 per day
    • The supplier is registered for the GST/HST

    Exempt situation

    The rent or lease (or sublease) of the same unit to the same individual is exempt even though the rent may be payable on a daily or weekly basis.

    Learn more: GST/HST Memorandum 19-2-2 Residential Real Property - Rentals

    Example

    A company rents a residential unit for the benefit of its employees while the employee is on a six-month work assignment. This lease is exempt since the purpose of the occupancy is as a place of resident or lodging for an indivual, and the individual occupies the unit for a least one month.

    Example 2 An individual enters into an agreement to rent a condominium from February 1st to July 31st and pays the rent every week. The rent is exempt from GST/HST since the occupancy is for a continuous period of more than 30 days.

    Tax status if all requirements are met: Exempt

    ETA: Schedule V, Part I, section 7 and paragraph 6 (a)

  • Type of supply: Farmland - Sale

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Taxable situations

    The sale of farmland is taxable when:

  • it is sold to a person other than an individual who is related to the individual .

    Exempt situations

    The sale of farmland is generally exempt when:

    • farmland is sold to related persons
    • an individual farmer sells or transfers ownership of farmland to an individual related to the farmer (or a former spouse or former common-law partner of the farmer) who, in turn, uses the land for the individual's own personal use and enjoyment,
    • the farmer begins to use the land for their own personal use and enjoyment
    • /p> The sale of farmland is exempt if a corporation, partnership or trust sells the farmland to a particular individual, an individual related to the particular individual, a former spouse or former common-law partner of the particular individual and if the conditions of the following 2 points are met:
    • Immediately before the time ownership of the property is transferred, the following conditions have been met:
    • all or substantially all of the property of the person is used in a commercial activity that is the business of farming
    • the particular individual is a member of the partnership, a beneficiary of the trust or a shareholder of, or related to, the corporation
    • the particular individual, the spouse or common-law partner of the particular individual or a child (within the meaning of subsection 70(10) of the Income Tax Act) of the particular individual is actively engaged in the business of the person
    • Immediately after the ownership of the property is transferred, the farmland is for the buyer's personal use and enjoyment, or for that of a related individual.

    Learn more: GST/HST Memorandum 19.5, Land and Associated Real Property

    Learn more: GST/HST Info Sheet, Sales of Farmland by Individuals

    NOTE: If a person sells farmland as part of an ongoing farming business, the supplier and recipient may jointly elect, using Form GST44, to have no GST/HST payale on the taxable sale. To qualify for the election, the supplier must sell all or part of the business, not just the assets and the recipient must carry on the business.

    Get a copy of the form at : GST44 GST/HST Election Concerning the Acquisition of a Business or Part of a Business - Canada.ca

    Example

    Example 1: Mike, who was previously engaged in a profitable farming business, has not grown crops on a particular parcel of property for the past two years. He has cultivated the land to control the weeds. He sells the land to his nephew who will use the land solely for his personal use and enjoyment. This sale is exempt. If John buys the land so that he can continue using it in farming activities, the sale would be taxable.

    Example 2: George owns farmland and sells it to a corporation. The sales of the farmland is taxable.

    Example 3:Mary uses her farmland in a commercial business but not farming. She sells it to her neighbour, Terry. The sale is taxable because they are not related to each other and Mary used the land for reasons other than farming.

    Example 2: A schoolteacher owns a farm where she undertakes limited farming activities without a reasonable expectation of profit. She decides to sell the farm to her brother. Since the teacher did not use the land at any time in a commercial activity that is the business of farming, the sale of the farmland is not exempt under the provisions of section 10 of Part I of Schedule V. It should be noted, however, that the sale would be exempt under section 9 of Part I of Schedule V.

  • Type of supply: Sale of vacant land (other than farmland) - Sale

    More information and example

    Business activity: Most businesses

    Category of supply: Real property

    Taxable situation

    The sale of vacant land (other than farmland) is taxable at 5%, 13% or 15% in either of the following situations: The land is owned and sold by a partnership or a corporation

  • The land was severed into more than 2 parts, or had previously severed the land from another parcel

    • NOTE: Exception for expropriation. For this rule, a part of a parcel of land that the individual, trust or settlor supplies to a person who has the right to acquire it by expropriation, and the remainder of that parcel, are deemed not to have been subdivided or severed from each other by the individual, trust or settlor.

    Exempt situation

    The sale of the vacant land (other than farmland) is exempt when sold by an individual to a relative for their personal use and enjoyment, or when the seller did not subdivide or sever the land into parts.

    <

    Learn more: GST/HST Memorandum 19.5, Land and Associated Real Property.

    Example

    Mr. Acres at one time owned a three-hectare parcel of land in Ontario. In 2012, he subdivided that parcel into one two-hectare lot and one one-hectare lot. He sold the one-hectare lot at that time. Since subparagraph 9(2)(c)(i) of Part I of Schedule V provides that a supply of a part of a parcel of land by an individual is exempt if the parcel is subdivided or severed into only two parts and if the originating parcel had not been subdivided or severed from another parcel, the sale of that one-hectare lot was exempt. In January 2020, Mr. Acres subdivided the remaining two-hectare lot into two lots of one hectare each and sold those lots to people who are not related to him. Until that sale, the vacant land had been used primarily for Mr. Acres' personal use and enjoyment. Since these one-hectare lots were produced by the further severance of the two-hectare lot which had itself been severed from the original three-hectare parcel, his sale of these one-hectare lots to people who are not related to him is GST/HST taxable at 13%. Even though there is a gap in time in this example between the first subdivision and the second, the rules found in subparagraph 9(2)(c)(i) made the January 2020 sale of the remaining lots taxable.

    Tax status if all requirements are met: Taxable at 5%, 13% or 15% depending on the location

    ETA: Schedule V, Part I, paragraph 9(2)(c)
