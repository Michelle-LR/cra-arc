Self-employed and payer relationship: Understand the tax obligations

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language, including additional examples and legislative references.

If you are not sure if the worker is self-employed, continue to: Determine if the worker is an employee or self-employed

Understanding the tax obligations based on the individual:

  • Self-employed - Worker
  • Payer - Payments made to a self-employed worker
Payer

Understanding the payer's tax obligations:

  • Payroll obligations
  • Income tax obligations
  • GST/HST obligations
  • Other tax obligations

Payroll obligations

Generally, any person must open a payroll account, calculate payroll deductions and contributions, remit (pay) deductions and contributions, and file information returns if they make payments to a self-employed worker.

Withholding and remitting deductions

Generally, the payer does not withhold or remit the income tax, EI, CPP or GST/HST on the payment made to a self-employed worker.

Under special EI regulations, the payer must withhold and remit EI on behalf of the self-employed worker if the worker is a:

  • Fisher
  • Barber or hairdresser
  • Taxi or other passenger vehicle driver

Learn more: Get ready to make deductions

If the payer makes a payment in a self-employment relationship, the employer must report on the following slip depending on who the payment is made to:

  • T4 slip

    Self-employed fisher, barber, hairdresser, taxi driver, or a driver of other passenger vehicle

  • T4A slip

    Self-employed farmer and other self-employed workers

Learn more: Payroll

Income tax obligations

The payer must report and pay tax on all income earned from taxable activities. The income is the net earnings from employment, property, investments and benefits. To report and pay tax, the payer must calculate the business income and expenses using one of the following form when filing their income tax return:

  • T1 return
    • Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities
    • Form T2042, Statement of Farming Activities
    • Form T2121, Statement of Fishing Activities

    Learn more: Lines 13499 to 14300 – Self-employment income

  • T2 return

    Schedule 125, Income Statement Information

    Learn more: Completing your corporation income tax return

GST/HST obligations

Generally, any person must open a GST/HST account, collect, file and remit (pay) the GST/HST if they provide goods or services and earn more than $30,000 over 4 calendar quarters.

Learn more: Collect, file, and remit (pay) the GST/HST

Other tax obligations

Depending on the type of industry, the payer may have other tax obligations, for example:

  • Fuel charge
Self-employed

Understanding the self-employed's tax obligations:

  • Income tax obligations
  • Remitting the GST/HST on payments made by the self-employed worker
  • Payroll and GST/HST obligations for employees of the self-employed worker

Income tax obligations

When filing the T1 return, the self-employed worker:

  • Calculates the amount of CPP/QPP contributions because a self-employed worker is responsible for both the employer’s and employee's contributions to the plan using one of the following:

    • Schedule 8, CPP Contributions and Overpayment (for all except QC)
    • Schedule 8, QPP Contributions (for QC only)
    • Form RC381, Inter-Provincial Calculation for CPP and QPP Contributions and Overpayments

    Learn more: Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions for CPP working beneficiaries

  • Calculates the amount of EI premiums only if the self-employed worker is not a barber, hairdresser, fisher, taxi driver, or a driver of other passenger vehicles using Schedule 13, EI Premiums on Self-Employment and Other Eligible Earnings (under special EI regulations, the payer is responsible to remit EI on behalf of the worker on all of their insurable earnings)

    Learn more: EI benefits for self-employed people

  • Calculates and reports business income and expenses using:

    • Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities
    • Form T2042, Statement of Farming Activities
    • Form T2121, Statement of Fishing Activities

    Learn more: Report business income and expenses

Receiving the slip

Generally, the payer must provide the self-employed worker with a T4A or T4 slip which includes any payment made regardless if the income is taxable or not.

Learn more:

  • Options if the self-employed worker does not expect to get the slip: Get a copy of a slip

Remitting the GST/HST on payments made by the self-employed worker

Generally, the self-employed worker must remit the GST/HST on taxable supplies or services provided by them.

Learn more: GST/HST for businesses

Payroll and GST/HST obligations for employees of the self-employed worker

The payroll and GST/HST obligations for employees of the self-employed worker are the same as regular employers.

Learn more: Employee and employer relationship: Understand the tax obligations

Keeping records

Individuals must keep track of all of the income that they earned and the expenses that they incurred for business purposes.

Learn more: Keeping records

Help for small businesses

Small businesses that would like help in understanding their tax obligations can ask for a visit from a Canada Revenue Agency Liaison Officer.

Learn more: Liaison Officer service - free tax help for small business owners and self-employed individuals.

Correcting tax affairs

Individuals that would like to correct their tax affair can apply for relief under the Voluntary Disclosure Program.

Learn more: Voluntary Disclosures Program – Introduction

Consequences of non-compliance

When non-compliance is identified, the CRA:

  • Educates the individual on their tax obligations
  • Ensures the correct returns are prepared and filed
  • Takes the necessary enforcement actions to ensure compliance with the law

Depending on the situation, penalties may be assessed. If individuals do not pay an amount that is due, the CRA may apply interest from the day the payment was due.
