Self-employed and payer relationship: Understand your tax obligations

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language, including additional examples and legislative references.

Depending if the worker is an employee, self-employed or an incorporated employee the tax obligations may be different.

If you are not sure if the worker is self-employed, continue to: Determine if the worker is an employee or self-employed

Understanding the tax obligations based on the individual:

  • Self-employed worker
  • Payer - Payments made to a self-employed worker
Self-employed worker

Understanding the self-employed's tax obligations:

  • Reporting the income earned on the income tax return
  • Contributing to the CPP/QPP, EI and QPIP
  • Withholding, remitting and reporting deductions for employees of the self-employed worker

Income tax obligations

Generally, the self-employed worker calculates and reports the following amounts when they fill out their T1 income tax and benefit return:

  • Income Tax

Learn more: Business expenses self-employed workers may deduct

CPP/QPP, EI and QPP obligations

Generally, the self-employed worker calculates their contributions and reports the following amounts when they fill out their T1 income tax and benefit return:

  • EI - Only if the self-employed worker participates in the special EI program
  • CPP - Both employer and employee portion
  • QPIP - Only if the self-employed worker works in the Province of Quebec

Learn more:

  • Contributions to the Canada Pension Plan
  • EI benefits for self-employed people
  • Québec Parental Insurance Plan (QPIP) | Revenu Québec

GST/HST obligations

Generally, the self-employed worker remits the following on the payment made by them:

  • GST/HST in certain situations

Learn more: GST/HST for businesses

Payer

Understanding the payer's tax obligations:

  • Withholding and remitting deductions from the payment
  • Reporting the payment on the slip
  • Reporting the payment on the income tax return

Withholding and remitting deductions from the payment

Generally, the payer does not withhold or remit the following deductions on the payment made to the self-employed worker:

  • Income tax
  • EI
  • CPP
  • GST/HST

Learn more: Get ready to make deductions

Reporting the payment on the slip

Generally, the payer must report the payment made to the self-employed worker on a T4A slip. Depending on the situation, the payer may need to fill out a T4A-NR instead.

Learn more: Fill out the slips and summaries

Reporting the payment on the income tax return

Generally, the payer includes the payment made to the worker in their calculation of business expenses using one of the following form when filing their income tax return:

  • T1 return

    • Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities
    • Form T2042, Statement of Farming Activities
    • Form T2121, Statement of Fishing Activities

    Learn more: Lines 13499 to 14300 – Self-employment income

  • T2 return

    Schedule 125, Income Statement Information

    Learn more: Completing your corporation income tax return

Receiving or distributing the slip

Slips are prepared by the employer or the payer. If the slip is late or lost, the worker can ask the payer of the slip for a copy.

If the payer distributes the slips late or does not distribute, the payer may be assessed a penalty of $25 per day per slip with a minimum penalty of $100 and a maximum of $2,500.

Learn more:

  • Options if the worker does not expect to get the slip, Get a copy of a slip
  • Obligations to distribute the slip, Distribute the slips

Keeping records

Individuals must keep track of all of the income that they earned and the expenses that they incurred for business purposes.

Learn more: Keeping records

Help for small businesses

Small businesses that would like help in understanding their tax obligations can ask for a visit from a Canada Revenue Agency Liaison Officer.

Learn more: Liaison Officer service - free tax help for small business owners and self-employed individuals.

Correcting tax affairs

Individuals that would like to correct their tax affair can apply for relief under the Voluntary Disclosure Program.

Learn more: Voluntary Disclosures Program – Introduction

Consequences of non-compliance

Depending on the situation, penalties may be assessed. If individuals do not pay an amount that is due, the CRA may apply interest from the day the payment was due.
