Determine if parties are dealing at arm's length

The following will help you determine if parties are dealing at arm's length with each other.

Related persons are deemed not to deal at arm's length with each other.

What person's include

Person includes:

  • An individual
  • A corporation
  • Any entity listed in subsection 149(1)
  • Heirs, executors, liquidators of a succession, administrators or other legal representatives of such a person
  • A partnership, for GST/HST purposes, but not for income tax purposes

Examples of entities listed in subsection 149(1) include municipalities, Crown corporations, registered charities, universities, athletic associations, labour organizations, non-profit organizations and trusts.

Steps

  1. Determine if persons are related

    You must determine if persons are related depending on the type of persons.

    Who is the person?

    Individuals

    The individuals in the following 4 situations are related to each other:

    Blood relationship

    The following individuals are related by blood:

    • a parent and a child (or other descendant, such as a grandchild or a great-grandchild)
    • a brother and a sister

    Blood relatives do not include more distant relatives such as aunts, uncles, nieces, nephews or cousins.

    Example

    Zuly and Silvia are sisters. Zuly is married to Juan and Silvia is married to Yuri. Zuly and Silvia are related because they are sisters. Zuly and Juan are related by marriage. Silvia and Yuri are related by marriage. As a result of their marriages, Yuri is related to Zuly and Juan is related to Silvia, but Juan and Yuri are not related to each other.

    Marriage

    Two individuals are related to each other if they are legally married to each other. They are also related to anyone connected by blood relationship (parents and siblings) to their spouse. They may be related by adoption to any children of their spouse, depending on the circumstances. For more information, see Situation 4 - Adoption

    If the marriage is dissolved because of divorce or death of a spouse, the individual ceases to be connected by marriage.

    Example

    Jack and Jill were married on June 15, 2016. Later, their marriage broke down and they were legally divorced on October 1, 2019. For the period from the date of marriage until the date of their legal divorce, Jack and Jill were related to each other as well as their respective parents-in-law and brothers/sisters-in-law.

    Common-law

    Two individuals are related to each other if they are in a common-law partnership with each other. CRA considers that two individuals are in a common-law partnership if:

    • they live together in a conjugal relationship and have done so for a continuous period of at least 12 months
    • they live together in a conjugal relationship and have a child together (this includes through adoption), in which case the 12-month period does not apply

    They are also related to anyone connected by blood relationship (parents and siblings) to their common-law partner. They may also be related by adoption to any children of their common-law partner, depending on the circumstances. For more information, see Situation 4 - Adoption.

    If there is a breakdown in the relationship, they are considered to continue to live in a common-law partnership until they have lived separate and apart for 90 consecutive days.

    Example

    Jack and Jill had a child together. They began living together in a conjugal relationship 1 month before their child was born. They are common-law partners as of the day their child is born. If they did not have a child together, they would not be common-law partners until they lived together for 12 months.

    Adoption

    Two individuals are related to each other through adoption if one individual is the adopted child of the other. In addition to their parents, the child is also related to their siblings, grandparents and great-grandparents.

    Adoption includes legal adoption and de facto adoption. The following factors are considered when determining whether a de facto adoption exists:

    • actual control and custody
    • an exercise of parental care and responsibility on a continuing basis
    • dependency
    • proximity to each other
    Example

    Jack and Jill are related to two of their children by blood (Mark and Tom) and one of their children by adoption (Eleanor).

    Eleanor is related to her siblings (Mark and Tom) and her four grandparents (the parents of Jack and Jill).
