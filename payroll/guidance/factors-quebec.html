Gather the facts

Answer a few questions based on the following factors to help you decide the worker classification.

The results from each question need to be viewed as a whole in order to help you decide if an employee or a self-employed person.

Factors

  1. Contract arrangement

    The CRA asks the worker and the payer what their intent was when they entered into the working arrangement. Workers and payers can choose how they set up their affairs. However, the employment status they choose must reflect their working relationship.

    Factor 1 - Question and indicator

    At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

    • Employee-employer
    • Self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

    If the contract of service is in writing, it normally contains information such as:

    • Scale or rate of pay
    • Hours of work
    • Location of work
    • Period of employment
    Example

    Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed on the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $10 an hour.

    Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

    Example

    Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

    IT Computers Ltd. hires Paul’s services under a written agreement for a period of 3 months. Paul’s Beautiful Gardens will maintain the lawns and shrubs for a monthly fee of $2,000.

    In this case, Paul has an agreement to perform specific work for IT Computers Ltd. in return for payment. Paul has entered into a valid contract for services with IT Computers Ltd. and is considered a self-employed individual.

    Factor 2 - Questions and indicators

    Is the worker listed on payer’s payroll?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Expenses related to an employee would generally be reported as salary/wage expenses.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Expenses related to a self-employed worker would generally be a subcontractor or other type of expense.

    Were there CPP, EI or income tax deductions withheld from the worker’s pay?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    A payer is required to withhold source deductions from an workers pay who is an employee.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is required to remit their own source deductions from the amounts they are paid.

    Was a pay stub given to the worker when they were remunerated?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Workers who are employees are usually given a pay stub showing their gross income, source deductions and net pay.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Self-employed workers generally invoice the payer for the services they have rendered.

    Was the worker issued a T4 information slip?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    T4 information slips are given to workers who are employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    T4 information slips are not given to workers who are self-employed. A self-employed person may receive a T4a, or a T5013 information slip instead.

    In addition to remuneration, did the worker receive vacation pay, paid vacation leave, statutory holiday or sick leave?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Additional benefits such as vacation and sick leave are generally provided to workers who are employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Additional benefits such as vacation and sick leave are generally not provided to workers who are self-employed.

    Did the worker participate or belong to the payer’s private pension plan?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Additional benefits such as participation in the payer’s pension plan are generally provided to workers who are employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Additional benefits such as participation in the payer’s pension plan are generally not provided to workers who are self-employed.

    Was the worker entitled or belonged to the payer’s benefit plans (for example: fitness membership, medical or dental plans)?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Additional benefits plans such as medical/dental plans are generally provided to workers who are employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Additional benefits plans such as medical/dental plans are generally not provided to workers who are self-employed.

    Was the worker listed as part of the staff in the payer’s staff phone directory or payer’s website?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer’s phone directory generally contains workers who are employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The payer’s phone directory generally does not contains workers who are self-employed.

    Was the worker named as part of the staff in any of the payer’s business brochure?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer’s business brochure would only list workers who are employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The payer’s business brochure would not list workers who are self-employed.

    Did the payer provide business cards to the worker?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Workers who are employees would have business cards of the payer.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Workers who are self-employed would have business cards bearing their own business’s name.

    Was the worker’s title or position listed on the payers business cards?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Workers who are employees generally have a job title or position within the payer’s organization.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    Workers who are self employed would not have an official job title or position within the payer’s organization.

  4. Remuneration

    In both types of arrangements (employee or self-employed) remuneration must be paid or due to the worker. However, what the remuneration was based on, the frequency of payments, whether there were deductions withheld, and how the worker requests payments can be key indicators of either a work who is an employee or one who is self-employed.

    Factor 4 - Questions and indicators

    Were there any non-monetary benefits associated with the remuneration (for example: paid vacation leave, medical/dental coverage, insurance plan, use of company's car)?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Benefits such as paid vacation leave, medical dental are generally reserved for employees.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A self employed person is generally not offered additional benefits such as vacation leave, medical/dental, by a payer.

    What was the agreed remuneration based on hourly/monthly rate of pay/piece work? If the worker is paid by the hour, is the worker entitled to overtime pay?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Workers who are employees are generally paid for their time while working on a task.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A worker who is self employed is generally paid based on completion of a task or job.

    If the worker is paid by the hour, is the worker entitled to overtime pay?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    Overtime pay is generally included as part of a contract with an employee.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A worker who is paid based on the completion of a task rather than by the hour is more than likely to be considered self employed.

    Were deductions withheld from the payments?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    A worker who is an employee generally has source deductions withheld from their pay for income taxes, CPP and EI.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A worker who is self employed is generally responsible for maintaining their own records and remitting taxes on their own behalf.

    Does the worker have a right to file a complaint with Employment Standards/Labor Standards for non-payments?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker is an employee when they have a right to make a complaint under the Employment Standards/Labour Standards for wages they are owed.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is in the business on their own and not entitled to make a complaint under Employment Standards but under other laws as applicable.

Analyze the facts

You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will move the worker into one side of the continuum, and confirm if there is an employment relationship, or if the worker is in business on his own account.

You must analyze the answer to each question from both the payer and the worker to help you decide if the evidence moves the worker to the employee or self-employed side of the continuum.

  • Worker is an employee
  • Worker is self-employed

Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support an employee-employer relationship and other facts indicate self-employement.

To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the working relationship of the parties. You must review the indicators in the factors of control, ownership of tools and equipment, opportunity for profit and financial risk, and responsibility for investment and management. As you respond to each question you should be able to decide what weight to assign each category based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, control may favour an employee relationship, but ownership of tools may be assigned more weight due to how the total relationship is structured. This may be an indicator because of the increased weight given to this factor, the worker is moved into the independent contractor side of the continuum.

Case-by-case basis

Whether a worker is an employee or self-employed needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to move a worker to one side of the continuum or the other, you must examine the total working relationship. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

Consider special situations

A deemed employer is one of the following:

  • a third party that generally controls, directly supervises, or pays the remuneration to an insured person in its own name and not on behalf of the actual employer.
  • a person who has rights or privileges on premises or property under a licence, permit, or agreement and who agrees to allow another employer's workers to work on these premises or property.

Analyzing the relationship:

  • who supervises the worker?
  • where do the workers perform their tasks?
  • does the party paying the salary act in its own name?
  • what is the procedure for issuing pay?
  • where do the funds necessary for the payment of the salaries come from?
