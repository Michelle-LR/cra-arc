Gather the facts

   Factor 1 - Contract arrangement

The CRA asks the worker and the payer what their intent was when they entered into the working arrangement. Workers and payers can choose how they set up their affairs. However, the employment status they choose must reflect their working relationship.

At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

  • Employee-employer
  • Self-employed

   Indicator: Worker is an employee

The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

If the contract of service is in writing, it normally contains information such as:

  • Scale or rate of pay
  • Hours of work
  • Location of work
  • Period of employment
Example

Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed on the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $10 an hour.

Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

Continue to: Factor 2 - Documents to review

   Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

Example

Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

IT Computers Ltd. hires Paul’s services under a written agreement for a period of 3 months. Paul’s Beautiful Gardens will maintain the lawns and shrubs for a monthly fee of $2,000.

In this case, Paul has an agreement to perform specific work for IT Computers Ltd. in return for payment. Paul has entered into a valid contract for services with IT Computers Ltd. and is considered a self-employed individual.

    Factor 2 - Documents to review

When reviewing the worker’s and payer’s documents, the type of documents and the manner in which the information is documented can provide important clues into the relationship between the 2 parties.

Is the worker listed on payer’s payroll?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Expenses related to an employee would generally be reported as salary/wage expenses.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Expenses related to a self-employed worker would generally be a subcontractor or other type of expense.

Were there CPP, EI or income tax deductions withheld from the worker’s pay?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

A payer is required to withhold source deductions from an workers pay who is an employee.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker is required to remit their own source deductions from the amounts they are paid.

Was a pay stub given to the worker when they were remunerated?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees are usually given a pay stub showing their gross income, source deductions and net pay.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Self-employed workers generally invoice the payer for the services they have rendered.

Was the worker issued a T4 information slip?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

T4 information slips are given to workers who are employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

T4 information slips are not given to workers who are self-employed. A self-employed person may receive a T4A, or a T5013 information slip instead.

In addition to remuneration, did the worker receive vacation pay, paid vacation leave, statutory holiday or sick leave?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Additional benefits such as vacation and sick leave are generally provided to workers who are employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Additional benefits such as vacation and sick leave are generally not provided to workers who are self-employed.

Did the worker participate or belong to the payer’s private pension plan?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Additional benefits such as participation in the payer’s pension plan are generally provided to workers who are employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Additional benefits such as participation in the payer’s pension plan are generally not provided to workers who are self-employed.

Was the worker entitled or belonged to the payer’s benefit plans (for example: fitness membership, medical or dental plans)?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Additional benefits plans such as medical/dental plans are generally provided to workers who are employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Additional benefits plans such as medical/dental plans are generally not provided to workers who are self-employed.

Was the worker listed as part of the staff in the payer’s staff phone directory or payer’s website?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The payer’s phone directory generally contains workers who are employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The payer’s phone directory generally does not contains workers who are self-employed.

Was the worker named as part of the staff in any of the payer’s business brochure?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The payer’s business brochure would only list workers who are employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The payer’s business brochure would not list workers who are self-employed.

Did the payer provide business cards to the worker?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees would have business cards of the payer.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Workers who are self-employed would have business cards bearing their own business’s name.

Was the worker’s title or position listed on the payers business cards?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees generally have a job title or position within the payer’s organization.

Continue to: Factor 3 - Performance of work

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Workers who are self-employed would not have an official job title or position within the payer’s organization.

   Factor 3 - Performance of work

Control over where, when and how the work is done  need to be considered when determining the degree of control held by the payer or the degree of independence held by the worker. The actual degree of control will vary with the type of work and the skills of the worker.

Did the worker’s position have a job description in the payer’s internal policies?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

A worker who is an employee would have a job description provided by the payer.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed would not have a job description provided by the payer.

Which location did the worker perform the agreed service?

  • Payer
  • Worker
  • Client

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees generally report to and carry out their tasks at the payer’s location.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed generally have more control over where they complete their work.

Indicator: Facts are neutral

The client’s location is not indicative of an employee or self-employed.

Who determined where the worker performed the agreed service?

  • Payer
  • Worker

Indicator: Worker is an employee

A worker who is an employee generally has less control over where work is to be completed.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed can exercise more control over where they carry out their work, however there may still be limitations, depending on the task.

Who determined when the worker performed the agreed service?

  • Payer
  • Worker
  • Client

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The payer has greater control over when certain tasks should be completed.

Continue to: Factor 4 - Remuneration

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker has greater control over when certain tasks are to be completed.

Continue to: Factor 4 - Remuneration

Indicator: Facts are neutral

The client is not indicative of an employee or self-employed.

   Factor 4 - Remuneration

In both types of arrangements (employee or self-employed) remuneration must be paid or due to the worker. However, what the remuneration was based on, the frequency of payments, whether there were deductions withheld, and how the worker requests payments can be key indicators of either a work who is an employee or one who is self-employed.

Were there any non-monetary benefits associated with the remuneration?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Benefits such as paid vacation leave, medical dental are generally reserved for employees.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A self-employed person is generally not offered additional benefits such as vacation leave, medical/dental, by a payer.

What was the agreed remuneration based on?

  • Hourly/monthly
  • Percentage of job completion/piecework

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees are generally paid for their time while working on a task.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed is generally paid based on completion of a task or job.

If the worker is paid by the hour, is the worker entitled to overtime pay?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Overtime pay is generally included as part of a contract with an employee.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is paid based on the completion of a task rather than by the hour is more than likely to be considered self-employed.

Were deductions withheld from the payments?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

A worker who is an employee generally has source deductions withheld from their pay for income taxes, CPP and EI.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed is generally responsible for maintaining their own records and remitting taxes on their own behalf.

Does the worker have a right to file a complaint with Employment Standards/Labour Standards for non-payments?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker is an employee when they have a right to make a complaint under the Employment Standards/Labour Standards for wages they are owed.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker is in the business on their own and not entitled to make a complaint under Employment Standards but under other laws as applicable.

How did the worker request payments?

  • Submission of a timesheet
  • Issuance of an invoice

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker's days and hours of work performed are recorded by the payer. The worker is required to keep a record of the starting and finishing time of each day of work.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker maintains books and records for the services provided, revenues generated and expenses incurred.

Is the worker entitled to any reimbursement of expenses incurred?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker will not have any financial risk as any expenses will be reimbursed.

Continue to: Factor 5 - Relationship of subordination

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker bears financial risk and responsibility for their own expenses.

   Factor 5 - Relationship of subordination

Under the civil law the relationship of subordination constitutes the main element for determining a worker’s status. From the Civil Code of Quebec it is concluded that the existence of a relationship of subordination with respect to the performance of work is the main element of a contract of employment. The subordination is a legal subordination and, however minimal, suggests the existence of a contract of employment.

Did the worker work at a designated location?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees generally report to and carry out their tasks at the payer’s location.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed generally have more control over where they complete their work.

Did the payer have a right to determine where the worker performs their service?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Workers who are employees generally report to and carry out their tasks at the payer’s location.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed generally has more control over where work is completed.

Did the payer set or haves the right to set the worker’s work schedule?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

All decisions made by the worker for the days and hours of work are subject to the approval of the payer.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker can decide what days and hours wants to work and they are not subject to the approval of the payer.

Was training provided to the worker at no charge?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Training is paid by the payer and there is no requirement for the worker to take training on their own.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Training is the workers responsibility and at their own cost. The payer is not required to pay or reimburse the worker’s training expenses.

Did the worker have a job description?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

A worker who is an employee would have a job description provided by the payer.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

A worker who is self-employed would not have a job description provided by the payer.

Were there established procedures and or code of conduct policy in place at the payer’s business as to how the work was to be performed?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker must adhere to the procedures set by the payer in all aspects of their performance. The payer has the final say in how the work is to be done.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker is responsible for satisfying the payer’s needs, and rectifying any problems that may arise.

Did the worker have a supervisor?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker works under the direct or indirect supervision of the payer or someone in the payer’s business.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker generally works alone and unsupervised. The worker has no contact with the payer until the job is complete.

Did the payer give or have the right to instruct the worker as to how the work is to be performed?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker must adhere to the procedures set by the payer in all aspects of their performance.

For example, the payer has an operational procedures manual and code of conduct expectations in place.

The payer has the final say in how the work is to be done.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker is responsible for satisfying the payer’s needs, and rectifying any problems that may arise.

Did the payer regularly review the work performed by the worker?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The services performed by the worker are subject to the payer’s review as frequently as the payer determines.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The worker works independently and is ultimately responsible for meeting the payer’s expectations.

Did the payer have the right to impose penalties against the worker or discipline the worker for poor performance?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The payer can take corrective action in response to worker's poor performance.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The payer can't issues a verbal or written warning to the worker for poor performance or evaluation.

Does the payer have the right to dismiss the worker?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The payer can terminate the worker’s employment in accordance with employment laws.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

The payer can’t dismiss the worker with cause and notice, but could terminate the services required under the contract.

Was the worker classified in the payer’s records such as payroll ledger as an employee?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

Expenses related to an employee would generally be reported as salary/wage expenses.

Indicator: Worker is self-employed

Expenses related to a self-employed worker would generally be a subcontractor or other type of expense, and not in payroll records of the payer.

Were there any written communications such as emails or written correspondence between the payer and the worker that acknowledged that the worker was performing duties as an employee?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Worker is an employee

The worker is an employee as the payer and the worker refer to a relationship of employment.

Make your final analysis based on the 5 factors

   Analyze the facts

You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will move the worker into one side of the continuum, and confirm if there is an employment relationship, or if the worker is in business on his own account.

You must analyze the answer to each question from both the payer and the worker to help you decide if the evidence moves the worker to the employee or self-employed side of the continuum.

  • Worker is an employee
  • Worker is self-employed

   Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support an employee-employer relationship and other facts indicate self-employement.

To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the working relationship of the parties. You must review the indicators in the factors of control, ownership of tools and equipment, opportunity for profit and financial risk, and responsibility for investment and management. As you respond to each question you should be able to decide what weight to assign each category based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, control may favour an employee relationship, but ownership of tools may be assigned more weight due to how the total relationship is structured. This may be an indicator because of the increased weight given to this factor, the worker is moved into the independent contractor side of the continuum.

   Review each situation on a case-by-case basis

Whether a worker is an employee or self-employed needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to move a worker to one side of the continuum or the other, you must examine the total working relationship. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

Employee performing services on behalf of their own corporation

You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an incorporated employee. If the worker is an incorporated employee, special rules may apply to their corporation for income tax purposes if an individual performs the services on behalf of their own corporation. This may be considered a personal services business.

What is a person

A trust, individual, corporation, partnership or a succession, or a body that is an association, club, commission, union or another organization of any kind.

What is a specified shareholder

A specified shareholder of the corporation is an individual who directly or indirectly owns at least 10% of the issued shares of any class of a capital stock of the corporation or a related corporation.

What is a full time employee

An employee who works a full business day on each working day of the year, subject to normal absences due to illness or vacation.

What is an associated corporation

A corporation is associated with another corporation in a taxation year if, at some point in the year if one of the following apply:

  • One of the Corporations directly or indirectly controls, in any manner whatsoever, the other corporation
  • The two Corporations are directly or indirectly controlled, in any manner whatsoever, by the same person or same group of persons
  • Each of the Corporations is directly or indirectly controlled, in any manner whatsoever, by a person, and the person that controls one of the Corporations is related to the person that controls the other corporation, and either of the persons owns at least 25% of the issued shares of any class, other than a specified class, of the capital stock of each corporation
  • One of the Corporations is directly or indirectly controlled, in any manner whatsoever, by a person that is related to each member of a group of persons that controls the other corporation, and this person owns at least 25% of the issued shares of any class, other than a specified class, of the capital stock of the other corporation
  • Each of the Corporations is directly or indirectly controlled, in any manner whatsoever, by a related group and each member of one of the related groups is related to all of the members of the other related group, and one person who is a member of both related groups owned alone, or several persons who are members of both related groups owned together, in respect of each corporation, at least 25% of the issued shares of any class, other than a specified class, of the capital stock thereof
