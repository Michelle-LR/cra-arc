Automobile or motor vehicle benefits – Allowances or reimbursements provided to an employee for the use of their own vehicle

You may be looking for:

  • Motor vehicle expenses claimed by self-employed individuals

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide an allowance  or a reimbursement  to your employee to compensate for use of their automobile or motor vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if your employee is required to use their vehicle in the course of their employment duties

    You need to determine if your employee was required to use their own automobile or motor vehicle in the course of their employment duties.

    • If you did not require your employee to use their own vehicle in the course of their employment duties, the allowance or reimbursement paid to your employee is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance or the reimbursement to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If you required your employee to use their own vehicle in the course of their employment duties, the allowance or reimbursement paid to your employee may not be taxable if they meet all conditions.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if there was business driving of the automobile.

  2. Determine if there was business driving of the automobile

    Business driving is any driving done by an employee between work locations for purposes related to their employment duties. This includes regular places of employment and a point of call.

    Personal driving is any driving by an employee of a vehicle for purposes not related to their employment.

    • If there is no business driving, the allowance or reimbursement paid to your employee is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance or the reimbursement to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If there is business driving, the allowance or reimbursement paid to your employee may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Get the required records from your employee to confirm the total number of kilometres travelled for business driving.

  3. Get the required records from your employee to confirm the total number of kilometres travelled for business driving

    If you provide an allowance or a reimbursement to your employee to compensate them for expenses paid for the use of their own automobile or motor vehicle in the course of their employment duties, your employee should keep a logbook or daily record of the total number of kilometres travelled for business driving made with their own vehicle.

    The logbook or daily record will justify that the allowance or the reimbursement that you paid was based solely on the number of kilometres for business driving. You must get the records from your employee.

    Logbook - Required records

    Your employee should record the following information in their records:

    • A log of all kilometres driven all year as part of their employment duties
    • A log of each work trip taken that includes the date, destination and purpose of the trip

    • If no record is kept, the allowance or reimbursement is taxable. You must use reasonable estimates to determine the amount of business and personal driving for the year based on the facts of the situation.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance or the reimbursement to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If your employee kept the required records and you received a copy, the allowance or reimbursement may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the allowance or the reimbursement.

  4. Determine if any exceptions apply to the allowance or the reimbursement

    The allowance or the reimbursement paid to your employee for the use of their vehicle may not be taxable in one of the following special situations:

    • Your employee works in remote locations
    • Your employee works at special work sites, including prescribed zones
    • If the use of the vehicle is in one of the above special situations, do not continue to next step.

    • If the use of the vehicle is not in one of the above special situations, the allowance or the reimbursement may be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if the allowance or the reimbursement is taxable.

  5. Determine if the allowance or the reimbursement is taxable

    Depending on your situation, the amounts you pay to your employees for the use of a vehicle they own and use in the course of their employment duties may not be taxable under the Income Tax Act or the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations

    Situation: You provide an allowance to your employee

    If you paid an allowance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the allowance is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The allowance is solely for the use of your employee's vehicle for the business kilometres driven  in the course of their employment duties (step 2)

    • The per-kilometre rate is reasonable 

      What is a reasonable per-kilometre rate

      Under the CRA's administrative policy, an allowance based on the per-kilometre rates prescribed in section 7306 of the Income Tax Regulations (maximum amount deductible as a business expense) is considered reasonable.

      Factors to consider to determine if using a lower or a higher per-kilometre rate is considered reasonable

      Depending on your situation and the factors, you may determine that a per-kilometre rate that is lower or higher than the prescribed rate is considered reasonable. For example, factors such as the type of vehicle, the driving conditions, or a higher fuel cost at a particular location, may be situations where you determine that the reasonable rates are higher or lower.

      To make sure you are using a rate that the CRA would consider reasonable in this situation, you can ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA.

      Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

      Reasonable per-kilometre rate

      Examples - Per-kilometre rates not considered reasonable
      Example of per-kilometre rates not considered reasonable
      Examples Result

      You are a controller at a retirement home. Currently, employees receive a rate of $0.35 per kilometre for automobile and motor vehicle allowances when they have to travel for work to help the residents with their daily tasks. This rate was negotiated in the last collective agreement and the collective agreement is signed for four years. You understand that the $0.35 rate is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

      The allowances are taxable to your employee because:

      • The per-kilometre rate is not considered reasonable because it is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate
      • Collective agreements do not supersede the Income Tax Act
      • If the allowance is considered too low than the reasonable per-kilometre rate, you may need to fill out and sign Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment (step 8 provides more information about the requirement to fill out Form T2200)
    • You did not reimburse your employee for expenses related to the same use of the vehicle (does not apply to situations where you reimburse your employee for supplementary business insurance, or tolls and ferry charges, if you determined the allowance without including these reimbursements)
    Situations and examples - Taxable

    Situations

    For example, allowances are taxable in all of the following situations:

    • Allowances paid at per-kilometre rates that are not considered reasonable because they are either too high or too low
    • Flat-rate allowances paid to employees where the amount paid is not related to the number of kilometres driven
    • Allowances that combine flat-rate and reasonable per-kilometre allowances for the same use of the vehicle
    • Allowances received where your employee is also reimbursed for the same use of the vehicle (except where the reimbursement is in respect of supplementary business insurance or toll or ferry charges and the amount of the allowance was determined without reference to the reimbursed expenses for supplementary business insurance or toll or ferry charges)
    Examples of allowances that are taxable
    Examples Result

    You own an office supply company where you employ workers on the road as salespersons. Each salesperson has a specific territory to cover and they use their own vehicle. They are paid a monthly flat rate allowance of $600 no matter where they go or how far they drive. This was agreed on when they were hired and is cited in their respective employment contracts.

    The allowance is taxable to your employee because:

    • The flat rate allowance is not based on the number of business kilometres driven.

    You pay an allowance to your employee as a flat per-diem rate to offset your employee's fixed expenses for each day the vehicle is required. You also pay a reasonable per-kilometre rate for each kilometre driven to offset the operating expenses.

    The total combined allowance is taxable to your employee because:

    • The flat per-diem rate compensates your employee for some of the same use on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based. That is, the fixed expenses incurred by your employee to operate the vehicle.
    • The combined amount is considered one allowance and it is not based only on the number of kilometres the vehicle is used for employment purposes.

    You pay an allowance to your employee as a flat-rate per month for travel inside the employment district. You also pay a reasonable per-kilometre rate for employment-related travel outside the employment district.

    The allowances are considered separetely and are taxable to your employee because:

    • The flat-rate allowance does not cover any of the same use of the vehicle on which the reasonable per-kilometer is based.
    • The reasonable per-kilometre allowance paid for travel outside the district is not taxable.
    • The amount based on a flat-rate paid for travel inside the district is taxable, since it is not based solely on the number of kilometres for which the vehicle is used in connection with the employment.

    • If the amount you paid to your employee for the use of their own vehicle does not meet all conditions above, the allowance is taxable.

    • If the amount you paid to your employee for the use of their own vehicle meets all conditions above, the allowance is not be taxable.

    Situation: You reimburse your employee

    If you reimburse your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the reimbursement is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The reimbursement is only for the use of your employee's vehicle for the business kilometres driven  in the course of their employment duties (step 2)
    • You received a copy of the required records  to justify your position regarding the amount you reimburse being reasonable (step 3)

    • If the amount you reimbursed to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle does not meet all conditions above, the reimbursement is taxable.

    • If the amount you reimbursed to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle meets all conditions above, the reimbursement is not be taxable.

    Situation: You provide an accountable advance to your employee

    If you provide an accountable advance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the accountable advance is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The accountable advance is only for the use of your employee's vehicle for the business kilometres driven  in the course of their employment duties (step 2)
    • You received a copy of the required records  to justify your position regarding the amount of the accountable advance being reasonable (step 3)
    Example - Not taxable
    Example of an accountable advance that is not taxable
    Example Result

    You own a sporting goods store. There is an out of town sales conference your business is participating in and you have scheduled one salesperson to attend. Two weeks prior to travel, you pay an accountable advance to your salesperson to cover expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicle to travel to and from the conference. This amount is based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate. The salesperson account for their expenses.

    In this example, the benefit is not taxable to your employee because:

    • The rate per kilometre is considered reasonable
    • Your employee accounted for their expenses by producing vouchers and returned any amount they did not spend

    • If the accountable advance you paid to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle does not meet all conditions above, the accountable advance is taxable.

      An accountable advance paid to your employee for personal driving (for example: expense incurred for travelling between a home and a regular place of employment) is considered employment income.

    • If the accountable advance you paid to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle meets all conditions above, the accountable advance is not be taxable.

    Situation: You are averaging an allowance or an advance to your employee

    To be updated - currently not accurate

    If you provide an accountable advance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the benefit is taxable if one of the following apply:

    • You are using a flat-rate allowance or lump-sum payment and average it at the end of the year to reach the reasonable per-kilometre rate 
    • The benefit does not meet all conditions below (non-taxable situation)

    If you use the reasonable per-kilometre rate to calculate the accountable advance, including situations where you are averaging the accountable advance, continue to: Non-taxable situation.

    Any accountable advance paid to your employee for their personal expenses is considered employment income.

    Non-taxable situation

    If you provide an accountable advance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The advance is only for the use of your employee's vehicle for business driving , (in the course of their employment duties) (step 1)
    • You received the required records (vouchers or expense claims for their kilometres travelled) to confirm the amounts paid as an accountable advance (step 2)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount of the accountable advance being reasonable (step 3)
    • You can justify your position regarding a pre-established per-kilometre rate that is not more than a reasonable amount (step 5)
    • You document in your employee’s record the method for establishing and keeping track of the accountable advance
    • No other provision of the Income Tax Act requires you to include the advances in your employee’s income
    • If your employee’s expenses were under the amount of the advance, your employee was required to return the difference by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • If your employee’s expenses were over the advance amount, you topped up the advance by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • If the allowance or reimbursement is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.
    • If the allowance or reimbursement is taxable, continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance or the reimbursement to be included on the T4 slip

  6. Calculate the value of the allowance or the reimbursement to be included on the T4 slip

    If the allowance, advance or reimbursement paid to your employee for the use of their own vehicle is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to the value of the benefit paid to your employee in the year. The amount must be included on the T4 slip of your employee.

  7. Determine if you need to fill out Form T2200 if the benefit is taxable

    You may need to fill out Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment, if your employee is eligible to deduct part of the cost from their income tax and benefit return.

    If you include an allowance as income for your employee and the allowance is considered too low, you must fill out and sign Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment and your employee can deduct on their income tax and benefit return:

    • An amount up to the amount of the allowance (if your employee did not keep a record or does not wish to claim employment expenses)
    • Expenses paid in the year (if a record was kept)
    • No expense if the allowance is not added to their income

    By signing the form, you are only certifying that your employee met the conditions of employment and had to pay for the expenses under their employment contract.

    It is your employee's responsibility to claim the expenses on their income tax and benefit return and to keep records to support the claim.

    Example

    You allow your employees to use their personal motor vehicle for business and pay them a monthly motor vehicle allowance to pay for the operating expenses and you include the allowance in your employees' employment income as a taxable benefit.
