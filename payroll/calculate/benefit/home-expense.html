Home office expenses - Provided to an employee working remotely

Content has been updated to include the new CRA administrative policy. This change is effective January 1, 2024.

You may provide an allowance , a reimbursement  or an advance  to your employee to compensate for use of their home or for home office expenses in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if any exceptions apply to the the allowance, reimbursement or advance

    The benefit may not be taxable if the benefit is in one of the following situations:

    • Your employee works at a special work site, including in a prescribed zone
    • Your employee works in remote locations
    • If the benefit is in one of the above situation, do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is not in one of the above situations, continue to Step 2 - Determine if your employee is required to work from home in the course of their employment duties.

  2. Determine if your employee is required to work from home in the course of their employment duties

    You need to determine if your employee was required to work from home in the course of their employment duties.

    • If you did not require your employee to work from home in the course of their employment duties, the allowance, reimbursement or advance paid to your employee is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance, reimbursement or advance to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If you required your employee to use their home in the course of their employment duties, the allowance, reimbursement or advance paid to your employee may not be taxable if they meet all conditions.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Determine if the worker is working more than 20% remotely.

  3. Determine if the worker is working more than 20% remotely

    The worker is working more than 20% remotely when all of the following apply:

    • Your employee had to be away from their principal place of residence because of their employment duties
    • Your employee had to work remotetly from home

    • If the benefit provided is for a period of less than 20%, the benefit is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance, reimbursement or advance to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If the benefit provided is for a period of more than 20%, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if the home office expense is within the limit.

  4. Determine if the home office expense is within the limit

  5. Get the required records from your employee to confirm the amount paid for home office expenses

    If you provide an allowance, a reimbursement or an advance to your employee to compensate them for expenses paid for the use of their home in the course of their employment duties, your employee should keep records of the total home office expenses.

    The records, such as expense claims or a log, will justify that the allowance or the reimbursement that you paid was based solely on the business use of the home. You must get the records from your employee.

    Required records

    Your employee should record the following information in their records:

    • A log of all days they worked at home as part of their employment duties
    • A log of each days worked at home that includes the date and the location

    • If no record is kept, the allowance, reimbursement or advance is taxable. You must use reasonable estimates to determine the amount of business and personal use of the home based on the facts of the situation.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance, reimbursement or advance to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If your employee kept the required records and you received a copy, the allowance, reimbursement or advance may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the allowance, reimbursement or advance.

  6. Determine if the allowance, the reimbursement or the advance is taxable

    Depending on your situation, the amounts you pay to your employees for the use of their home in the course of their employment duties may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations

    Situation: You provide an allowance to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you provide an allowance to your employee to compensate for home office expenses, the allowance is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The allowance is based only on business use of the home office expense in the course of their employment duties (step 2)

    • The amount is reasonable 

    • The total home office expense is $500 or less (including taxes)
    What is a reasonable amount

    • Using the amount under the CRA administrative policy

      Under the CRA administrative policy, the CRA considers an allowance based on the following amounts to be reasonable:

    • Using higher or lower amount

      Generally, if you use an amount to cover your employee’s home office expenses for using their own home as part of their employment duties, it is considered reasonable.

      If you use an amount that is lower or higher than the amount, it may not be considered reasonable.

      All of the facts relevant to your employee’s situation must be considered to determine if a lower or higher amount is considered reasonable. For example, factors such as the type of building, or a higher hydro cost at a specific location, may be situations where you determine that the reasonable amounts are higher or lower.

      If you think that your employee’s situation justifies using an amount that is lower or higher than the CRA administrative policy but you are not sure if the CRA will accept it, you can ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA.

      However, if you pay your employee an allowance based on an amount that is not considered reasonable in the situation, the allowance is taxable.

      Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

    Example - Amount not considered reasonable
    Example of amounts not considered reasonable
    Examples Result

    You are a controller at a retirement home.

    Currently, employees receive a rate of $0.35 per kilometre for automobile and motor vehicle allowances when they have to travel for work to help the residents with their daily tasks.

    This rate was negotiated in the last collective agreement and the collective agreement is signed for four years.

    You understand that the $0.35 rate is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

    The allowances are taxable to your employee because:

    • The per-kilometre rate is not considered reasonable because it is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate
    • Collective agreements do not supersede the Income Tax Act
    • If the allowance does not cover all of the employee’s out-of-pocket expenses, they may be eligible to claim their automobile and motor vehicle expenses on their personal income tax return.

      You would need to fill out and sign Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment (step 7 provides more information)

    Taxable situation

    If you provide an allowance to your employee to compensate for using their home, the allowance is taxable if all of the above are not met. For example:

    • The amounts are not considered reasonable because they are either too high or too low
    • You provided a flat-rate allowance that is not based on the invoices
    • You combined allowances for which you used a flat-rate and an amount for the same expense (the combined amount is considered 1 allowance)
    Examples - Taxable
    Examples of allowances that are taxable
    Examples Result

    You own an office supply company where you employ workers on the road as salespersons.

    Each salesperson has a specific territory to cover and they use their own vehicle.

    They are paid a monthly flat rate allowance of $600 no matter where they go or how far they drive.

    This was agreed on when they were hired and is cited in their respective employment contracts.

    The allowance is taxable to your employee because:

    • The flat rate allowance is not based on the number of business kilometres driven.

    You pay an allowance to your employee as a flat per-diem rate to offset your employee’s fixed expenses for each day the vehicle is required.

    You also pay a reasonable per-kilometre rate for each kilometre driven to offset the operating expenses.

    The total combined allowance is taxable to your employee because:

    • The flat per-diem rate compensates your employee for some of the same use on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based. That is, the fixed expenses incurred by your employee to operate the vehicle.
    • The combined amount is considered one allowance and it is not based only on the number of kilometres the vehicle is used for employment purposes.

    You pay an allowance to your employee as a flat-rate per month for travel inside the employment district.

    You also pay a reasonable per-kilometre rate for employment-related travel outside the employment district.

    The allowances are considered separately and are taxable to your employee because:

    • The flat-rate allowance does not cover any of the same use of the vehicle on which the reasonable per-kilometer is based.
    • The reasonable per-kilometre allowance paid for travel outside the district is not taxable.
    • The amount based on a flat-rate paid for travel inside the district is taxable, since it is not based solely on the number of kilometres for which the vehicle is used in connection with the employment.
    Situation: You reimburse your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you reimburse your employee to compensate for amounts they spent for specific expenses for using their home, the reimbursement is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The reimbursement is for specific expenses they spent for using their home in the course of their employment duties
    • The amounts reimbursed are reasonable and you can justify your position
    • You received from your employee records of the home office expense (for example: receipts, vouchers, log books) to support these expenses

    Taxable situation

    If the amount you reimbursed to your employee to compensate for using their home does not meet all conditions above, the reimbursement is taxable. Generally, it is only taxable if you reimburse your employee for personal expenses.

    Situation: You provide an accountable advance to your employee

    An advance is an amount you give to employees for expenses they will incur on your business. An accountable advance is one that you give to an employee who has to account for their expenses by producing vouchers and returning any amount they did not spend.

    Non-taxable situation

    If you provide an accountable advance to cover your employee’s expenses they will incur for using their home as part of their employment duties, the advance is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The amount is reasonable and you can justify your position
    • You received from your employee records of the home office expenses (for example: receipts, vouchers, log books) to support these expenses
    • Your received from your employee any amount that they did not spend from the advance

    Generally, an accountable advance is not taxable unless it is a payment of the personal expenses of your employee.

    Example - Not taxable
    Example of an accountable advance that is not taxable
    Example Result

    You own a sporting goods store.

    There is an out of town sales conference your business is participating in and you have scheduled one salesperson to attend.

    Two weeks prior to travel, you pay an accountable advance to your salesperson to cover expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicle to travel to and from the conference.

    This amount is based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

    The salesperson account for their expenses.

    In this example, the benefit is not taxable to your employee because:

    • The rate per kilometre is considered reasonable
    • Your employee accounted for their expenses by producing vouchers and returned any amount they did not spend

    Taxable situation

    If you provide an accountable advance to cover your employee’s expenses they will incur for using their home as part of their employment duties which does not meet all conditions above, the advance is taxable.

    Situation: You are averaging allowances or advances to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    If you provide allowances or advances to your employee for home office expenses and you average them, the allowances or advances are not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • You have a pre-established an amounts starting at the beginning of the year
    • The amount of the allowances or advances and the pre-established amounts are reasonable under the circumstances and you can justify your position
    • You record in your employee’s file the details of how the averaging was done
    • You received from your employee records of the home office expenses (for example: receipts, vouchers, log books) to support these expenses by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • No other provision of the Income Tax Act requires you to include the advances in your employee’s income

    How to calculate

    If you are averaging the allowances or advances, your employee must record the business kilometres they travelled and any allowances or advances they received on the date their employment ends in the year or by December 31, whichever is earlier. At that time, you need to review the total amount of allowances or advances you paid using the following calculation:

    • Kilometres driven for business driving in the year
    • multiply by Pre-established per kilometre rate
    • equals Result A
    • minus Total of allowances or advances you paid to your employee in the year
    • equals Result B

    • If the result B is positive, you must pay your employee the difference at that time.

    • If the the result B is negative, your employee must repay you the difference at that time.

    Taxable situation

    If you provide your employee with a flat-rate or lump sum allowance that is not based on the number of kilometres driven, you cannot average the allowances or advances at the end of the year to determine a reasonable per-kilometre rate. The allowances or advances is taxable and must be included in the employee’s income.

    • If the allowance, reimbursement or accountable advance is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations.

      Do not continue to next step.

    • If the allowance, reimbursement or advance is taxable, continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the allowance, reimbursement or advance to be included on the T4 slip

  7. Calculate the value of the allowance, reimbursement or advance to be included on the T4 slip

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is:

    Board, lodging or housing expenses

    What is the fair market value (FMV)

    The FMV is the highest price that can be obtained on an open market for a property. This is where the buyer and seller consent to the transaction, are well-informed and are dealing at arm's length.

    The FMV of property or a service does not include GST/HST.

    • Fair market value (FMV) of the board, lodging and housing expense per month
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of suitability of size (step 6a)
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of loss of privacy and quiet enjoyment (step 6b)
    • equals Value of the board and lodging benefit to be included in code 30 and box 14

    Utilities expenses

    • Cost of utilities per month
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for utilities expenses to be included on the T4 slip in code 40 and box 14

    Travel expenses

    • Value of the travel expense
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for travel expenses to be included on the T4 slip in code 40 and box 14

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.
