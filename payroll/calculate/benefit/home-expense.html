Home office expenses - Provided to an employee working remotely

You may be looking for:

  • Self-employed - Business-use-of-home expenses

Content has been updated to include the new CRA administrative policy. This change is effective January 1, 2024.

You may provide an allowance , a reimbursement  or an advance  to your employee to compensate for use of their home or for home office expenses in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

Determine if a benefit is taxable

Generally, if you provide a benefit to your employee to compensate for use of their home or for home office expenses in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties, the benefit is taxable. Depending on the situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the new CRA's administrative policy .

Situation: You provide your employee with an allowance for home office expenses

Non-taxable situation

Under the CRA's new administrativey policy, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

  • You required your employee to use their home in the course of their employment duties
  • Your employee was working more than 50% remotely from home
  • The amount paid is to compensate for home office expenses in the course of their employment duties

  • The following total amount of the allowance is equal or less than:

    Allowable amounts
    Average days per week working at home Allowable percentage Amount of allowance
    5 days per week 100% $500
    More than 4, but less than 5 90% $450
    4 days per week 80% $400
    More than 3, but less than 4 70% $350
    3 days per week 60% $300
  • You did not reimburse your employee for all of their home office expenses
  • You certify the percentage of time working from a home office using box 11 of the T4 slip

Taxable situation

If the benefit does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

Situation: You reimburse your employee for their home office expenses

Non-taxable situation

Under the CRA's new administrativey policy, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

  • You required your employee to use their home in the course of their employment duties
  • Your employee was working more than 50% remotely from home
  • The amount paid is to compensate for home office expenses in the course of their employment duties
  • The amount is considered reasonable  and you can justify your position
  • You receive the required records  from your employee of the home office expense (for example: receipts, vouchers, log books) to support these expenses
  • You provide your employee with a completed and signed Form T2200 Declaration of Conditions of Employment
    • What is a reasonable amount

    • Using the allowable amount under the CRA administrative policy

    • Using higher or lower amount

      Generally, if you use an amount to cover your employee’s home office expenses for using their own home as part of their employment duties, it is considered reasonable.

      If you use an amount that is lower or higher than the amount, it may not be considered reasonable.

      All of the facts relevant to your employee’s situation must be considered to determine if a lower or higher amount is considered reasonable. For example, factors such as the type of building, or a higher hydro cost at a specific location, may be situations where you determine that the reasonable amounts are higher or lower.

      If you think that your employee’s situation justifies using an amount that is lower or higher than the CRA administrative policy but you are not sure if the CRA will accept it, you can ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA.

      However, if you pay your employee an allowance based on an amount that is not considered reasonable in the situation, the allowance is taxable.

      Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

    What are the required records

    If you provide a reimbursement or an accountable advance to your employee to compensate them for expenses paid for the use of their home in the course of their employment duties, your employee should keep records of the total home office expenses.

    The records, such as expense claims or a log, will justify that the allowance or the reimbursement that you paid was based solely on the business use of the home. You must get the records from your employee.

    Your employee should record the following information in their records:

    • A log of all days they worked at home as part of their employment duties
    • A log of each days worked at home that includes the date and the location

    If no record is kept, the benefit is taxable.

    Taxable situation

    If the benefit does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is:

Determine if you need to fill out Form T2200 if the benefit is taxable

You may need to fill out Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment, if your employee is eligible to deduct employment expenses (other than home office expenses) from their income on their income tax and benefit return.

It is your employee’s responsibility to claim the expenses on their income tax and benefit return and to keep records to support the claim.

Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

Report the benefit on a slip

You must report the following amounts on the T4 slip:

  • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

    Report on:

    • New Box 11 - Percentage of time working from a home office
    • Box 14 - Employment Income
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • New Code 28 - Home office expenses benefits
    • Code 40 - Other Information

  • Cash: Option 2

    Report on:

    • New Box 11 - Percentage of time working from a home office
    • Box 14 - Employment Income
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
    • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
    • New Code 28 - Home office expenses benefits
    • Code 40 - Other Information

You cannot claim the value of the benefit as an expense.
