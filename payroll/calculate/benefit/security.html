Security (stock) options

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may grant options  to your employees as a form of compensation to encourage their greater involvement in the corporation. Generally, there are no tax consequences to your employee at the time the option is granted. There is no obligation for your employee to excercise  the stock option.

Steps

  1. Determine if a stock option agreement was made

    Generally, where you grant options to your employee, a written stock option agreement exist between you (as a qualifying person (a corporation or a mutual trust fund)) and your employee.

    View example of a written stock option agreement 

    If there is no written stock agreement, you may find the terms for issuing securities, stock and trust units in the financial records of a corporation (for example: a directors' resolution in the corporate minutes or notes to financial statements) to determine the employment status of the person receiving the benefit.

    • If no stock option agreement exist or there is no intention in the agreement for the shares to be issued (for example: Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan ), the benefit is taxable on the date that the cash was received under under section 5(1) or 6(1)(a).

      Depending on your situation, continue to:

      • Stock bonus or cash bonus paid in shares: Your employee transfers or disposes of rights, including security option cash outs.
      • All other situations: Step 5 - Calculate the value of the benefit.
    • If a stock option agreement exist, continue to: Step 2 - Determine if the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employment.

  2. Determine if the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employment

    To determine if the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employement, you need to determine if there is an agreement between:

    • You as a qualifying person (corporation or a mutual trust fund), including if you are in one of the following situation:

      You are a non-resident corporation

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person who is a non-resident corporation and your employee is working in Canada (or employed in Canada by a corporation with which the non-resident corporation does not deal at arm’s length), the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7.

      Example

      On March 7, an employee is granted 100 options to purchase shares of their employer's parent corporation at the FMV of $10 US ($1 US = $1.35 CDN).

      On September 2 the employee informs their employer that they wish to exercise their 100 options and pays the employer $1000 US ($1 US = $1.25 CDN). The exercise price is $10 US and the FMV is $20 US on this date.

      On September 5 the treasury shares are transferred to the employee at a FMV of $19 US ($1 US = $1.20 CDN).

      You are using a trustee as an intermediary

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person who is using a truste as an intermediary and the person receiving the benefit is your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(2).

      Example
      Description

      Ashley is granted 10,000 options on March 4, 2014, when the FMV of each share is $20. Ashley must be an employee for at least three years after date of exercise before the shares are fully vested, or the employer may buy back the shares for $0.01 each. Ashley exercises her option on May 1, 2015, when the FMV of each share is $25. In 2015, Ashley's benefit is equal to $50,000 [10,000 x (25-20)], and this amount is reported on her T4.

      On June 1, 2016, Ashley leaves the company, and as a result, forfeits her securities, and receives $100 in consideration for the $50,000 of shares. There are no changes to Ashley's T4 for 2014, 2015, or 2016, but when Ashley files her tax return, she may, in the year in which she forfeited the securities, claim a deduction of $50,000 from her income.

    • The person receiving the benefit is in one of the following situation:

      Your employee

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and the person is your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7.

      Person ceased to be your employee

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and the person ceased to be your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(4).

      Example
      Description
      • A person had the option to purchase 10,000 shares of ABC Inc. when the employment began.
      • On November 30, 2020, the person employment ceased when they resigned from his position as Manager.
      • A clause in the stock option agreement allowed the person to purchase these shares within a period of 90 calendar days after the date of termination.
      • On January 20, 2021, the person chose to purchase 20,000 shares at the price of $.50 cents per share.
      Result

      Although the person's employment ceased on November 30, 2020, any benefit from the purchase of shares in ABC Inc. on January 20, 2021 is considered taxable under subsection 7(1).

      Explanation

      If a person was your employee at the time of receiving a right to acquire shares, but subsequently ceases to be your employee (for example, change jobs or retire) before the exercise or transfer of that right, the person is treated as your employee until the transaction is completed. The benefit is included in their employment income and the person is deemed to have received the benefit as if they are still employed.

      Your employee is also a shareholder

      Not by virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and your employee is also a shareholder, the benefit is not considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(5) if all of the following apply:

      • Benefit is a distribution of profits
      • Similar benefits are not available to employees who were not shareholders
      • Duties of other employees not equally important
      Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment
      Description
      • A person is president of ABC Inc.
      • They own 51% of that corporation's common voting shares.
      • The person's spouse owns 10%.
      • The person and his spouse are the only people with a stock option agreement from ABC Inc.
      • The plan was not offered to any other employee.
      Result

      The person is in a position of control. The person and the spouse receive the benefit because of his capacity as a shareholder.

      This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

      Factors to consider to determine if the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee

      If your employee is also a shareholder, it is a question of fact whether the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee.

      You need to determine if the benefit resulted from the individual's role as an employee or their role as a shareholder. In addition to control, other factors must be considered.

      Factors to be considered based on court cases includes:

      • Was the benefit given to the taxpayer pursuant to a worker agreement with the corporation?
      • Was the benefit given pursuant to a formal agreement with the corporation?
      • Was the benefit given to reimburse costs of fees incurred by the taxpayer?
      • Was the same benefit offered to other workers, officers or directors?
      • Was the stock options or discretionary dividend shares?
      • Did clients of the corporation receive the same benefit?
      • Did the taxpayer owned 50% or more of the corporation's shares?
      • Did the corporation receive fair market value for the benefit from the taxpayer?
      • Does the benefit relate to the performance of the taxpayer’s duties at the corporation?
      • Was the benefit given to entertain clients or potential clients?
      • Did the corporation give the benefit to pay off a debt owed to the taxpayer?

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

      Self-employed person

      Not by virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and a self-employed person, this is not considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(5).

      Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment
      Description
      • A person is a self-employed consultant.
      • The person is contracted on a short-term basis by a junior public mining corporation to raise capital.
      • Part of the contract, the person was offered participation in the corporation's stock option benefit plan with details outlined in a stock option agreement.
      Result

      The person was not an employee of the junior public mining corporation.

      This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock option agreement does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

    • If the stock option agreement is not considered received by virtue of employment, this is not taxable as an employee benefit.

      Do not continue to next step.

      These amounts are not reported on a T4 slip. However, the shareholder benefit must be included on the person's T1 return and reported as business income.

    • If the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employment, the benefit is generally taxable under under section 7.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Determine when the benefit is considered taxable.

  3. Determine when the benefit is considered taxable

    The benefit is taxable when one of the following applies:

    • Your employee acquires, purchases or receives the securities at less than the fair marker value (FMV) 
    • Your employee sells, transfers, or disposes of options rights for a financial gain

    Event that caused a benefit

    Employee chooses to exercises their rights and acquires the shares [7(1)(a)]

    This applies when your employee chooses to excercise  their rights under the agreement and acquire the securities. An acquisition is considered to take place when:

    • Title to the shares passes to your employee
    • The employer has legal right to payment for the shares

    Generally, your employee notifies the employer that they wishes to exercise their options to acquire shares.

    Security option is exercised for preferred shares and a share is acquired, including shares convertible to common shares

    Where an employee acquires convertible preferred shares, they are taxable as employee benefits because the benefits are considered to be received by virtue of employment under 7(1)(a) of the ITA.

    Explanation

    Convertible preferred shares are preferred shares of a corporation that are convertible to common shares of the corporation. Convertible preferred shares include forward participating shares. Forward participating shares are generally those shares of a separate class of a corporation that are purchased by the individual for fair market value consideration. The forward participating shares derive most of their value from their convertibility into common shares of the corporation.

    Pursuant to an agreement between an employee and the employer corporation, or a corporation with which it does not deal at arm's length, the initial acquisition of preferred shares to which a conversion feature is attached is no different than the acquisition of any other shares of the capital stock of the corporation.

    Paragraph 7(1)(a) deems a benefit equal to the difference between the value of the shares at the date of acquisition and the amount paid or to be paid for the shares to have been received by your employee in the taxation year in which the shares are acquired. In the case of a CCPC, the benefit is deferred until your employee disposes of the acquired share.

    The value of the shares at the date of acquisition may not be readily determinable because of the necessity to assign a value to the conversion feature, or to allow a discount for any restrictions that the conversion feature may impose.

    Security option is exercised, a share is acquired and sold on before the share is received by your employee (short selling)

    Short selling transactions are taxable as employee benefits because the benefits are considered to be received by virtue of employment under 7(1)(a) of the ITA.

    Explanation

    The concept of short selling can be best illustrated with the following example. An employee exercises a stock option and at the same time instructs their broker to sell the shares on the market immediately. The actual transactions are as follows:

    • Your employee notifies the corporation in writing June 30, 2018, that they wish to exercise the stock options.
    • On the same notification that your employee sends to the corporation, your employee authorizes their broker to sell the shares obtained upon the exercise of the options and to pay the corporation the exercise price for the shares.
    • The broker immediately sells shares of the corporation on the open market (June 30). This, however, is a so-called "short sale" because the corporation has not, and will not, actually issue shares from treasury to the broker (the agent of your employee) until it is paid the exercise price by the broker.
    • The broker sells the shares on the trade date (June 30), obtains cash, and then, generally, a few days later, pays the corporation the exercise price on your employee's behalf. On July 3, 2018, your employee receives the treasury shares from the corporation (this covers the broker's position in respect of the shares that it sold "short" a few days earlier).

    July 3rd is the date the shares are "acquired" for purposes of calculating the taxable benefit.

    Situation: Employee acquires shares from a non-CCPC employer

    Taxable situation

    If your employee acquires shares under a security options agreement entered into by a non-CCPC, the benefit is taxable.

    The benefit is taxable in the year in which your employee acquires the sharesunder 7(1)(a).

    Situation: Employee acquires shares from a CCPC, including employers who was a CCPC at the time the security option agreement was entered into and subsequently ceased to be one

    Taxable situation

    If your employee acquires shares under a security options agreement entered into by a CCPC, the benefit is taxable.

    The benefit is taxable in the year in which your employee disposes of the shares under 7(1.1).

    Employee transfers or disposes of rights to a person with whom your employee was dealing at arm's length or to the particular qualifying person with whom your employee was not dealing at arm's length , including security option cash outs [7(1)(b) or 7(1)(b.1)]

    This applies in one of the following situations:

    • There is a significant change in the terms of the security option agreement that resulted in a disposition of the option
    • Your employee has disposed of security option rights to a third party as part of a transaction that results in an ownership change in the corporation
    • Your employee received cash, or other in-kind benefit, from their employer instead of shares on exercise of an option. This is often referred to as "cash out".

    This also applies when your employee is deemed to have received a benefit when they dispose of rights under a security option agreement to an employer with which your employee does not deal at arm's length (or to a person not dealing at arm's length with such an employer), including cash outs.

    Situation: Employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement and the employer elects to cash out the rights

    Taxable situation

    The benefit is taxable if:

    • Employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement
    • Employee has the option of receiving shares or cash
    • Employer elects to cash out the rights
    • Employee chooses to receive shares in lieu of a cash bonus already declared by the employer

    The benefit is taxable on the date the security option was cashed out under 5(1) or 6(1)(a). This is because your employee received cash, or other in-kind benefit, from their employer instead of shares on exercise of an option. The security option cash out cannot be deferred.

    Examples
    • An employer offers an annual bonus when key corporate objectives have been met. Although your employee doesn't know whether the objectives will be met this year, your employee advised their employer that, if he is entitled to a bonus, he wants to take shares of the corporation instead of receiving a cash bonus. Section 7 will apply in this situation.
    • If your employee waits until the bonus is declared and then opts to take shares instead of the cash payment, the value of the shares your employee receives will be taxed as employment income under 5(1) or 6(1)(a). Section 7 would not apply in this situation.

    Your employee is not eligible to claim any deductions because it is not considered to have been received under section 7 (by virtue of employment).

    Situation: Employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement and the employee elects to cash out the rights

    Taxable situation

    The benefit is taxable if:

    • Employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement
    • Employee has the option of receiving shares or cash
    • Employee elects to cash out the rights
    • Employee chooses to receive shares rather than a cash bonus before the bonus is declared by the employer

    The benefit is taxable for the employee in the taxation year in which the security option is cashed out under 7(1)(b) or 7(1)(b.1).

    Situation: Employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement and the employee does not have the option to receive shares or cash

    Taxable situation

    The benefit is taxable if:

    • Employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement
    • Employee does not have the option to receiving shares or cash
    • Employee has disposed of security option rights to a third party as part of a transaction that results in an ownership change in the corporation

    The benefit is taxable for the employee in the taxation year in which the security option is disposed of under 7(1)(b) or 7(1)(b.1).

    Situation: Employee exchanges their rights under the security option agreement for new security options rights

    Non-taxable situation

    Where your employee exchanges the option for a new option, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Employee exchanges their security options for new security options as a result of any type of corporate reorganization or capital restructuring within a non-arm’s length corporate group
    • Employee receives no consideration for the exchange other than the new option
    • Employee does not derive an economic gain from the exchange

    The original option is deemed under 7(1.4) of the ITA to have not been disposed of and the new option is deemed to be the same as and a continuation of the old option. A taxable benefit will only arise when the new option has been exercised or otherwise disposed of by your employee.

    Taxable situation

    Where an employee exchanges the security option for a new option and the transaction does not meet all of the following conditions listed above, the original option is deemed to have been disposed under 7(1)(b).

    The benefit is taxable for the employee in the taxation year in which the security option is disposed of under 7(1)(b).

    Employee transfers rights to a non-arm's length person, and that person exercises the option to acquire the shares [7(1)(c)]

    Taxable situation

    If your employee transfers their rights under a security options agreement to a non-arm’s length person and that person exercises the option, the benefit is taxable.

    The benefit is taxable for the employee who initially received the option for the taxation year in which the security option is exercised under 7(1)(c).

    Employee transfers rights to a non-arm's length person, and that person disposes of the rights [7(1)(d) and 7(1)(d.1)]

    Taxable situation

    If the employee transfers their rights under a security options agreement to a non-arm’s length person (transferee), in whom the rights vest, who then disposes of the rights to a party at arm’s length from the transferee or a qualifying person not at arm’s length from the transferee (or a corporation or mutual fund trust not dealing at arm’s length with such a qualifying person), the benefit is taxable.

    The benefit is taxable for the employee who initially received the option for the taxation year in which the rights to the security option are disposed of by the transferee under 7(1)(d) or 7(1)(d.1).

    Employee dies holding unexercised options [7(1)(e)]

    Taxable situation is nil

    If the security option plan provides the options to expire on an employee's death, the value of options immediately after death is nil even if the benefit is taxable in the taxation year in which your employee dies.

    If the security option plan expire on an employee's death or the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations.

    Do not continue to next step.

    Taxable situation

    Where an employee owns unexercised options under a security option agreement immediately before death, the benefit is considered to have been received by your employee for the taxation year in which they died.

    The benefit is taxable in the taxation year in which your employee dies under 7(1)(e).

    Determine if your employee is eligible for the option benefit deduction – Paragraph 110(1)(d)

    Where the stock option does not expire, the securities are acquired within the first taxation year of the deceased's graduated rate estate (GRE) by either the GRE itself, a person who is a beneficiary under the GRE or a person in whom the deceased's rights under the security option agreement have vested  as a result of the death.

    Employee exchanges shares acquired from a CCPC under the security option agreement for new shares [7(1.5)]

    Non-taxable situation

    If your employee exchanges the shares of a CCPC for new shares, the benefit from the transaction is not taxable under 7(1.5) if all of the following apply:

    • Employee exchanges their shares for new shares of the employer corporation, a corporation with which the employer corporation does not deal at arm's length immediately after the disposition or exchange of the old shares, the newly amalgamated or merged corporation, or a corporation with which the newly amalgamated or merged corporation does not deal at arm's length immediately after the amalgamation
    • Employee receives no consideration for the exchange other than the new option
    • Fair market value of the new shares immediately after the disposition or exchange is not more than the fair market value of the old shares immediately before the disposition or exchange

    Taxable situation

    If transaction does not meet all of the above, the benefit is taxable to your employee when the new option has been exercised or otherwise disposed of by your employee .

    The benefit is taxable to your employee in the taxation year in which the old shares are disposed of under 7(1.1).

    Employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement [7(1.7)]

    Non-taxable situation

    If your employee had rights to acquire shares under a security options agreement and the rights cease to be exercisable under the agreement, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement
    • Your employee did not receive consideration for the loss of the ability to exercise the option
    Example

    Examples of this situation includes merger where the previous corporation no longer exists for your employee to exercise the option and situations where the option is cancelled by the employer.

    If your employee has exchanged their rights for rights under a new stock plan, continue to: Employee transfers or disposes of rights to a person with whom your employee was dealing at arm’s length [7(1)(b)] or to the particular qualifying person with whom your employee was not dealing at arm’s length [7(1)(b.1)], including security option cash outs.

    Taxable situation

    If your employee receives consideration for this loss, or does not meet all of the above, the benefit is taxable to your employee in the taxation year in which the rights to the option are disposed of under 7(1.7).

    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations.

      Do not continue to next step.

    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to Step 3 - Determine if your employee is eligible for any option benefit deductions

Definitions

Definitions for security options
Annual vesting limit
The total of the amount of employee stock options that may vest in an employee in a year and be eligible for the stock options deduction is subject to an annual limit of $200,000. The limit is based on the fair market value of the securities at the time the option was granted and the year in which these options could first be exercised. The amount of the benefit can be eligible for the stock options deduction.
Exercise
The term is used to describe the acquisition of securities under the terms of the option agreement but does not include the acquisition of shares under a provision of the option that allows an employee to receive shares equal in value to an increase in the value of the option.
Fair market value
The fair market value of a security at any particular time is usually equal to the value quoted on a stock exchange, if it is publicly traded. However there may be circumstances where this may not be the case. Specifically the fair market value of a security is the price that can be obtainable in an open and unrestricted market between knowledgeable, informed and prudent parties acting at arm's length where neither party is under any compulsion to purchase or sell the security.
Grant an option
The provision by an employer, or a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer, to an individual, of a right to acquire securities of the employer or of a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer.
Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan
Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan is an arrangement where the employee is compensated based on how the shares of the employer corporation perform on the open market.
Qualified donee
A qualified donee is generally a person to whom gifts can be made that qualify for the charitable donation deduction or tax credit.
Qualifying person
A qualifying person is a corporation or a mutual fund trust.
Security
A security is a share of the capital stock of a corporation or a unit of a mutual fund trust that is qualifying person.
Specified value
In most cases, the specified value of a security is the fair market value (FMV) of the security at the time the option is granted. Where there have been exchanges of options (to which subsection 7(1.4) of the Act apply or the security has been subject to stock splits or consolidations, the specified value is the initial FMV adjusted to take into account such events.
Specified shareholder
In general, a specified shareholder is a person who directly or indirectly owns 10% or more of the issued shares of any class of the capital stock of the corporation or any related corporation.
Vested
The term vested refers to the time when the employee can first exercise an option.
