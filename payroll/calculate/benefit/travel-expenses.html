Travel expenses

You may be looking for:

  • Parking
  • Transportation and airline passes

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide an allowance  or a reimbursement  to your employee to compensate for the cost of travel expenses (other than automobile or motor vehicle expenses) in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if your employee is considered in a special situation

    The benefit may not be taxable if your employee is in one of the following special situations:

    • Your employee works in a remote location
    • Your employee works at a special work site, including in a prescribed zone
    • Your employee has a severe or prolonged impairment
    • If the travel expenses benefit is in one of the above special situation, do not continue to next step.

    • If the travel expenses benefit is not in one of the above special situations, the benefit provided for travel expenses may be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if the employee is required to travel in the course of their employment duties

  2. Determine if the employee is required to travel in the course of their employment duties

    You need to determine if the employee was required to travel in the course of their employment duties.

    • If you did not require your employee to travel in the course of their employment duties, the benefit from the allowance or the reimbursement paid to the employee is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    • If you required your employee to travel in the course of their employment duties, the benefit from the allowance or the reimbursement paid to the employee may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Determine if the allowance or reimbursement is reasonable

  3. Determine if the allowance or reimbursement is reasonable

    You must consider all of the facts relevant to your employee's situation to determine if the travel expense is considered reasonable.

    This determination must be done based on a review of the facts in each specific situation.

    Generally, the CRA considers the amount to be reasonable if:

    • Employee is required to travel in the course of their employment duties (step 2)
    • Your employee kept the required records, and you received a copy to support these amounts. For example, receipts for:
      • Airfare
      • Hotel
      • Meal
    • You can justify your position for the amount being reasonable
    • It is not a form of additional remuneration and is not a form of tax avoidance

    If you cannot get the required records, you must use reasonable estimates to determine the amount is based on facts.

    Depending on your situation, you may determine that a reasonable amount would be lower or higher.

    To confirm what the CRA would consider reasonable in your situation, you can ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA.

    Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

    • If the amount of the payment for travel expenses is not reasonable, the benefit is taxable.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    • If the amount of the payment for travel expenses is reasonable the benefit may not be taxable.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if the benefit is taxable

  4. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, if you provide an allowance  or a reimbursement  for travel expenses to your employee, the benefit is taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the Income Tax Act or the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations

    Situation: Your employee works part-time

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA), if you paid an allowance or a reimbursement to your part-time employee for reasonable travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee incurs the expenses from travelling to and from the part-time job
    • You and your employee are at arm's length
    • Your employee is in one of the following situation:
      • Employee is a teacher or professor who works part-time and all of the following apply:
        • Works in a designated educational institution in Canada
        • Provides service to you as a teacher or professor
        • The location of their employment is 80 kilometres or more from their home
      • Employee works part-time and all of the following apply:
        • Employee has other employment or carries on a business outside of their employment with you
        • The location of their employment is 80 kilometres or more from both their home and the place of the other employment or business
    • The amount of the allowance or the reimbursement is reasonable (step 3)

    A thorough review of the facts in each specific situation must be done to determine if the benefit is taxable.

    Learn more: Income Tax Folio S1-F5-C1, Related persons and dealing at arm's length

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or the reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    Situation: Your employee is a salesperson, an agent selling property or negotiating contracts or a member of the clergy

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA), if you paid an allowance or a reimbursement to your employee who is a salesperson, an agent selling property or negotiating contracts or a member of the clergy for travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The expenses are related to the performance of their employment duties (step 2)
    • The amount of the allowance or the reimbursement is reasonable (step 3)

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or the reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    Situation: Your employee is a member of the Canadian Forces and it is for separation allowances under service regulations

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA), if you paid a travel and separation allowance under service regulations to your employee who is a member of the Canadian Forces, the benefit is not taxable.

    Learn more from the Department of National Defence: Chapter 209 - Transportation and Travelling Expenses

    Situation: Your employee is travelling outside of your municipality or metropolitan area

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA), if you provide a reasonable travel allowance (other than an allowance for the use of a motor vehicle) to your employee for travelling outside of your municipality or metropolitan area, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The travelling is from the municipality or metropolitan area where your establishment is located and where your employee ordinarily reports
    • The expenses is related to the performance of their employment duties (step 2)
    • The amount of the allowance or the reimbursement is reasonable (step 3)

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or the reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    Situation: Your employee is travelling within your municipality or metropolitan area

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide a reasonable travel allowance (other than an allowance related to the use of a vehicle) to your employee for travelling within a municipality or metropolitan area, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The travelling is within a municipality or metropolitan area where your establishment is located and where your employee ordinarily reports
    • The travelling is to make sure your employee can perform their duties in a more efficient manner
    • The amount of the allowance is reasonable (step 3), for meal expenses this is generally up to $23 including taxes (higher amounts may be reasonable if the meal cost in a location is higher)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount being reasonable (step 3)
    • You are the primary beneficiary of the allowance
    • The travel allowance is not a form of additional remuneration and is not a form of tax avoidance

    This means that you do not have to include this type of travel allowance if its main reason is so that your employee's duties are performed in a more efficient way during a work shift.

    Examples
    Examples
    Examples Result

    Antoine installs and repairs power lines for a hydroelectric company. He works in an area of the municipality located over an hour from his office, where he generally has lunch. Instead of Antoine driving to the office during his normal meal break and returning to his workplace afterward, the employer pays him a reasonable meal allowance to allow him to eat at a restaurant closer to where he performs his duties. The employer is the primary beneficiary of this arrangement and the allowance would not be included in Antoine's salary.

    The benefit is not taxable.

    Jeannine works for DEF Corporation and must occasionally use her vehicle to travel into the municipality for supplies as part of her duties. This generally takes up the better part of her day. DEF gives Jeannine a daily allowance of $50 to cover vehicle expenses and all food and drink expenses that she may incur while performing her duties outside her normal workplace. Because this policy does not include a motor vehicle use allowance, we would consider unreasonable the $50 allowance that would cover her meals and her motor vehicle expenses.

    The allowance is a taxable benefit for Jeannine except if DEF Corporation can prove that the portion related to meals is reasonable under the circumstances.

    An employer gives its employees a travel allowance that covers food and drink when they have to go outside their normal workplace to perform duties. One of the employees regularly returns to the office before noon and stays there until having to go to an appointment that afternoon. It would be to the employee's advantage to return to the office to have his meal.

    The allowance is taxable because it takes the form of another type of compensation. However, if it happens that the employee is legitimately outside the office during his normal meal break, the allowance for those days may be excluded from his income.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the full amount is a taxable benefit.

    Situation: You pay travel expenses on a business trip for your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid travel expenses on a business trip to your employee for business-related travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The amount of the travel expenses paid is reasonable (step 2)
    • You require their presence on the business trip to accomplish the business objectives of the trip
    • Employee or their spouse (or common-law partner) spends more than 50% of the time on business-related activities

    The amount of the expense which is not taxable is the amount of the employee's travelling expenses necessary to accomplish the business objectives of the trip.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    For example, the travel is taxable in one of the following situation:

    • Free holiday or vacation you paid to your employee, the employee's family, or both
    • You pay a third party for a trip or vacation for your employee or their family
    • Vacation property that you own and allow your employee to use for other than your business purpose
    • Business trip extended by your employee (only the cost the employer paid for the extension of the trip is a taxable benefit)
    Situation: You reimburse travel expenses on a business trip for your employee’s spouse or common-law partner

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you reimburse your employee's spouse or common-law partner for travel expenses, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The amount of the travel expenses paid for the spouse is reasonable (step 2)
    • You requested the spouse or common-law partner to be present on the business trip
    • The spouse or common-law partner spent more than 50% of the time on business-related activities

    The amount of the expense which is not taxable is the amount of the employee's spouse or common-law partner travelling expenses necessary to accomplish the business objectives of the trip.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for travel expenses for your employee’s spouse or common-law partner does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable to the employee.

    Situation: Your employee acts as host of an incentive award trip

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid travel expenses to your employee acting as host of an incentive award trip, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The trip was arranged as an incentive award trip for your employees or your customers
    • Your employee acts as host for the incentive trip
    • Your employee works directly in business activities at least 90% of each day (a substantial part)
    • Your employee's travelling expenses are considered necessary to accomplish the business objectives of the trip.

    Taxable situation

    If the travel expenses paid for your employee acting as host of the incentive award trip does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    If the employee works directly in business activities for less than 90% of each day (not a substantial part), the CRA will accept a proration of the benefit if all other conditions are met.

    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to: Step 5 - Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

  5. Calculate the value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to:

    • Total value of the benefit received or enjoyed
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 1 - No calculation

    An employer paid an allowance of $4,000 to his employee for travel expenses. The employee is sent to Toronto in Ontario for 9 days to meet with key partners. Presence is required on all days with free time to tour Toronto at own expense. The airfare for the employee is $1,300. The hotel cost is $300 per night for 9 nights for a total of $2,700.

    The allowance paid by the employer is not a taxable benefit because the benefit is for travel expenses in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

    You do not need to do any calculations.

    Example 2 - Calculations

    An employer paid an allowance of $5,200 to his employee for travel expenses. Employee sent to Niagara Falls in Ontario for 9 days to meet with key partners. The employee requested to stay an additional four nights after the end of the meetings and was allowed to do so by the employer. The extra days resulted in no increased cost for the flights, but four additional nights’ accommodations. Presence is required on all days with free time to tour Toronto at own expense. The airfare for the employee is $1,300. The hotel cost is $300 per night for 13 nights for a total of $3,900.

    • $5,200 is the total value of the travel allowances
    • minus $4,000 ($2,700 for 9 nights + $1,300 airfare) is the amount of the travel allowance that is not taxable
    • equals $1,200 (4 nights) is the value of the taxable benefit
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $1,200 is the value of the benefit to be included on employee's T4 slip
    Example 3 - Calculations

    An employer paid an allowance of $200 monthly ($2,400 yearly) to his part-time employee for travel expenses. The employee is a teacher at a designated educational institution. The school is 60 kilometres from their home.

    • $2,400 is the total value of the travel allowances
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals 2,400 is the value of the benefit to be included on employee's T4 slip

  7. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

  8. Report the benefit on a slip
