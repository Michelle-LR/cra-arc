Determine if the benefit is taxable

Generally, if you provide a benefit , an allowance  or a reimbursement  for educational assistance to your employee, a former employee or an employee's family member, the benefit is taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the Income Tax Act or the CRA's administrative policy .

What is a scholarship, a bursary, a research grant or a fellowship

Scholarships and bursaries

Scholarships and bursaries are amounts awarded to students, or on their behalf, to enable them to pursue their education. They are not defined in the Income Tax Act, but the meaning of bursary is broad enough to include almost any form of financial assistance to enable a student to pursue their education. They can include amounts paid to defray living expenses, as well as amounts that are directly related to tuition fees. They usually apply to studies at the postsecondary level or beyond, intended to obtain a university degree, a diploma or a certificate. However, there are circumstances under which they are awarded for education below the postsecondary school level. Normally, a student is not expected to do specific work for the payer in exchange for a scholarship or bursary.

Research grants

Research Grants are sums of money provided to an individual for the payment of expenses necessary to conduct a research project. The grant may include personal or living expenses. The grant may also include an element of remuneration to the recipient. Funds originating from various sources may be paid to and administered by a college, university, hospital, or similar institution where the researcher is employed. However, only that part of the grant paid to, or otherwise available for, the researcher is considered to be a research grant.

Fellowship

The term fellowship is not defined in the Income Tax Act. Generally, a fellowship is an amount paid or benefits given for the purpose of advancing a person’s education. It is similar to a scholarship or a bursary, with the difference being that a fellowship is generally awarded to a graduate student by a university, charity, or similar body for doctoral studies or post-doctoral work.

Benefit provided to your current, former or future employee

Situation: You provide or reimburse for courses, programs, or training to your current or former employee

Non-taxable situation

Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide or reimburse for a course, a program, or training to your current or former employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

  • Your employee returns to work for you for a reasonable amount of time after the completion of the course, program or training, you must be able to justify your position regarding the length of the period of time being reasonable

  • The course, program or training is for one of the following employment-related purpose:

    • Specific 
    • Business or general 
  • The course, program or training is not for your employee's personal interest 

You must be able to clearly demonstrate you are the primary beneficiary of the expense.

If the benefit is not taxable and you paid for the expense, your employee cannot claim the non-refundable tuition tax credit, or the educational amount, on their T1 return.

What is specific employment-related, general employment-related or personal interest training

The CRA considers there are 3 categories of training:

  • Specific employment-related courses, programs or training

    Courses, programs or training taken to maintain or upgrade employment-related skills are mainly for the employer's benefit when it is reasonable to assume that the employee will resume their employment for a reasonable period of time after they finish the course.

    This includes tuition fees and other associated costs such as books, meals, travel, and accommodation that employers pay for courses leading to a degree, diploma, or certificate in a field related to the employee's current or future responsibilities in the employer's business.

  • Business or general employment-related courses, programs or training

    Courses, programs or training for general employment-related, even if not directly related to an employer's business, which are taken mainly for the employer's benefit.

    This includes fees the employer pays for stress management, employment equity, first aid, and language courses.

  • Personal interest courses, programs or training

    Courses, programs or training for personal interest or technical skills not related to the employer's business which are taken mainly for the employee's benefit.

Taxable situation

If the benefit does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

Situation: You provide or reimburse for courses, programs, or training to your future employee

Non-taxable situation

If you provide or reimburse for a course, a program, or training to your future employee, this is not taxable as an employee benefit because there is no employee/employer relationship between the payer and the future employee (student).

The individual must report the amounts received on their T1 return.

Benefit provided to one of your employee's family member

Situation: You provide free or reduced tuition fees to your employee’s family member

Non-taxable situation

If you provide free or reduced tuition fees to your employee’s family member, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

  • You and your employee deal at arm’s length
  • Benefit is not provided as a substitute for salary, wages or other remuneration

In this situation, there is no employee/employer relationship between the payer and the family member of the employee. The individual must report the amounts received on their T1 return.

Report on T4A slip

The payer must report the full amount of the educational assistance paid on a T4A slip. If they meet certain criteria, the individual may claim the non-refundable tuition tax credit or the educational amount, on their T1 return.

Situation: You pay or reimburse your employee for educational allowance for their children

Non-taxable situation

If you pay or reimburse your employee for educational allowance for their children, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

  • Your employee and their family has to live in a specific location away from their home
  • The schools in the area do not meet the educational needs of your employee’s children (for example: if your employee's child has a learning disability but none of the schools in the specific location have a special education program)
  • The education is provided in the official language of Canada primarily used by your employee
  • The school is the nearest suitable school available in that official language
  • The child is in full-time attendance at the school
  • The subsidy you provide is reasonable, you must be prepared to justify your position

In this situation, there is no employee/employer relationship between the payer and the family member of the employee. The individual must report the amounts received on their T1 return.

Report on T4A slip
