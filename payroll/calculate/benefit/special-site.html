Travel, board and lodging expenses - Provided to an employee in special work sites

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide a benefit for travel, board and loding expenses to your employee who works or lives in a location that is in a special work site.

Generally, a special work site is an area where temporary duties are performed by an employee who keeps a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE) at another location as their principal place of residence.

Because of the distance between the two areas, the employee is not expected to return daily from the work site to his or her principal place of residence.

Steps

  1. Determine if the benefit is considered in a special situation

    The benefit may not be taxable if you provide to your employee is in one of the following special situations:

    • Refer to: Your employee is working in a prescribed zone
    • Refer to: Your employee has a severe or prolonged impairment
    • If the benefit is in one of the above special situation, do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is not in one of the above special situations, the benefit provided may be a taxable benefit. Continue to Step 2 - Determine if the principal place of residence is considered a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE).

  2. Determine if the principal place of residence is considered a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE)

    A self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE) is:

    • A dwelling-house, apartment, or other similar place of residence in which place a person as a general rule sleeps and eats
    • Generally a living unit with restricted access that contains a kitchen, bathroom, and sleeping facilities
    • Separate from any other living unit in the same building.

    Generally, a room in a hotel, dormitory, boarding house, or bunkhouse is not considered a SCDE.

    Special work sites specific to fishers and seafarers

    A ship may qualify as a special work site or a remote work location when it is at sea for a period of 36 hours or more. Time at sea may include time spent on board because of:

    • Quarantine
    • Waiting for docking
    • Unloading cargo
    • Immigration or customs clearance

    The crew members of a fishing vessel, or any other type of vessel, who are paid a salary or wage (that is, they are employees rather than self-employed), may qualify for exemption from tax on the value of, or a reasonable allowance for, board and lodging received while on a ship, if the requirements of subsection 6(6) are met. The central concern regarding fishers is whether they are employees or self-employed. Questions on this determination should be referred to the CPP/EI Rulings Officer in the local tax services office. When the duties performed by an employee are of a temporary nature and the employee meets the conditions in subparagraph 6(6)(a)(i), a ship will qualify as a special work site at any time the employee is on board, whether it is in port or at sea. Where subsection 6(6) does not apply, the fair market value of rations and quarters supplied to the crew is included in their income and subject to deductions at source in the normal manner.)

    It is possible for an employee to work at a location that is both a special work site and a remote work location. However, the benefit can only be excluded from the employee's income once.

    • If the principal place of residence is not considered a SCDE, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable
    • If the principal place of residence is considered a SCDE, continue to: Step 3 - Determine if the duties performed are considered temporary

  3. Determine if the duties performed are considered temporary

    The duties performed are considered temporary if they are performed on a short-term or interim basis.

    Generally, the CRA considers that an employee's duties is temporary if they are unlikely to provide continuous employment for the employee for more than two years.

    • If the duties performed are not considered temporary, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable
    • If the duties performed are considered temporary, continue to: Step 4 - Determine if the distance between the principal place of residence and the special work site is considered unreasonable

  4. Determine if the distance between the principal place of residence and the special work site is considered unreasonable

    Generally, the CRA considers that it is not reasonable for an employee to travel between their principal place of residence and the workplace if the distance between those two places by the most direct route normally taken is at least 80 kilometres.

    Even if the distance is less than 80 kilometres, the CRA may still consider that it is not reasonable for the employee to make that trip after taking into account the following factors:

    • Condition of the road taken
    • Available means of transportation
    • Number of hours of work required of the employee
    • Time the employee would have for rest if he or she returned home every day
    • Employee's general state of physical and mental health
    • Time it takes to travel the distance, and the time of day the travel takes place
    • If the distance is considered reasonable, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable
    • If the distance is considered unreasonable, continue to: Step 5 - Determine if the benefit is taxable

  5. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, travel allowances or reimbursements you provide to your employees are taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situation: Special work sites

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The free or subsidized transportation, or the allowance, was for transportation between the special work site and your employee's principal place of residence
    • Your employee's duties require them to be away from their principal place of residence or at a special work site for a period of more than 36 hours
    • You (or a third party) provided board and lodging, or a reasonable allowance for board and lodging, to your employee for that period
    • Form TD4 is completed

    You and your employee must fill out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site, to exclude board, lodging and transportation from your employee's income. You must keep a copy. The TD4 requires the exact location of the work site and the distance to your employee's principal place of residence.

    Taxable situation

    If the travel expense you provide to your employee is:

    • For both business and personal purposes, the personal portion is taxable
    • Does not meet all of the conditions above, it is taxable

    Continue to Step 4 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

  6. Calculate the value of the benefit

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to the fair market value (FMV) of:

    • Total value of the housing or utilities expenses
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of suitability of size (step 3)
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of loss of privacy and quiet enjoyment (step 3)
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip
    Example 1 - Calculations

    Fred works at Barney Investments in the city in Ontario, where he lives in an apartment provided by his employer for 6 months. With the help of the Evaluations Division, you determined that the FMV for Fred's rent is $750 per month. The employer also covers the cost of utilities, which is approximately $150 per month. Each month, Fred pays his employer $500 for the apartment.

    • $900 ($750 + $150) is the total value of the housing or utilities that are taxable per month
    • minus $500 because Fred reimburse his employer for the housing
    • equals $400 is the value of the benefit per month, the amounts to be included on Fred's T4 slip are $400 in box 14, $250 in code 30 and $150 in code 40

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 2 - Calculations

    Tim works as a superintendent in a large apartment building. His employer provides him with a two-bedroom apartment at no charge for 6 months, which he usually rents out for $1,200 per month.

    • $1,200 is the total value of the housing that is taxable per month
    • minus $0 because Tim does not reimburse his employer for the housing
    • equals $1,200 is the value of the benefit per month, the amounts to be included on Tim's T4 slip are $1,200 in box 14 and $1,200 in code 30

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 3 - Calculations (reduction of the value)

    Tim works as a superintendent in a large apartment building. His employer provides him with a three-bedroom apartment at no charge for 6 months, which he usually rents out for $1,800 per month. The employer determined, based on the FMV of an apartment or house with two bedrooms, that only $1,200 is appropriate.

    • $1,800 is the total value of the housing that is taxable per month
    • minus $600 ($1,800 - $1,200) is the value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of suitability of size (step 3b)
    • minus $0 because Tim does not reimburse his employer for the housing
    • equals $1,200 is the value of the benefit per month, the amounts to be included on Tim's T4 slip are $1,200 in box 14 and $1,200 in code 30

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

  7. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

  8. Report the benefit on a slip
