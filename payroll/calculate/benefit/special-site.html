Board, lodging, housing, utilities and travel expenses - Provided to an employee in special work sites, including a work site located in a prescribed zone

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide a provide a benefit  for board, lodging, housing, utilities and travel expenses to your employee who works or lives in a location that is in a special work site, including in a prescribed zone, in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

Steps

  1. Determine if the benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is considered to be for board and lodging or housing and utilities

    You need to determine if the benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is considered to be for board and lodging or housing and utilities. For example:

    • If the benefit is housing and utilities, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more: Housing and utilities

    • If the benefit is board and lodging or transportation, continue to Step 2 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the benefit or allowance.

  2. Determine if any exceptions apply to the benefit or allowance

    The benefit or allowance you provide to your employee may may not be taxable if the benefit or allowance is in one of the following situations:

    • Your employee is working in a remote location
    • Housing: Your employee is a member of the clergy or of a religious order

    It is possible for an employee to work at a location that is both a special work site and a remote work location. However, the benefit can only be excluded from the employee's income once.

    • If the benefit is in one of the above situations, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more on the above exceptions using the links.

    • If the benefit is not in one of the above situations, continue to Step 3 - Determine if the benefit provided is for a period of at least 36 hours.

  3. Determine if the work location is considered a prescribed zone

    Use the following links to determine if the work location is considered a prescribed zone:

    • Alberta
    • British Columbia
    • Manitoba
    • Newfoundland and Labrador
    • Nova Scotia
    • Ontario
    • Quebec
    • Saskatchewan
    • Yukon, Nunavut, and the Northwest Territories

    • If the work location is not considered a prescribed zone, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more about benefits for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If the work location is considered a prescribed zone, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if the distance between the self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE) and the work location is not considered reasonable for your employee to return daily.

  4. Determine if the distance between the self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE) and the work location is not considered reasonable for your employee to return daily

    Generally, the CRA considers that it is not reasonable for your employee to travel between their SCDE  and the work location if the distance between those 2 places by the most direct route normally taken is at least 80 kilometres.

    Even if the distance is less than 80 kilometres, the CRA may still consider that it is not reasonable for the employee to make that trip after taking into account the following factors:

    • The condition of the road taken
    • The availability of transportation
    • The number of hours of work required of the employee
    • The time the employee would have for rest if they returned home every day
    • The employee's general state of physical and mental health
    • The time it takes to travel the distance, and the time of day the travel takes place

    • If your employee can reasonably be expected to travel between a SCDE and the special work site, do not continue to next step.

      Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If the distance travelled is considered unreasonable, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Complete Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site.

  5. Determine if the benefit provided is for a period of at least 36 hours

    The benefit is provided for a period of more than 36 hours (includes time spent to travel between your employee's place of residence and the special work site) and one of the following apply:

    • Your employee had to be away from their principal place of residence because of their employment duties
    • Your employee had to be at the special work site
    Special situation: Fishers and seafarers

    A ship may qualify as a special work site when it is at sea for a period of 36 hours or more. Time at sea may include time spent on board because of:

    • Quarantine
    • Waiting for docking
    • Unloading cargo
    • Immigration or customs clearance

    Your employee may qualify for exemption from tax on the value of, or a reasonable allowance for, board and lodging received while on a ship. If all conditions are met, a ship will qualify as a special work site at any time the employee is on board, whether it is in port or at sea.

    • If the benefit provided is for a period of less than 36 hours, do not continue to next step.

      Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If the benefit provided is for a period of more than 36 hours, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Determine if the duties performed are considered temporary.

  6. Determine if the duties performed are considered temporary

    The duties performed are considered temporary if they are performed on a short-term or interim basis.

    Generally, the CRA considers that an employee's duties is temporary if they are unlikely to provide continuous employment for the employee for more than 2 years.

    To determine if the work is considered temporary, the CRA considers the following factors:

    • Nature of the work the employee performs
    • Total estimated duration of a project or a given phase of a project for which the employee is hired
    • Agreed period of time stated in the employment contract or other terms of the employment agreement

    This must be based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific situation.

    • If the duties performed are not considered temporary, do not continue to next step.

      Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If the duties performed are considered temporary, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if your employee has a principal place of residence or a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE).

  7. Determine if your employee maintained, at another location, a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE)

    You must determine if your employee maintained, at another location, a SCDE as their principal place of residence.

    Situations: Conditions not met

    The CRA generally considers that this condition is not met if one of the following applies:

    • Your employee's principal place of residence or their SCDE is not available throughout the period for occupancy by your employee
    • Your employee rents their principal place of residence or their SCDE to another person

    • If the your employee does not have a principal place of residence or a SCDE, do not continue to next step.

      Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If your employee has a principal place of residence or a SCDE, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if your employee cannot reasonably be expected to travel between their principal place of residence or a SCDE and the special work site.

  8. Complete Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site

    You and your employee must fill out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site. You must keep a copy.

    The TD4 requires the exact location of the work site and the distance to your employee's principal place of residence.

    Once the Form TD4 is completed, the board and lodging benefit (including the travel expenses), or allowance may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

    • If you and your employee did not fill out Form TD4, do not continue to next step.

      Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If you and your employee filled out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Determine if the benefit is taxable.

  9. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, if you provide an allowance  or a reimbursement  for board, lodging, housing, utilities and travel expenses  to your employee, the benefit is taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide an allowance  or a reimbursement  to your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee was required by employment duties to be away for at least 36 hours (step 2)
    • Your employee's employment duties are considered temporary (step 3)
    • Your employee maintains a SCDE, at another location, as their principal place of residence (step 4)
    • Your employee cannot reasonably be expected to travel between their principal place of residence or a SCDE and the special work site (step 5)
    • Form TD4 is completed (step 6)
    • If the benefit is an allowance, it is reasonable

    • If the benefit is for medical travel expenses:

      • Must paid or reimbursed under the terms of a private health services plan
      • Expenses must be eligible for the medical expense tax credit (METC)

    • If the benefit is for travel expenses:

      • Was for a period during which your employee also received board and lodging benefits or reasonable allowances
      • Must relate to travel between the work location and your employee's principal place of residence
      • Must relate to travel between the work location and a location in Canada or in the country in which your employee is working

    You still have to report the benefit on the T4 slip even if part of the benefit is not taxable.

    Examples

    Example 1 - CRA administrative policy does not apply

    A northern community hires Simon to come teach at the local school. The contract is for 1 year. Simon keeps his apartment for the duration of his contract. Simon receives an allowance for board and lodging.

    The CRA administrative policy does not apply because the work is not temporary.

    The position of "teacher" will exist for as long as the school exists, and it cannot be considered temporary, even though the individual currently in this position has a contract for a limited time.

    The temporary nature of the duties is from the employer's point of view, not the employee's.

    Example 2 - CRA administrative policy applies

    Diane is an engineer who lives and works in Montréal, where she maintains a self-contained domestic establishment. She's superving the initial phase of a project in Toronto. The initial phase is for 2 years. The employer provides Annie with lodging in Toronto. Diane and her employee also filled out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site. Employer kept a copy with her records.

    The CRA administrative policy applies based on the following conditions:

    • Work is temporary
    • Employee maintained a self-contained domestic establishment in Montréal for her personal use
    • Diane has to stay in Toronto for over 36 hours
    • Form TD4 is completed and employer kept a copy

    Example 3 - Income Tax Act applies

    Peter works at a special work site and receives an allowance of $150 per day for board and lodging. This allowance is exempt from income tax under 6(6) of the ITA. On weekends, Peter does not stay on the work site and his employee gives him a check-out allowance of $300. This allowance is equal to the allowance the employee waives when he leaves the site. (2 X $150 per day)

    The Income Tax Act applies because the check-out allowance is reasonable.

    Example 4 - CRA administrative policy does not apply

    Mary works as a crew member of the Pearl. Because the vessel is undergoing repairs, Mary is off work for three weeks. However, her employer is allowing her to remain on board, free of charge, while the repairs are being completed.

    The conditions are not met because the vessel is not at sea. The employer must add the FMV of the meals and lodging (stay on board the ship) to Mary's income.

    Taxable situation

    If the benefit does not meet all of the above conditions, the personal portion of the benefit is taxable.

    • If the benefit is not related to housing expenses, continue to: Step 12 - Calculate the value of the benefit.
    • If the benefit is related to housing expenses, continue to: Step 8 - Determine if your employee is eligible for the reduction.

  10. Determine if your employee is eligible for the reduction

  11. Determine if the prescribed zone that contains the employee’s place of residence or work have a developed rental market

    If the employee resides or works in a prescribed zone, the following cities have a developed market:

    • Dawson Creek, British Columbia
    • Fort McMurray, Alberta
    • Fort St. John, British Columbia
    • Grande Prairie, Alberta
    • Labrador City, Newfoundland
    • Thompson, Manitoba
    • Yellowknife, Northwest Territories
    • Walbush, Newfoundland
    • Whitehorse, Yukon
    • If the special work site is located in a prescribed zone and has developed rental markets, continue to Step 12 - Calculate the value of the benefit.
    • If the special work site is located in a prescribed zone and does not have developed rental markets, continue to Step 11 - Determine the value of the board and lodging benefit.

  12. Determine the value of the board and lodging benefit

    In places in prescribed zones without developed rental markets, you have to use other methods to set a value on the board and lodging benefit.

    The ceiling amount  for board and lodging benefits is the maximum amount you can include in an employee's income if the employee does not pay any expenses related to board and lodging.

    What is the monthly ceiling for board and lodging benefits

    The following table shows the monthly ceiling for board and lodging benefits (including GST/HST) for the years 2023 and 2022, by type of housing and utilities.

    Monthly ceiling for housing benefits
    Type of housing Description of services 2023 2022
    Common shelter N/A $227 $213
    Apartment and duplex Rent only $612 $575
    Utilities only $297 $279
    Rent and utilities $910 $855
    House and trailer Rent only $1,024 $962
    Utilities only $452 $425
    Rent and utilities $1,476 $1,387

    Calculate the value - Accommodations you own

    If you own a residence that you provide rent free to your employee (including utilities), calculate the value of the housing or the utilities expenses using one of the following lesser amounts:

    • Fair market value of the rent or of the utilities
    • Ceiling amount

    Calculate the value - Accommodations you rent from a third party

    If you rent a residence from a third party and provide it rent free to your employee (including utilities), calculate the value of the housing or the utilities expenses using one of the following lesser amounts:

    • Amount you pay the third party
    • Ceiling amount

  13. Calculate the value of the benefit

    You must calculate based on the following benefit provided:

    Special work sites that is not in prescribed zones

    Board, lodging or housing expenses

    What is the fair market value (FMV)

    The FMV is the highest price that can be obtained on an open market for a property. This is where the buyer and seller consent to the transaction, are well-informed and are dealing at arm's length.

    The FMV of property or a service does not include GST/HST.

    • Fair market value (FMV) of the board, lodging and housing expense per month
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of suitability of size (step 8a)
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of loss of privacy and quiet enjoyment (step 8b)
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for board, lodging and housing to be included on the T4 slip in code 30 and box 14

    Utilities expenses

    • Cost of utilities per month
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for utilities to be included on the T4 slip code 40 and box 14

    Travel expenses

    • Value of the travel expense
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for travel expenses to be included on the T4 slip in code 40 and box 14

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 1 - Calculations

    Employer paid an employee $2,000 for them to take a trip from the special work site to their principal place of residence and back in Ontario. Employer paid an employee $850 to assist them to make a personal shopping trip from the special work site in a prescribed zone to a different nearby community on a different trip. The special work site was located 27 kilometres from a town with a population of 43,000 people. Employer and employee completed Form TD4, Declaration of Exemption – Employment at a Special Work Site

    • $2,850 ($2,000 + $850) is the total value of the benefit
    • minus $2,000 is the amount of the travel assistance that is not taxable to be included on the employee's T4 slip in code 31
    • equals $850 is the value of the benefit that is taxable
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $850 is the amount of the benefit that is taxable to be included the employee's T4 slip in code 40 and box 14
    Example 2 - Calculations

    An employer provides an employee with board and lodging in a prescribed zone without a developed rental market with a fair market value of $4,000. The accommodations were owned by the employer. The ceiling value of the board and lodging was $3,000.

    • $4,000 is the total value of the board and lodging
    • minus $1,000 is the amount for the difference between the fair market value and the ceiling amount that is not taxable to be included on the employee's T4 slip in code 31
    • equals $3,000 is the value of the board and lodging that is taxable
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $3,000 is the amount of the board and lodging that is taxable to be included the employee's T4 slip in code 30 and box 14
