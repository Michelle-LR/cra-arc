Board and lodging, and transportation - Provided to an employee working at a remote location

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

Generally, if you provide board and lodging, or transportation or an allowance  for board and lodging, or transportation to your employee in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties, the benefit is taxable. This includes situations where you provide board and lodging, or transportation to your employee at a cost that is less than the fair market value (FMV) of the board and lodging, or transportation.

Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable for board and lodging, or transportation to your employee who works in a remote location.

Steps

  1. Determine if the benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is considered to be for board and lodging or housing and utilities

    You need to determine if the benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is considered to be for board and lodging or housing and utilities. For example:

    • If the benefit is housing and utilities, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more: Housing and utilities

    • If the benefit is board and lodging, or transportation, continue to Step 2 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the benefit or allowance.

  2. Determine if any exceptions apply to the benefit or allowance

    The board and lodging, or transportation benefit or allowance you provide to your employee may may not be taxable if the benefit or allowance is in one of the following situations:

    • Your employee works at a special work site, including a work site located in a prescribed zone
    • Your employee is a member of the clergy or of a religious order

    It is possible for an employee to work at a location that is both a remote work location and a special work site. However, the benefit can only be excluded from the employee's income once.

    • If the benefit or allowance is in one of the above situations, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more on the above exceptions using the links.

    • If the benefit or allowance is not in one of the above situations, continue to Step 3 - Determine if the work location is considered remote.

  3. Determine if the work location is considered remote

    The CRA considers a location remote based on all of the following factors:

    1. The availability of transportation

      • If the transportation availability is reasonable, do not continue to the next step.

        Learn more about benefits for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

      • If the availability of the transportation is not reasonable, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions under the 3 factors are met.

        Continue to: Step 3b - Distrance between the remote location and the nearest established community.

    2. Distrance between the remote location and the nearest established community

      Generally, the CRA considers the work location to be remote when it is 80 kilometres or more from the nearest established community with a population of at least 1,000 people.

      A location is considered an established community if it has essential services or those services are available within a reasonable commuting distance.

      Essential services may include access to:

      • Basic food store
      • Basic clothing store, with merchandise in stock (not a mail-order outlet)
      • Accommodation
      • Certain medical services
      • Certain educational facilities

      • If the distance travelled is 80 kilometres or less, the work location is not considered remote. Do not continue to the next step.

        Learn more about benefits for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

      • If the the distance travelled is 80 kilometres or more, the work location meets this factor.

        Continue to: Step 3c - The time required to travel the distance.

    3. The time required to travel the distance

      The board and lodging, or transportation is provided for a period of more than 36 hours and one of the following apply:

      • Your employee had to be away from their principal place of residence because of their employment duties
      • Your employee had to be at the remote work location
      Fishers and seafarers

      A ship may qualify as a remote work location when it is at sea for a period of 36 hours or more. Time at sea may include time spent on board because of:

      • Quarantine
      • Waiting for docking
      • Unloading cargo
      • Immigration or customs clearance

      Your employee may qualify for exemption from tax on the value of, or a reasonable allowance for, board and lodging received while on a ship. If all conditions are met, a ship will qualify as a remote work location at any time the employee is on board, whether it is in port or at sea.

      • If the benefit provided is for a period of less than 36 hours, the work location is not considered remote. Do not continue to the next step.

        Learn more about benefits for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

      • If the benefit provided is for a period of more than 36 hours, the work location meets this factor.

    • If the work location does not meet all 3 factors, the work location is not considered remote. Do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more about benefits for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

    • If the work location meet all 3 factors, the work location is considered remote.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if the benefit or allowance is taxable.

  4. Determine if the benefit or allowance is taxable

    Generally, board, lodging and transportation benefit or allowance you provide to your employees is taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the Income Tax Act (ITA).

    1. Determine if the board and lodging benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is taxable

      Under the ITA, if you provide free or subsidized board and lodging or an allowance to your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

      • The work location is considered remote from any established community (meets all conditions in step 3)
      • The benefit or allowance is provided for a period of at least 36 hours (meets all conditions in step 4)
      • Your employee could not reasonably be expected to set up or maintain a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE)  in a remote location
        How to determine what is considered reasonable and examples where conditions are not met

        You must determine if your employee cannot reasonably be expected to establish or maintain a SCDE considering the remoteness of the location and the distance from any community. This must be based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific situation.

        Situations: Conditions not met

        The CRA generally considers that this condition is not met if one of the following applies:

        • You provide your employee with a SCDE in the remote location
        • Your employee is a member of the local community and has already established a home there
        • Your employee decides to buy land and build a SCDE
        • Your employee rents a SCDE in the remote location
        Examples

        Example 1 - CRA administrative policy applies

        Nicolae was hired by a mining company to work in Muskrat Cove on a 3 year contract. He can go home for 2 weeks every 3 months. Muskrat Cove has a population of 800 people. The closest community is 90 km away. Nicolae receives free board and lodging (dormitory).

        The CRA administrative policy applies based on the following conditions:

        • Nicolae cannot reasonably expect to maintain a SCDE since the closest community is 90 km away and has only 800 inhabitants
        • Nicolae has to be in the remote location for 3 months before being able to return home (over 36 hours)
        • Employer is not providing him with a SCDE

        Example 2 - CRA administrative policy applies

        Ben was hired by a mining company along with 300 other employees to work in a community of 900 inhabitants and the closest community with 1,000 residents or more is 120 kilometres away. There is not enough accommodations or services available in the community he works or nearby to provide for a SCDE. Ben's principal place of residence is Vancouver. Ben works for 11 days and then has 3 days off. Ben receives free board and lodging at a local hotel.

        The CRA administrative policy applies based on the following conditions:

        • Ben cannot reasonably be expected to establish and maintain a SCDE given:
          • The work site is remote from any established community
          • The distance from any established community is greater than 80 kilometres
        • Ben works at this location for over 36 hours
        • Employer is not providing him a SCDE

        Example 3 - Income Tax Act applies

        Peter works at a remote site and receives an allowance of $150 per day for board and lodging. This allowance is exempt from income tax under 6(6) of the ITA. On weekends, Peter does not stay on the remote site and his employer gives him a check-out allowance of $300. This allowance is equal to the allowance the employee waives when he leaves the site. (2 X $150 per day)

        The Income Tax Act applies because the check-out allowance is reasonable.

        Example 4 - CRA administrative policy does not apply

        Mary works as a crew member of the Pearl. Because the vessel is undergoing repairs, Mary is off work for three weeks. However, her employer is allowing her to remain on board, free of charge, while the repairs are being completed.

        The conditions are not met because the vessel is not at sea. The employer must add the FMV of the meals and lodging (stay on board the ship) to Mary's income.

      • You did not provide a SCDE  to your employee.
      • If you provided a board and lodging allowance, the allowance must be reasonable.

      • If the board and lodging benefit or allowance is taxable, the transportation benefit or allowance will also be taxable.

        Continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the benefit or allowance.

      • If the board and lodging benefit or allowance is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations.

        Continue to: Step 5b - Determine if the transportation benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is taxable.

    2. Determine if the transportation benefit or allowance you provide to your employee is taxable

      Under the ITA, if you (or a third party) provide free or subsidized transportation or an allowance to your employee, the benefit or allowance is not taxable if all of the following apply:

      • The work location is considered remote from any established community (meets all conditions in step 3)
      • The benefit or allowance is provided for a period of at least 36 hours (meets all conditions in step 4)
      • The transportation was for a period during which your employee also received board and lodging benefit or allowance (meets all conditions in step 5a).
      • The transportation must relate to transportation between the work location and a location in Canada or in the country in which the employee is working.
      • If you provided a transportation allowance, the allowance must be reasonable.

      • If the transportation benefit or allowance is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations.

        Do not continue to the next step because you do not include this amount in the income of your employee.

      • If the transportation benefit or allowance is taxable, continue to: Step 6 - Calculate the value of the benefit or allowance.

  5. Calculate the value of the benefit or allowance

    If the benefit or allowance is taxable, the value of the benefit or allowance is:

    Board and lodging

    • FMV  of the board and lodging that is taxable
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for board and lodging to be included on a T4 slip in code 30 and box 14 (or code 028 on a T4A slip)

    Transportation

    • Value of the transportation
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit for transportation to be included on the T4 slip in code 40 and box 14 (or code 028 on a T4A slip)

    If the allowance is more than the reasonable amount but meets all other conditions under the ITA (steps 3, 4 and 5), only the value over that is over the reasonable amount for board, lodging and transportation must be included in the employee's income.

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

  6. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

  7. Report the benefit or allowance on a slip

    If the benefit or allowance is taxable, you must report the following on a T4 or T4A slip:

    • T4 slip - Employer

      • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

        • Box 14 - Employment Income
        • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
        • Code 30 - Board and lodging
        • Code 40 - Other information, transportation

      • Cash: Option 2

        • Box 14 - Employment Income
        • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
        • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
        • Code 30 - Board and lodging
        • Code 40 - Other information, Transportation

    • T4A slip - Payer

      • Code 028 - Other income, Total amount of board, lodging and transportation
