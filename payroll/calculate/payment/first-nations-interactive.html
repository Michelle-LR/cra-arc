Payments to First Nations workers

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement or to the CRA administrative policy.

You must report all income and deductions on a T4 or T4A slip for First Nations workers regardless of whether the income is taxable or exempt.

The term First Nations worker on this page refers only to an worker who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act. Many Indigenous people in Canada prefer not to describe themselves as Indians and view this term as rooted in colonialism and racism. Under the Indian Act, the precise legal meaning of the term "Indian" refers to First Nations persons who are entitled to registration.

Employment income

Steps

  1. Determine if the First Nations employment income is exempt from tax

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    To determine if the First Nations employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt, ask your employee to fill out Form TD1-IN, Determination of Exemption of an Indian's Employment Income to certify they meet all of the following conditions for the exemption:

    • Your employee is registered under the Indian Act
    • Your employee is not a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act
    • If your employee lives on a reserve, it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of my daily routine

    You must also certify that information given on Form TD1-IN is correct and complete.

    Situation: Non-employment income (for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP)

    Generally, if the worker is receiving unemployment insurance benefits, retiring allowances, Canada Pension Plan (CPP) payments, Quebec Pension Plan (QPP) payments, Registered Pension Plan (RPP) benefits or Wage Loss Replacement Plan (WLRP) benefits is exempt from income tax when the payment is received as a result of employment income that was exempt from income tax.

    If the employment income was partly-exempt from income tax, the exemption is prorated to the portion of the these amounts.

    Exempt income situation

    Generally, the income is exempt from income tax if all of the following apply:

    • Worker lives on a reserve
    • Employer is a resident on a reserve

    Examples where exempt (guideline 2)

    Examples where not exempt (guideline 2)

    Where an employee is a shareholder who controls the business (non-arm's length with the corporation), the source of the business income may be relevant in determining whether the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act applies to the income received in the shareholder's capacity as an employee. Where the source of the business income in this situation is off the reserve, the employment income (salary and bonuses) will not be considered to be connected to the reserve and will not be exempt from tax.

    Situation: Other situations where your employee is required to perform duties on a reserve

    Depending on your situation, the employment income of status Indians may be exempt or partly exempt from tax. It is the income that is exempt from tax and not the person.

    The rules for CPP and EI are different. EI premiums are always required. Income that is exempt from income tax is not pensionable employment although you or the employee may elect into the CPP.

  5. Determine the percentage of duties related to employment on a reserve

    To determine the portion of employment duties performed on a reserve, two factors are generally taken into account:

    • Whether an actual "duty of employment" was performed (the employee was required to perform the particular task and was compensated by the employer for performing that particular task)
    • The extent to which the duty of employment was performed on a reserve

    How to calculate the percentage of duties related to employment performed on and off a reserve

    • Total working days on a reserve (do not include vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays)
    • divide by Total working days
    • equals Result
    • multiply by 100
    • equals This is the % of duties of employment performed on reserve (paid leave such as vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays leave is also exempt)
    Calculation example

    An employer requires their status Indian employee to perform duties related to their employment both on and off reserve. The employee had a total of 220 working days in the year, 44 of which were on reserve. These days spent working on the reserve did not include either vacation or sick leave.

    The percentage of the status Indian employee’s duties related to employment performed on a reserve is calculated as follows:

    • 44 total working days on a reserve (not including vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays)
    • divide by 220 total working days
    • equals 0.2 is the result
    • multiply by 100
    • equals 20% is the percentage of duties of employment performed on reserve (paid leave such as vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays is also exempt)

  6. Withhold and report the payment on a slip

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions and report on a T4 slip the payment you made to your employee:

    Income all exempt

    • Withholding obligations

      • Income tax (tax-exempt income)
      • EI (all income)
      • CPP (only if you filed Form CPT124)

    • Reporting obligations

      • Boxes 16 and 26 (only if you filed Form CPT124)
      • Boxes 18 and 24 (all income)
      • Code 69 on tax-exempt non-eligible retiring allowances you paid
      • Code 71 on tax-exempt employment income you paid

    CPP is only withheld on the portion of the income that is taxable. Where the income is not taxable, CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed a CPT124 election - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

    If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, a First Nations employee can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

    Income partly-exempt

    • Withholding obligations

      • Income tax (only on taxable income)
      • EI (on all income)
      • CPP (only on taxable income or on all income if you filed Form CPT124)

    • Reporting obligations

      • Boxes 14 (only on taxable income)
      • Boxes 16 and 26 (only on taxable income or on all income if you filed Form CPT124)
      • Boxes 18 and 24 (on all income)
      • Code 66 if you paid eligible retiring allowances (only on taxable income)
      • Code 67 if you paid non-eligible retiring allowances (only on taxable income)
      • Code 69 if you paid non-eligible retiring allowances (only on tax-exempt income)
      • Code 71 (only on tax-exempt income)
      • Codes for taxable benefits (for example, code 40) (only on taxable income)
