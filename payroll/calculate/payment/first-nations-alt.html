Payments to First Nations workers

Content has been updated to remove the word Indian where it is not a direct reference to the Indian Act, including improving clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement or to the CRA administrative policy.

Meaning of the terms “First Nations employee”, “First Nations recipient” and “First Nations worker”

On this webpage, the terms “First Nations employee”, “First Nations recipient” and “First Nations worker” are used to describe an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

We recognize that many First Nations people in Canada prefer not to describe themselves as Indians. However, we sometimes use the term “Indian” because it has a legal meaning in the Indian Act.

You have a legal obligation to report all income and deductions on a T4 or T4A slip for First Nations workers regardless of whether the income is taxable or exempt.

What is the purpose of reporting exempt income on a T4 or T4A slip

Since net exempt income is not reported on a T1 return, these amounts are used to calculate using Form T90, Income Exempt from Tax under the Indian Act. The information on Form T90 is used to determine the eligibility of the worker for the Canada Workers' Benefit and other related provincial or territorial benefits.

Employment income

Steps

  1. Determine if the employee’s employment income is exempt from tax

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    To determine if the First Nations employee’s employment income is fully or partially tax-exempt, ask your employee to fill out part 1 of Form TD1-IN, Determination of Exemption of an Indian's Employment Income to certify they meet all of the following conditions for the exemption:

    • Your employee is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act
    • Your employee is not a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act
    • If your employee lives on a reserve, it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine

    You must also certify that information you provided on Form TD1-IN is correct and complete.

    What if the First Nations employee's employment situation changes

    If the facts of the First Nations employee's employment situation change over the course of the year, you may need to reexamine whether the tax exemption will still apply. Changes that requires you to reexamine whether the income will be exempt from tax for the remainder of the year include:

    • A change in the First Nations employee’s residence, on or off reserve
    • A change in the employer’s residence, on or off reserve
    • A change in the location of the employment duties

    Learn more about registration under the Indian Act and the Indian Register:

    • From ISC: How to prove registration
    • From CIRNAC: Treaty and agreement negotiations

    • If the employee does not meet all conditions for the exemption, do not continue to next step.

      You must deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees.

    • If the First Nations employee meets all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 2 - Determine if you can use the guideline for common employment situations.

  2. Determine if you can use the guidelines for common employment situations

    You can use the Indian Act exemption for employment income guidelines for common employment situations.

    Depending on the situation, there may be uncommon factors that result in employment income being treated differently. This means that any factors connecting the income to a reserve must be analyzed based on various court decisions to determine if the exemption applies.

    For example, situations where a First Nations employee is also a shareholder are considered uncommon employment situations.

    Examples of uncommon situations: Exempt , not exempt and employee leasing - Tax planning arrangements

    • If your situation is uncommon, do not continue to next step.

      You can call CRA at 1-800-959-8281.

      You must deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees until you receive confirmation from the CRA.

    • If your situation is common, you can use the Indian Act exemption for employment income guideline.

      Continue to Step 3 - Determine if the employment income is fully or partially tax-exempt.

      or you can read the full text of the Indian Act exemption for employment income guidelines for common employment situations

  3. Determine if the employment income is fully or partially tax-exempt

    You must determine if your First Nations employee’s employment income is fully or partially tax-exempt depending on the situation.

    1. Determine if guideline 2 applies

      Generally, all of the income is exempt from income tax if all of the following apply:

      • Employee lives on a reserve
      • Employer is a resident on a reserve

      Examples: Exempt and not exempt

      • If your employee lives on a reserve and you (as the employer) are a resident on a reserve, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income and select income fully tax-exempt.

      • If the situation does not apply, continue to Step 3b - Determine if guideline 4 applies.

    2. Determine if guideline 4 applies

      Generally, the income is exempt from income tax if all of the following apply:

      • You (as the employer) are resident on a reserve

      • You (as the employer) are:

        • A First Nations band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing 1 or more First Nations bands that have reserves
        • A First Nations organization controlled by a First Nations band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing 1 or more First Nations bands that have reserves, and the organization is dedicated exclusively to the social, cultural, educational, or economic development of First Nations individuals who for the most part live on reserves
      • Employee's duties of employment are in connection with the employer's non-commercial activities carried on exclusively for the benefit of First Nations individuals who for the most part live on reserves

      Examples: Exempt and not exempt

      • If you (as the employer) are resident on a reserve and you meet all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income and select income fully tax-exempt.

      • If the situation does not apply, continue to Step 3c - Determine if guideline 3 applies.

    3. Determine if guideline 3 applies

      Generally, the First Nations employee’s income is exempt from income tax if all of the following apply:

        • Employee lives on a reserve
        • Employer is a resident on a reserve
      • Employee performs more than 50% of their employment duties on a reserve

      Examples: Exempt and not exempt

      • If your employee performs more than 50% of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income and select income fully tax-exempt.

      • If the situation does not apply, continue to Step 3d - Determine if guideline 1 applies.

    4. Determine if guideline 1 applies

      Generally, the First Nations employee’s income is exempt from income tax if the employee performs at least 90% of the duties related to their employment on a reserve.

      Examples: Exempt and not exempt

      • If your employee performs at least 90% of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income and select income fully tax-exempt.
      • If your employee performs less than 90% of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 3e - Determine if a prorated exemption applies to the employment income (guideline 1 – proration rule).

    5. Determine if a prorated exemption applies to the employment income (guideline 1 – proration rule)

      Generally, the income is partly tax-exempt from income tax if your First Nations employee performs less than 90% of the duties related to their employment on a reserve and the income is not exempt in other situations.

      The exemption is prorated to the portion of the income related to the duties the employee performed on the reserve.

      Examples of proration: Exempt and not exempt

      Continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income and select income partly tax-exempt.

  4. Withhold deductions on the employment income

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions on the employment income you paid to your First Nations employee:

    • Fully tax-exempt

      • Income tax - Do not withhold
      • EI - Withhold on all income
      • CPP - Withhold only if you filed Form CPT124

    • Partly tax-exempt

      • Income tax - Withhold only on taxable income
      • EI - Withhold on all income
      • CPP - Withhold only on taxable income or on all income if you filed Form CPT124

    5. CPP is only withheld on the portion of the income that is taxable. Where the income is not taxable, CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed Form CPT124 - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

    If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, a First Nations employee can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

    Learn more: How to calculate payroll deductions and contributions

      • Boxes 16 and 26 - Report CPP contributions and pensionable earnings only if you filed Form CPT124
      • Boxes 18 and 24 - Report EI premiums and insurable earnings on all income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 20 - Do not report if you withheld RPP contributions (you must provide proof to your employee of RPP contributions for all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)

        2024 and after For T4 slips filed for calendar year 2024 and after, you will need to report RPP contributions relating to tax-exempt income using a new code.

      • Code 69 - Report tax-exempt non-eligible retiring allowances you paid
      Union dues
      • Box 44 - Do not report if you withheld union dues (you must provide proof to your employee of union dues withheld from all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)

        2024 and after For T4 slips filed for calendar year 2024 and after, you will need to report union dues relating to tax-exempt income using a new code.

    • Partly tax-exempt

      Income and source deductions
      • Boxes 14 - Report only on taxable employment income
      • Code 71 - Report only tax-exempt employment income
      • Boxes 16 and 26 - Report CPP contributions and pensionable earnings only on taxable income or on all income if you filed Form CPT124
      • Boxes 18 and 24 Report EI premiums and insurable earnings on all income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 20 - Report only on taxable income if you withheld RPP contributions (you must provide proof to your employee of RPP contributions for all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)

        2024 and after For T4 slips filed for calendar year 2024 and after, you will need to report RPP contributions relating to tax-exempt income using a new code.

      • Code 66 - Report only on taxable income if you paid eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 67 - Report only on taxable income if you paid non-eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 69 - Report only tax-exempt if you paid non-eligible retiring allowances
      Union dues
      • Box 44 - Report only on taxable income if you withheld union dues (you must provide proof to your employee of union dues withheld from all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)

        2024 and after For T4 slips filed for calendar year 2024 and after, you will need to report union dues relating to tax-exempt income using a new code.

      Taxable benefits
      • Codes for taxable benefits (for example, code 40) - Report only on taxable income if you provided employment benefits

    Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted CPP, EI or income tax from the payment.

Employment-related income

Steps

  1. Determine if the employment-related income is exempt from tax

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    Generally, if the First Nations recipient is receiving employment-related income (for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP), the employment-related income is exempt from income tax when the income is received as a result of employment income that was exempt from income tax.

    Type of employment-related income

    Example employment-related income

    • If the employment income was not exempt, do not continue to next step.

      You must deduct income tax as you would for your other employees.

    • If the employment income was exempt or partly-exempt, continue to Step 2 - Determine the percentage of employment-related income that was exempt.

  2. Determine the percentage of employment-related income that was exempt

    You need to determine the portion of the employment-related income of the First Nations employee that is related to tax-exempt employment income when the payment was received as a result of employment income.

    Generally, the employment-related income will be exempt to the extent that the share of employment income that gave rise to the payment was exempt from tax as follows:

    • Fully tax-exempt

      • All employment-related income related to tax-exempt employment income will be exempt from tax

    • Partly tax-exempt

      • The portion of employment-related income related to tax-exempt employment income will be exempt from tax
      • Any employment-related income arising from the taxable portion of employment income will be taxable

  3. Withhold deductions on the employment-related income paid to the First Nations recipient

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions on the employment-related income paid to the First Nations recipient:

    • Fully tax-exempt

      • Income tax - Do not withhold
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • CPP - Do not withhold

    • Partly tax-exempt

      • Income tax - Withhold only on taxable income
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • CPP - Do not withhold

    Learn more: How to calculate payroll deductions and contributions

      • Code 195 - Report tax-exempt PRPP payments
      Lump-sum payments

      • Code 148 - Report tax-exempt lump-sum payments

    5. Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted income tax from the payment.

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    The self-employment income earned by the First Nations worker must be situated on a reserve to be exempt from tax.

    To determine if the income is situated on a reserve, you should apply the connecting factors test .

    Examples of business or self-employment income situated on a reserve

    Examples of self-employment fishing income where exempt or not exempt

    • If the self-employment income is not considered to be situated on a reserve, do not continue to next step.

    • If the self-employment income meets all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 2 - Determine the percentage of income situated on a reserve.

  2. Determine the percentage of income situated on a reserve

    You need to determine the portion of the income-earning activities of the self-employed First Nations worker that takes place on a reserve.

    Generally, the exemption applies depending on the percentage of income situated on a reserve as follows:

    • Fully tax-exempt

      • All income-earning activities will be tax-exempt
      • All business expenses will be allocated to the tax-exempt income

    • Partly tax-exempt

      • All income-earning activities that are situated on reserve will be tax-exempt
      • All income-earning activities that are situated off reserve will be taxable
      • Business expenses arising from the taxable portion of the income will be allocated to that income, unless another allocation can be shown to be more reasonable

  3. Remit deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations worker

    Generally, you must remit the following deductions on the self-employment income paid to the First Nations worker in these industries regardless if the income is exempt or partly-exempt:

    • Income tax - Do not remit
    • EI - Remit on all income (under special EI regulations, EI is remitted on behalf of the worker on all of their insurable earnings)
    • CPP - Do not remit

    • If the self-employment income meets all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 2 - Determine the percentage of income situated on a reserve.

    Introduction

    Section 87 of the Indian Act exempts from taxation the personal property of a First Nations employee situated on a reserve. The courts have determined that, for the purposes of section 87 of the Indian Act, employment income is personal property. In the case of employment income earned by a First Nations employee, therefore, what must be determined is whether the employment income is situated on a reserve. The courts have directed that connecting factors must be considered when making this determination. The Canada Revenue Agency, after receiving representations from interested First Nations groups and individuals, has identified a number of connecting factors that can be used to determine whether employment income is situated on a reserve.

    The location of the employment duties is a major connecting factor. However, the Department also recognizes that employees of bands, tribal councils, or organizations that operate on behalf of bands or tribal councils may perform most of their activities off reserve. For the employees, the connection to a reserve is that the employer is resident on a reserve and the First Nations employee is employed in a non-commercial activity for the social, cultural, educational, or economic development of First Nations individuals who for the most part live on reserves.

    With a view to assisting the First Nations community, the Department has developed the following guidelines, incorporating the various relevant connecting factors, that describe the employment situations covered by section 87 of the Indian Act. The treatment of other income related to employment is also discussed.

    These guidelines are an administrative tool to assist First Nations employees in determining whether their employment income is exempt or not.

    Most employment situations that are subject to the exemption from taxation are covered in this brochure. However, it should be noted that, when applying all the connecting factors, there may be unusual or exceptional circumstances where all of the following apply:

    • The income may not be taxable even though it does not fall within one of the guidelines; or
    • The income may be taxable even though it appears to fall within one of these guidelines.

    If you have any questions about a particular situation, contact us toll free at 1-800-959-8281.

    Each of the individuals in the following examples is a First Nations employee or worker

    Guideline 1

    When at least 90% of the duties of an employment are performed on a reserve, all of the income of a First Nations employee from that employment will usually be exempt from income tax.

    Examples where exempt

    Example - Employee A

    Employee A works as a mechanic for an automobile repair shop, performing their duties in a garage located on a reserve. The wages the employee receives are exempt because the duties are performed on a reserve.

    Example - Employee B

    Employee B lives off reserve and works as a driver for a heating-oil supplier, performing almost all of their duties making deliveries to houses on a reserve. The wages the employee receives are exempt because more than 90% of the duties are performed on a reserve.

    Example - Employee C

    Employee C works in an office on a reserve. The employee's duties include a daily drive into a nearby town off reserve where the employee does the banking for the business and picks up the mail and supplies for the business. The wages the employee receives are exempt, although the employee incidentally leaves the reserve in the course of carrying out the duties.

    Example where not exempt

    Example - Employee D

    Employee D works for a logging company that is not resident on a reserve, cutting trees under license on provincial Crown land. The wages the employee receives are taxable because the land does not form part of a reserve.

    Employee D lives on a reserve, but this factor alone is not sufficient to connect their income to a location on a reserve.

    Proration rule

    When less than 90% of the duties of an employment are performed on a reserve and the employment income is not exempted by another guideline, the exemption is to be prorated. The exemption will apply to the portion of the income related to the duties performed on the reserve.

    Examples where exempt

    Example - Employee E

    Employee E lives off reserve and works for a government department, mostly at a location off reserve. The employee spends one-half day per week on a regular basis working in a clinic on a reserve. Since 1/10 of the employee's work is performed on a reserve, 10% of the employee's income from this employment is exempt.

    Example - Employee F

    Employee F lives off reserve and works for a club at a location off reserve. Each summer for 1 month, the employee works for the club at a camp operated on a reserve. The employee's income earned at the summer camp is exempt.

    Example where not exempt

    Example - Employee G

    Employee G lives off reserve and works for a stationery store located off reserve. On rare occasions, the employee delivers some of the store's goods to customers on reserve. None of the employee's income is exempt. The trips to the reserve are merely occasional and are not a meaningful connecting factor.

    Guideline 2

    All of the income of a First Nations employee from an employment will usually be exempt from income tax when all of the following apply:

    • The employer is resident on a reserve
    • The First Nations employee lives on a reserve
    Examples where exempt

    Example - Employee H

    Employee H works for a logging company that is resident on a reserve. The employee lives on a reserve, but performs their duties at lumber camps located off the reserve. The employee's employment income is exempt from income tax because the place where the employee lives and the residence of the employee's employer are factors connecting the income to a reserve.

    Example - Employee I

    Employee I is a construction worker, employed by a construction company that is resident on a reserve, to work on building sites that are not located on a reserve. The employee lives on a reserve, except for short periods each year when the employee lives near the construction sites. When away from the reserve, the employee retains their residence on the reserve where the employee's family lives. Employee I is exempt from income tax on their employment income because the place where the employee lives and the residence of the employee's employer are factors connecting his employment income to a reserve.

    Examples where not exempt

    Example - Employee J

    Employee J lives on a reserve and works as an accountant at the head office of a bank located off reserve in a nearby city. The bank maintains a branch on employee J's reserve and arranges for the employee to be paid at that branch. The employee's employment income is taxable because the only substantive factor linking the employee's employment income to a reserve is their residence which, without other substantial connecting factors, does not result in exemption. Payment on the reserve is viewed as a connecting factor of relatively little weight.

    Example - Employee K

    Employee K is a construction worker employed by a corporation situated on a reserve. The corporation is an employment agency that makes employees available to work for clients that are not situated on a reserve, on building sites not located on a reserve. Employee K lives on a reserve. The employment agency had an office on the reserve, but carried out very few of its business activities on the reserve. The guidelines would not apply in this case, and one must therefore do an analysis of the connecting factors. Employee K's employment income is taxable because the residence of the employer in this case has little weight as a connecting factor. The only other substantive factor connecting employee K's employment income to a reserve is their residence, which is not sufficient to bring the employment income within the exemption.

    Guideline 3

    All of the income of a First Nations employee from an employment will usually be exempt from income tax when all of the following apply:

    • More than 50% of the duties of an employment are performed on a reserve
    • The employer is resident on a reserve, or the First Nations employee lives on a reserve
    Example where exempt

    Example - Employee L

    Employee L lives on a reserve and works as a police officer for an off-reserve employer. The employee performs more than 1/2 of their duties on reserve, and the employee has an additional connecting factor in that the employee lives on the reserve. Therefore, the employee is exempt on the whole of the employment income.

    Example where not exempt

    Example - Employee M

    Employee M lives off reserve in a town where the employee works in a restaurant owned by a corporation resident on a reserve. Once a week, the employee drives to the reserve to pick up the pay cheques for the restaurant staff. The employee is taxable on the whole of their employment income because the only factor connecting the income to a reserve is the residence of the employer and, without other connecting factors, this is not sufficient to confer the exemption.

    Guideline 4

    All of the income of a First Nations employee from an employment will usually be exempt from income tax when all of the following apply:

    • The employer is resident on a reserve
    • The employer is one of the following:
      • A First Nations band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing one or more First Nations bands which have reserves
      • An First Nations organization controlled by one or more such bands or tribal councils, if the organization is dedicated exclusively to the social, cultural, educational, or economic development of First Nations individual who for the most part live on reserves
    • The duties of the employment are in connection with the employer's non-commercial activities carried on exclusively for the benefit of First Nations individual who for the most part live on reserves
    Examples where exempt

    Example - Employee N

    Employee N works for the education department of a tribal council at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Employee N lives off reserve and the tribal council is resident on one of the reserves. Employee N is exempt from income tax on their employment income because the duties the employee performs for the tribal council are connected to the reserves served by the council, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

    Example - Employee O

    Employee O is a librarian working for a technical training institute operated by 5 First Nations bands for First Nations individuals who live on reserve. The administrative office of the institute is on a reserve. Employee O lives off reserve and works at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Employee O is exempt from income tax on their employment income because the duties the employee performs for the First Nations organization that employs the employee are connected to the reserves served by the First Nations organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

    Example - Employee P

    Employee P works for an First Nations organization providing child and family related services to members of a large number of bands with reserves scattered over a large area within a province. Some of these services are provided in the provincial capital, where employee P works, and the organization's administrative office is at an off-reserve location central to the bands served. However, the organization's directors, consisting of the band chiefs, meet at each reserve in rotation. Employee P is exempt from income tax on their employment income because the duties the employee performs for the First Nations organization that employs the employee are connected to the reserves served by the First Nations organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

    Example - Employee Q

    Employee Q works for a company that is resident on a reserve and owned by a tribal council. The company exists to provide adequate low-cost housing on reserves and the company's property is for the most part on reserves. Employee Q performs their duties at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Employee Q is exempt from income tax on their employment income because the duties the employee performs for the First Nations organization that employs the employee are connected to the reserves served by the First Nations organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

    Example - Employee R

    Employee R works for an First Nations organization that manages funds provided by the government to assist in planning for future education needs on reserves. The employee's employer is resident on a reserve, but the employee works at a location off reserve. Employee R is exempt from income tax on their employment income because the duties the employee performs for the First Nations organization that employs the employee are connected to the reserves served by the First Nations organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

    Examples where not exempt

    Example - Employee S

    Employee S works for a commercial building supplies company that is owned by a tribal council and is resident on a reserve. The employee performs their duties off reserve and lives off reserve. Employee S is taxable on their employment income because, although there is one factor, the residence of the employer, connecting the income to a reserve, this factor by itself is not sufficient to confer the exemption when the employer and the employee are active in the commercial mainstream of society.

    Example - Employee T

    Employee T works for an First Nations organization dedicated to organizing social programs for off-reserve First Nations individuals. The organization is located off reserve. Employee T is taxable on their employment income because there are no factors connecting that income to a location on a reserve.

    Employment-related income

    The receipt of unemployment insurance benefits, retiring allowances, Canada Pension Plan payments, Quebec Pension Plan payments, registered pension plan benefits or wage loss replacement plan benefits will usually be exempt from income tax when received as a result of employment income that was exempt from tax.

    If a portion of the employment income was exempt, then a similar portion of these amounts will be exempt.

    Examples

    Example - Employee U

    Employee U has always lived on a reserve and, until recently, the employee worked as a labourer for an employer located on the reserve. Employee U is currently unemployed and receives Employment Insurance benefits. Since the income from employment that entitled the employee to these benefits was not subject to income tax, the Employment Insurance benefits will not be subject to income tax.

    Example - Employee V

    Employee V worked for a public health office located in a city off reserve. Throughout the employee's career, 1/5 of the employee's annual employment income was exempt from income tax because the employee spent 1 day a week performing their duties at a clinic on a nearby reserve. Upon retiring, employee V received a retiring allowance from their employer and started receiving Canada Pension Plan payments. 1/5 of the employee's retiring allowance and 1/5 of the employee's Canada Pension Plan payments will not be subject to income tax.

    Meaning of terms used

    Employer is resident on a reserve

    "Employer is resident on a reserve" means that the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located.

    The central management and control of an organization is usually considered to be exercised by the group that performs the function of a board of directors of the organization. However, it may be that the real management and control of an organization is exercised by some other person or group. Generally, management and control is exercised at the principal place of business but it is recognized that this function may be legitimately exercised in a place other than the principal administrative office of the organization. It is a question of fact where the central management and control is exercised.

    First Nations

    "First Nations" means an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    First Nations employee lives on the reserve

    "First Nations employee lives on the reserve" means the First Nations employee lives on the reserve in a domestic establishment that is his or her principal place of residence and that is the centre of his or her daily routine.

    On a reserve

    "On a reserve" means on a reserve as defined for purposes of the Indian Act, including any settlements deemed to be reserves for purposes of the Indian Settlements Remission Order, and any other areas given similar treatment under federal legislation (for example, Category 1-A lands under the Cree-Naskapi (of Quebec) Act).

    Social, cultural, educational or economic development

    "Social, cultural, educational or economic development" includes the provision of social services such as health care or counselling.
