Lump-sum payments

Content to be covered: Lump-sum payments and Qualifying retroactive lump-sum payments

You may be looking for:

  • Bonuses, retroactive pay increases or irregular amounts
  • Wages in lieu of termination notice

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may provide a lum-sum payment to an individual. Generally, you make payroll deductions on lum-sum payments.

  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if the payment is considered a lump-sum payment

    If you make one of the following payment, it is consider a lump-sum payment:

    • Retiring allowances (severance pay)
    • Death benefits
    • Payments from a registered retirement savings plan (RRSP)
    • Payments from a registered pension plan (RPP)
    • Payments from a Deferred Profit Sharing Plan (DPSP)
    • Payments to the original annuitant under a registered retirement income fund (RRIF)
    • Payments from an individual pension plan (IPP)
    • Payments from a retirement compensation arrangement (RCA)
    • Payments from an amended plan
    • Proceeds from the surrender, cancellation, or redemption of an income-averaging annuity contract (IAAC)

    • If the payment is not one of the following, the payment is not considered a lump-sum payment. Do not continue to next step.

    • If the payment is one of the above, the payment is considered a lump-sum payment.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if any exceptions apply to the lum-sum payment you provide .

  2. Determine if any exceptions apply to the lump-sum payment you provide

    The following lump-sum payments you provide have different withholding or reporting requirements:

    • Payments of retiring allowances
    • Payments related to death benefits
    • Payments to First Nations workers
    • Lump-sum payment transferred directly from an RPP or DPSP

    • If one of the above exceptions applies, do not continue to next step.

      Learn more on the above exceptions using the links depending on the payment you provide.

    • If one of the above payments does not apply, continue to: Step 3 - Determine if the payment is a qualifying retroactive lump-sum payment (QRLP)

  3. Determine if the payment is a qualifying retroactive lump-sum payment (QRLP)

    Recipients may be eligible for a special tax calculation that is more beneficial for a QRLSP, regardless of the amount of tax you withhold from the payment.

    Since a retroactive lump-sum payment must be reported for the year in which it was received, the tax payable may be higher than it would have been if the portions of the payment relating to previous years had been reported and taxed for the relevant year.

    A lump-sum payment must meet all of the following conditions to be a QRLSP:

    • Payment is a qualifying amount
      Qualifying amounts

      A qualifying amount is the principal portion of a payment or benefit that is eligible for the special tax calculation:

      Eligible source of income are one of the following:

      • Income from an office or employment received under one of the following:
        • An order or judgment from a court or other competent tribunal
        • An arbitration award
        • A lawsuit settlement agreement (including damages for loss of office or employment)
      • Benefits from unemployment insurance or employment insurance
      • Benefits from a superannuation or pension plan (including from a foreign pension plan and Old Age Security) otherwise payable on a periodic basis
      • Spousal, common-law partner, or taxable child support payments
      • Benefits from a wage-loss replacement plan
      • Canadian Forces members' and veterans' income replacement benefits
      Amounts not qualifying

      An amount received under any part of a retroactive lump-sum payment that is eligible for any of the following deductions, is not a qualifying amount:

      • Legal expenses
      • Salary reimbursements
      • Reimbursement of top-up disability payments
      • Repayment of pension or benefits
      • Deduction of social assistance payments, workers' compensation benefits

      An amount under normal collective bargaining, such as negotiated back pay, is not a qualifying amount because it is not paid under a court order or arbitration award. A different tax treatment may apply if the employee is deceased.

    • Payment has been made after 1994
    • Payment relates to one or more prior eligible tax years
      What is an eligible tax year

      An eligible tax year is a previous tax year that ended after 1977, during which the recipient:

      • Was a resident of Canada
      • Did not become a bankrupt during the calendar year
      • Did not include the year in a farmer/fisher election for income averaging
      • Filed an income tax and benefit return for the year.

      The portion of QRLSP related to an ineligible tax year is always included in the current year in the special tax calculation.

    • The sum of the payments relating to the preceding years is at least $3,000 not including interest (if a person receives more than one type of QRLSP in a year, the $3,000 threshold applies to the total amount of QRLSP, not to each individually)

    • If the payment does not meet all of the above conditions, it is not a QRLSP. It is a lump-sum payment.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Withhold and report the payment on a slip

    • If the payment meets all of the above conditions, it is a QRLSP.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if you need to fill out Form T1198

  4. Determine if you need to fill out Form T1198

    If the payment is a QRLSP, the recipient can request a special income tax calculation be applied to their income tax and benefit return.

    The recipient must receive from you (as the payer) one of the following:

    • A completed Form T1198, Statement of Qualifying Retroactive Lump-Sum Payment

    • A letter with the following information:

      • The year in which the lump-sum payment was made to the recipient
      • A complete description of the lump-sum payment and the circumstances that required it to be paid
      • The total amount of the lump-sum payment, including a breakdown between the principal and the interest element, if any, of the payment
      • The principal amount of the lump-sum payment that relates to the current year and each of the preceding years covered by the payment

    It is the recipient's responsibility to send Form T1198 or the letter from the payer to their tax centre.

  5. Withhold deductions on the lump-sum payment paid to the recipient

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions on the lump-sum payment paid to the recipient:

    • Lump-sum payment that is not a QRLSP

      • Income tax - Do not withhold
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • CPP - Do not withhold

    • QRLSP

      • Income tax - Withhold only on taxable income
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • CPP - Do not withhold

    Learn more: How to calculate payroll deductions and contributions

  6. Report the lump-sum payment on a slip

    You must withhold the following deductions and report on a slip depending on who the payment is made to:

    QRLSP: CPP, EI, and income tax are deducted if the income is from an office or employment. The deductions are calculated using the using the method for Bonuses, retroactive pay increases or irregular amounts

    T4 slip
    • Do not withhold CPP, EI or income tax deductions
    • Report the payments on a T4 slip in box 14 -

    Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted tax from the payment.

    Learn more on when and how to report: T4 slip - Information for employers

    T4A slip

    Generally, you must report the following on a T4A slip the lump-sum payment made to the recipient:

    • Withhold and remit income tax on the payment XXX
    • Do not withhold CPP and EI deductions
    • Report the payment for services on a T4A slip in:
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 016 - Pension or superannuation
      Lump-sum payments
      • Box 018 – Lump-sum payments
      • Code 102 – Lump-sum payments – non-resident services transferred under paragraph 60(j)
      • Code 108 – Lump-sum payments from a registered pension plan (RPP) that you cannot transfer
      • Code 110 – Lump-sum payments accrued to December 31, 1971
      • Code 158 – Lump-sum payments that you cannot transfer that are not reported elsewhere
      • Code 180 – Lump-sum payments from a deferred profit-sharing plan (DPSP) that you cannot transfer
      • Code 190 – Lump-sum payments from an unregistered plan

    Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted tax from the payment.

    Learn more: T4A slip - Information for payers

    T4E slip
    • Withhold and remit income tax on the payment for services at the rate of 15%
    • Do not withhold CPP and EI deductions
    • Report the payment for services on a T4A-NR slip in:
      • box 18 - Gross amount
      • box 22 - Income tax deducted
    • Do not include GST/HST and PST in the amount reported in box 18 or box 22

    The CRA administrative policy does not apply where tax is withheld. A slip must be issued if any tax is withheld, even if the total of all payments is under $500.
