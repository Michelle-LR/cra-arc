Payments to barbers and hairdressers

Content to be covered and in development based on fishers: Barbers and hairdressers, taxi drivers and drivers of other passenger-carrying vehicles

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

You may provide payments to barbers and hairdressers that are self-employed. Depending on the situation, you may be considered a deemed employer.

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if the worker is considered a self-employed barber or hairdresser

    Barbers and hairdressers who provide their services in an establishment that offers barbering or hairdressing services can either be:

    • Self-employed
    • Employee
    Types of workers that are not considerd barbers or hairdressers for the purpose of employment insurance

    The following workers who provide services in a barbershop, hair salon, spa, or similar establishment are not considered barbers or hairdressers for the purpose of EI:

    • Manicurists
    • Beauticians
    • Massage therapists

    • If the worker is not a self-employed barber of hairdresser, do not continue to next step.

      You must deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees.

      Learn more: How to calculate

      If you are not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed for CPP/EI purposes

      You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an employee or is self-employed. You can use the CRA guidance to help you gather the facts based on your situation.

      Learn more: Employee or Self-employed

    • If the worker is a self-employed barber or hairdresser, continue to Step 2 - Determine if you are a deemed employer.

  2. Determine if you are a deemed employer

    You are a deemed employer if all of the following conditions apply:

    • The worker is in the occupation of a barber or a hairdresser
    • The barber or hairdresser provides services normally provided in a barbering or hairdressing establishment
    • The barber or hairdresser is not the owner or operator of the establishment
    Example of employment that meets all conditions

    Generally, the barbers or hairdressers:

    • The barber or hairdresser is not an employee (not employed under a contract of service).
    • The barber or hairdresser enters into an agreement with the owner or operator of a barbering or hairdressing establishment as a “chair renter”.
    • The “chair renter” pays the owner or operator of the establishment a fixed amount each month for the exclusive use of a chair and access to the sinks and stationary hair dryers.
    • The “chair renter” supplies their own combs, brushes, scissors, and other small hand instruments, as well as products used in the performing of their service.
    • The “chair renter” has their own customers and makes their own appointments.
    • The “chair renter” has their own customers and makes their own appointments.
    • The “chair renter” is normally seen as running their own business and considered self-employed.

    In this example the “chair renter” meets the conditions of the EIR as stated above, and is considered employed in insurable employment.

    • If you are not a deemed employer, do not continue to next step.

      You do not withhold CPP , EI or income tax deductions because the worker is considered self-employed.

    • If you are a deemed employer, continue to Step 3 - Review your responsibilities as a deemed employer.

  3. Review your responsibilities as a deemed employer

    If you are a deemed employer of self-employed barber or hairdresser, including self-employed First Nations barbers and hairdressers whose earnings are tax-exempt, you are responsible to:

    • Make appropriate deductions depending on the circumstances of the barber or hairdresser (step 5)
    • Calculate the insurable earnings of each barber or hairdresser (step 6)
    • Report the payment on a T4 slip (step 7)
    • Keep records (step 4)

  4. Determine the records you must keep to support your calculation of the insurable earnings

    If you are a deemed employer, you have to keep your books, records, accounts, and documents for the barber or hairdresser separate from those of other insured persons to support the following:

    • The earnings of the barber or hairdresser
    • The EI premiums you have to remit
    • The dates on which the EI premiums are payable

    Your records should include:

    • The name, address, and social insurance number of each barber or hairdresser
    • The amount and date of each barber or hairdresser's insurable earnings for the period
    • The amount and date the EI premiums are payable

    Do not send your records, but keep them in case the CRA asks to see them.

    Learn more: Keeping records.

    What happens if your records do not support the insurable earnings

    If there is no record of the hours the barber or hairdresser worked but the parties agree on the hours normally worked and those hours are reasonable, the insurable hours will be based on the agreed to hours.

    If the parties do not agree on the hours normally worked or if the hours are not reasonable, the insurable hours will be based on an analysis of the working conditions and the number of hours normally required to perform similar work.

    Insurable hours

    The Employment Insurance Regulations provides how to determine the hours of insurable employment.

    Learn more: Insurable hours - CPP/EI explained.

  5. Determine which deductions you need to withhold

    Generally, if you pay amounts to barbers or hairdressers that are self-employed, you must withhold the following deductions:

    • Income tax
      Do not withhold income tax.
    • EI premiums
      Under special EI regulations, you must withhold EI premiums on behalf of the worker.

      You have to remit your employee's share and your share of the EI premiums.

    • CPP contributions
      Do not withhold CPP contributions.

      Self-employed barbers or hairdressers are responsible for remitting CPP contributions in the same way as other self-employed individuals.

  6. How to calculate insurable earnings

    The insurable earnings of a barber or hairdresser are calculated based on the net revenue.

    Your calculation of the insurable earnings of the worker for a week depends if you know or not the barber or hairdresser’s actual earnings and expenses.

    • Calculate if you know the actual earnings and expenses

      If you know how much the worker earned in a pay period and the expenses incurred in generating revenue from the worker’s operation in the establishment:

      The amount of insurable earnings is the total actual earnings (net revenue) from the barber or hairdresser’s employment for the pay period up to the maximum annual insurable earnings.

    • Calculate if you do not know the actual earnings and expenses

      If you do not know how much the worker earned in a week or the expenses the worker incurred in generating revenue from their operation in the establishment during a pay period, the amount of insurable earnings is the lesser of:

        • Working days per week

    • If the payments are made to self-employed First Nations barbers or hairdressers, do not continue to next step.

      Learn more on how exemptions applies to: Payments to First Nations workers.

    • If the payments are not made to self-employed First Nations barbers or hairdressers, continue to Step 7 - Report the payment on a T4 slip.

  7. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    Depending on the self-employed barber or hairdresser’s situation, you must report the following amounts on a T4 slip:

    • Box 29 - Employment code 13
    • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings if barber or hairdresser worked in a province other than Quebec
    • Box 56 - PPIP insurable earnings if barber or hairdresser worked in the province of Quebec
    • Code 83 - Barbers or hairdressers – Gross income
    • Code 88 - Indian (exempt income) – Self-employment

    Learn more: How to report - T4 slip - Information for employers
