Payments to First Nations workers

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement or to the CRA administrative policy.

You must report all income and deductions on a T4 or T4A slip for First Nations wokers regardless of whether the income is taxable or exempt.

Many Indigenous people in Canada prefer not to describe themselves as "Indians" and view this term as rooted in colonialism and racism. Under the Indian Act, the precise legal meaning of the term "Indian" refers to First Nations persons who are entitled to registration.

Steps

  1. Determine if the worker is registered with Indigenous Services Canada (ISC)

    You must determine if the worker is registered with ISC and their effective date for registration.

    The CRA requires proof of registration with ISC. The effective date for registration means the date that the First Nations worker is eligible for the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act on the income they earn on a reserve.

    ISC is responsible for the Indian Register and is the only one authorized to issue a "certificate of Indian status" to an individual.

    Learn more on how to prove registration from ISC: How to prove registration

    • If the worker does not have proof of registration, do not continue to next step.

      Deduct CPP, EI and income tax in the same as for your other workers. The exemption under section 87 only applies to a worker who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    • If the worker has proof of registration, continue to Step 2 - Determine if ???.

  2. Determine the residency of the worker or payer

    Situation: Your employee lives on a reserve and you are a resident on a reserve

    Exempt income situation

    The status Indian individual's (status confirmed in step 1) income is exempt from income tax under section 87 of the Indian Act if all of the following apply:

    • Employee lives on a reserve (this means it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine)
    • Employer is a resident on a reserve (this means the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located)

    To find out what payroll deductions you have to withhold on exempt income in this situation, continue to: Step 4a: Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

    Examples where exempt

    Example 1

    Mrs. H works for a logging company that is resident on a reserve. Mrs. H lives on a reserve, but performs the duties at lumber camps located off the reserve. The employment income is exempt from income tax because the place where the employee lives and the residence of their employer are factors connecting the income to a reserve.

    Example 2

    Mr. I is a construction worker employed by a construction company that is resident on a reserve, to work on building sites that are not located on a reserve. Mr. I lives on a reserve, except for short periods each year when he lives near the construction sites. When away from the reserve, he retains his residence on the reserve where his family lives. Mr. I is exempt from income tax on his employment income because the place where he lives and the residence of his employer are factors connecting his employment income to a reserve.

    Examples where not exempt

    Example 1

    Ms. J lives on a reserve and works as an accountant at the head office of a bank located off a reserve in a nearby city. The bank maintains a branch on Ms. J's reserve and arranges for her to be paid at that branch. Ms. J's employment income is taxable because the only substantive factor linking her employment income to a reserve is her residence which, without other substantial connecting factors, does not result in exemption. Payment on the reserve is viewed as a connecting factor of relatively little weight.

    Example 2

    Mr. K is a construction worker employed by a corporation situated on a reserve. The corporation is an employment agency that makes employees available to work for clients that are not situated on a reserve, on building sites not located on a reserve. Mr. K lives on a reserve. The employment agency had an office on the reserve, but carried out very few of its business activities on the reserve. The guidelines would not apply in this case, and one must therefore do an analysis of the connecting factors. Mr. K's employment income is taxable because the residence of the employer in this case has little weight as a connecting factor. The only other substantive factor connecting Mr. K's employment income to a reserve is his residence, which is not sufficient to bring the employment income within the exemption.

    Example 3

    Ms. B lives on a reserve and works as director general on that reserve for their corporation (of which they are a shareholder). Their administrative offices are located on reserve but the work is carried out mostly off the reserve. Ms. B receives a salary from their corporation. This salary is exempt. Ms. B pays themselves a bonus on top of their salary. The bonus is based on the company's performance. The connecting factor of the bonus is not sufficient to bring that income within the exemption.

    Learn more on how to report amounts on a T4 slip when you pay tax-exempt or partly tax-exempt employment income to your employee: How to report income paid for Indian employees - T4 slip - Information for employers

    Where an employee is a shareholder who controls the business (non-arm's length with the corporation), the source of the business income may be relevant in determining whether the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act applies to the income received in the shareholder's capacity as an employee. Where the source of the business income in this situation is off the reserve, the employment income (salary and bonuses) will not be considered to be connected to the reserve and will not be exempt from tax.

    Learn more: Guideline 2

    Situation: Other situations where your employee is required to perform duties on a reserve

    Depending on your situation, the employment income of status Indians may be exempt or partly exempt from tax. It is the income that is exempt from tax and not the person.

    The rules for CPP and EI are different. EI premiums are always required. Income that is exempt from income tax is not pensionable employment although you or the employee may elect into the CPP.

    To determine if the income is exempt or partly from tax in this situation, continue to: Step 3: Determine the percentage of duties related to employment on a reserve.

  3. Determine the percentage of duties related to employment on a reserve

    To determine the portion of employment duties performed on a reserve, two factors are generally taken into account:

    • Whether an actual "duty of employment" was performed (the employee was required to perform the particular task and was compensated by the employer for performing that particular task)
    • The extent to which the duty of employment was performed on a reserve
    How to calculate the percentage of duties related to employment performed on and off a reserve
    • Total working days on a reserve (do not include vacation or sick leave)
    • divide by Total working days
    • equals Result
    • multiply by 100
    • equals This is the % of duties of employment performed on reserve (paid leave such as vacation or sick leave is also exempt)
    Calculation example

    An employer requires their status Indian employee to perform duties related to their employment both on and off reserve. The employee had a total of 220 working days in the year, 44 of which were on reserve. These days spent working on the reserve did not include either vacation or sick leave.

    The percentage of the status Indian employee’s duties related to employment performed on a reserve is calculated as follows:

    • 44 total Working days on a reserve (not including vacation or sick leave)
    • divide by 220 total working days
    • equals 0.2 is the result
    • multiply by 100
    • equals 20% is the percentage of duties of employment performed on reserve (paid leave such as vacation or sick leave is also exempt)

    Residency situation

    • Employee lives on a reserve or you are a resident on a reserve, continue to Step 3a
    • Employee does not live on a reserve and you are not resident on a reserve but the employee is required to perform duties on a reserve, continue to Step 3b
    1. Employee lives on a reserve or you are a resident on a reserve
      Situation: Your employee performs more than 50% of their employment duties on a reserve

      Exempt income situation

      The status Indian individual's (status confirmed in step 1) income is exempt from income tax under section 87 of the Indian Act if all of the following apply:

      • Employer or the employee is in one of the following situations:
        • Employee lives on a reserve (this means it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine)
        • Employer is a resident on a reserve (this means the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located)
      • Employee performs more than 50% of their employment duties on a reserve

      To find out what payroll deductions you have to withhold on exempt income in this situation, continue to: Step 4a: Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

      Learn more on how to report amounts on a T4 slip when you pay tax-exempt or partly tax-exempt employment income to your employee: How to report income paid for Indian employees - T4 slip - Information for employers

      Example where exempt

      Ms. L lives on a reserve and works as a policewoman for an off-reserve employer. She performs more than one-half of her duties on a reserve, and she has an additional connecting factor in that she lives on the reserve.

      All of her employment income from this employer is exempt in this situation.

      Example where not exempt

      Ms. M lives off reserve in a town where she works in a restaurant owned by a corporation resident on a reserve. Once a week, she drives to the reserve to pick up the pay cheques for the restaurant staff.

      All of her employment income from this employer is taxable because the only factor connecting the income to a reserve is the residence of the employer and, without other connecting factors, this is not sufficient for the exemption to apply.

      Learn more: Guideline 3

      Situation: Your employee performs 50% or less of their employment duties on a reserve

      Partly-exempt income

      The status Indian individual's (status confirmed in step 1) income is partly-exempt from income tax under section 87 of the Indian Act if if the employee performs 50% or less of their employment duties on a reserve. The exemption may be prorated where those duties are not merely incidental to duties they performed off a reserve. The exemption will apply only to the portion of the income related to the duties the employee performed on the reserve if all of the following apply:

      • Employer or the employee is in one of the following situations:
        • Employee lives on a reserve (this means it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine)
        • Employer is a resident on a reserve (this means the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located)

      To find out what payroll deductions you have to withhold on partly-exempt income in this situation, continue to: Step 4b: Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

      Examples where exempt

      Example 1

      Mr. E lives off a reserve and works for a government department, mostly at a location off a reserve. As part of his position, he spends one-half day per week on a regular basis working in a clinic on a reserve.

      Since one- tenth of Mr. E's work is performed on a reserve, 10% of Mr. E's income from this employment is exempt. The other 90% of his income is taxable.

      Example 2

      Ms. F lives off a reserve and works for a boys and girls club at a location off a reserve. Each summer, Ms. F works for the club at a one-month camp operated on a reserve.

      The income Ms. F earns at the summer camp operated on the reserve is exempt.

      Example where not exempt

      Mrs. G lives off a reserve and works for a stationery store located off a reserve. On rare occasions, Mrs. G delivers some of the store's goods to customers on a reserve.

      None of her income is exempt. The trips to the reserve are occasional and are not a meaningful connecting factor. All her employment income from this employer is taxable.

      Learn more: Guideline 1 and Proration rule

      You are an Indian band or organization and your employee’s duties of employment are in connection with your non-commercial activities carried on exclusively for the benefit of Indians who for the most part live on reserves

      Exempt income situation

      The status Indian individual's (status confirmed in step 1) income is exempt from income tax under section 87 of the Indian Act if all of the following apply:

      • Employer is one of the following:
        • An Indian band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing one or more Indian bands that have reserves
        • An Indian organization controlled by an Indian band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing one or more Indian bands that have reserves, and the organization is dedicated exclusively to the social, cultural, educational, or economic development of Indians who for the most part live on reserves
      • Employee's duties of employment are in connection with the employer's non-commercial activities carried on exclusively for the benefit of Indians who for the most part live on reserves

      To find out what payroll deductions you have to withhold on exempt income in this situation, continue to: Step 4a: Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

      Examples where exempt

      Example 1

      Mr. N works for the education department of a tribal council at an off-reserve location central to several reserves. Mr. N lives off a reserve and the tribal council is resident on one of the reserves.

      All the employment income of Mr. N from this employer is exempt from income tax because the duties he performs for the tribal council are connected to the reserves served by the council, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Example 2

      Ms. O is a librarian working for a technical training institute operated by five Indian bands for Indians who live on a reserve. The administrative office of the institute is on a reserve. Ms. O lives off a reserve and works at an off-reserve location central to several reserves.

      All the employment income of Ms. O from this employer is exempt from income tax because the duties she performs for the Indian organization that employs her are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Example 3

      Mr. P works for an Indian organization providing child and family related services to members of a large number of bands with reserves scattered over a large area within a province. Some of these services are provided in the provincial capital, where Mr. P works, and the organization's administrative office is at an off-reserve location central to the bands served. However, the organization's directors, consisting of the band chiefs, meet at each reserve in rotation.

      All the employment income of Mr. P from this employer is exempt from income tax because the duties he performs for the Indian organization that employs him are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Example 4

      Ms. Q works for a company that is resident on a reserve and owned by a tribal council. The company exists to provide adequate low-cost housing on reserves and the company's property is for the most part on reserves. Ms. Q performs her duties at an off-reserve location central to several reserves.

      All the employment income of Ms. Q from this employer is exempt from income tax because the duties she performs for the Indian organization that employs her are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Example 5

      Mr. R works for an Indian organization that manages funds provided by the government to assist in planning for future education needs on reserves. His employer is resident on a reserve, but he works at a location off a reserve.

      All the employment income of Mr. R from this employer is exempt from income tax because the duties he performs for the Indian organization that employs him are connected to the reserves served by the Indian organization, and the employer is resident on a reserve.

      Examples where not exempt

      Example 1

      Mr. S works for a commercial building supplies company that is owned by a tribal council and is resident on a reserve. He performs his duties off a reserve and lives off a reserve.

      All the employment income of Mr. S from this employer is taxable because, although there is one factor, the residence of the employer, connecting the income to a reserve, this factor by itself is not sufficient to confer the exemption when the employer and the employee are active in the commercial mainstream of society.

      Example 2

      Ms. T works for an Indian organization dedicated to organizing social programs for off-reserve Indians. The organization is located off a reserve.

      All the employment income of Ms. T from this employer is taxable because there are no factors connecting that income to a location on a reserve.

      Learn more on how to report amounts on a T4 slip when you pay tax-exempt or partly tax-exempt employment income to your employee: How to report income paid for Indian employees - T4 slip - Information for employers

      Learn more: Guideline 4

    2. Employee does not live on a reserve and you are not resident on a reserve but the employee is required to perform duties on a reserve
      Situation: Your employee performs at least 90% of the duties related to their employment on a reserve

      Exempt income situation

      The status Indian individual's (status confirmed in step 1) income is exempt from income tax under section 87 of the Indian Act if the employee performs at least 90% of the duties related to their employment on a reserve.

      To find out what payroll deductions you have to withhold on exempt income in this situation, continue to: Step 4a: Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

      Examples where exempt

      Example 1

      Mr. A lives off a reserve and works as a mechanic for an automobile repair shop, performing his duties in a garage located on a reserve.

      All the employment income he receives from this employer is exempt because the duties are performed on a reserve.

      Example 2

      Mr. B lives off a reserve and works as a driver for a heating-oil supplier, performing almost all of his duties making deliveries to houses on a reserve.

      All the employment income he receives from this employer is exempt because more than 90% of the duties are performed on a reserve.

      Example 3

      Ms. C lives off a reserve and works in an office on a reserve. Her duties include a daily drive into a nearby town where she does the banking for the business and picks up the mail and supplies for the business.

      All the employment income she receives from this employer is exempt, although she incidentally leaves the reserve in the course of carrying out her duties.

      Example where not exempt

      Mr. D works for a logging company that is not resident on a reserve, cutting trees under license on provincial Crown land.

      All the employment income he receives from this employer is taxable because the land does not form part of a reserve.

      Mr. D lives on a reserve, but this factor alone is not sufficient to connect the income to a location on a reserve.

      Learn more on how to report amounts on a T4 slip when you pay tax-exempt or partly tax-exempt employment income to your employee: How to report income paid for Indian employees - T4 slip - Information for employers

      Situation: Your employee performs certain duties related to their employment on a reserve

      Partly-exempt income

      The status Indian individual's (status confirmed in step 1) income is partly-exempt from income tax under section 87 of the Indian Act. The exemption may be prorated only to the portion of the income related to the duties the employee performed on the reserve if the employee performs certain duties related to their employment on a reserve.

      To find out what payroll deductions you have to withhold on partly-exempt income in this situation, continue to: Step 4b: Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST.

      Examples where partly exempt

      Example 1

      Mr. E lives off a reserve and works for a government department, mostly at a location off a reserve. As part of his position, he spends one-half day per week on a regular basis working in a clinic on a reserve.

      Since one-tenth of Mr. E's work is performed on a reserve, 10% of Mr. E's income from this employment is exempt. The other 90% of his income is taxable.

      Ms. F lives off a reserve and works for a boys and girls club at a location off a reserve. Each summer, Ms. F works for the club at a one-month camp operated on a reserve.

      The income Ms. F earns at the summer camp operated on the reserve is exempt.

      Example not exempt

      Mrs. G lives off a reserve and works for a stationery store located off a reserve. On rare occasions, Mrs. G delivers some of the store's goods to customers on reserve. None of her income is exempt. The trips to the reserve are merely occasional and are not a meaningful connecting factor.

  4. Withhold and report the payment on the slip

    The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

    You must withhold the following deductions and report on a slip depending on who the payment is made to:

    1. Exempt income situation

      • Salary, wages and taxable benefits in cash: Option 1

        Withhold:

        • CPP, only if the employer filed a CPT124 election
        • EI

        Remit:

        • GST/HST - Reimbursements
        • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

      • Non-cash and near-cash taxable benefits: Option 2

        Withhold:

        • Income tax (do not withhold)
        • CPP, only if the employer filed a CPT124 election
        • EI (do not withhold)

        Remit:

        • GST/HST

      CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed a CPT124 election - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

      If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, an Indian can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

      Learn more on how to calculate deductions and the GST/HST to remit on benefits: How to calculate - Calculate payroll deductions and contributions

    2. Partly-exempt income

      • Salary, wages and taxable benefits in cash: Option 1

        Withhold:

        • Income tax, on the portion of the income which is taxable
        • CPP, on the portion of the income which is taxable or all income if employer filed a CPT124 election
        • EI

        Remit:

        • GST/HST - Reimbursements
        • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

      • Non-cash and near-cash taxable benefits: Option 2

        Withhold:

        • Income tax, on the portion of the income which is taxable
        • CPP, on the portion of income which is taxable or all income if employer filed a CPT124 election
        • EI (do not withhold)

        Remit:

        • GST/HST

      CPP is only withheld on the portion of the income that is taxable. Where the income is not taxable, CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed a CPT124 election - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

      If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, an Indian can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

References

Common terms

Terms used on this page
Employer is resident on a reserve

"Employer is resident on a reserve" means that the reserve is the place where the central management and control over the employer organization is actually located.

The central management and control of an organization is usually considered to be exercised by the group that performs the function of a board of directors of the organization. However, the real management and control of an organization may be exercised by some other person or group. Generally, management and control is exercised at the principal place of business but it is recognized that this function may be legitimately exercised in a place other than the principal administrative office of the organization. It is a question of fact where the central management and control is exercised.

Indian
"Indian" means an Indian as defined for the purposes of the Indian Act. An Indian is a person who is registered or entitled to be registered as an Indian under the Indian Act.
Indian lives on the reserve
"Indian lives on the reserve" means the Indian lives on the reserve in a domestic establishment that is their principal place of residence and that is the centre of their daily routine.
On a reserve
"On a reserve" means on a reserve as defined under the Indian Act, including all settlements deemed to be reserves for purposes of the Indian Settlements Remission Order, and any other areas given similar treatment under federal legislation (for example, Category I-A lands under the Cree-Naskapi (of Quebec) Act).
Social, cultural, educational or economic development
"Social, cultural, educational or economic development" includes the provision of social services such as health care or counselling.
