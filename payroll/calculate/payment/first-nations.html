Payments to First Nations workers

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement or to the CRA administrative policy.

You must report all income and deductions on a T4 or T4A slip for First Nations workers regardless of whether the income is taxable or exempt.

The term First Nations employee or worker on this page refers only to an employee or a worker who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

Many Indigenous people in Canada prefer not to describe themselves as Indians and view this term as rooted in colonialism and racism. Under the Indian Act, the precise legal meaning of the term "Indian" refers to First Nations persons who are entitled to registration.

Determine your withholding and reporting obligations based on the type of income you paid to the worker:

  • Employment income
  • Employment-related income (for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP)
  • Self-employed fishing, barber, hairdresser or driver income
  • Self-employed farming and other self-employed income
What if you do not know if a worker is an employee or is self-employed

If you or an individual working for you is not sure of the worker's employment status (employee or self-employed), either one of you can request a CPP/EI ruling to determine the status and whether the employment or the earnings are pensionable, insurable or both.

Employment income

Steps

  1. Determine if the First Nations employment income is exempt from tax

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    To determine if the First Nations employee’s employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt, ask your employee to fill out part 1 of Form TD1-IN, Determination of Exemption of an Indian's Employment Income to certify they meet all of the following conditions for the exemption:

    • Your employee is registered under the Indian Act
    • Your employee is not a treaty beneficiary of a First Nation with a final or self-government agreement that ends the tax exemption under section 87 of the Indian Act
    • If your employee lives on a reserve, it is their principal place of residence and is the centre of their daily routine

    You must also certify that information you provided on Form TD1-IN is correct and complete.

    What if the First Nations employee's employment situation changes

    If the facts of the First Nations employee's employment situation change over the course of the year, you may need to reexamine whether the tax exemption will still apply. Changes that requires you to reexamine whether the income will be exempt from tax for the remainder of the year include:

    • A change in the First Nations employee’s residence, on or off reserve
    • A change in the employer’s residence, on or off reserve
    • A change in the location of the employment duties

    Learn more about registration under the Indian Act and the Indian Register:

    • From ISC: How to prove registration
    • From CIRNAC: Treaty and agreement negotiations

    • If the employee does not meet all conditions for the exemption, do not continue to next step.

      You must deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees.

    • If the First Nations employee meets all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 2 - Determine if you can use the guidance to determine if the employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt.

  2. Determine if you can use the guidance to determine if the employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt

    You can use the employment income guidance for common employment situations. Depending on the situation, there are other uncommon factors that result in employment income being treated differently. This means that any factors connecting the income to a reserve must be analyzed based on various court decisions to determine if the exemption applies.

    For example, situations where a First Nations employee is also a shareholder are considered uncommon employment situations.

      You can call CRA at 1-800-959-8281.

      You must deduct CPP, EI and income tax as you would for your other employees until you receive confirmation from the CRA.

    • If your situation is common, continue to Step 3 - Use the guidance to determine if the employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt.

  3. Use the guidance to determine if the employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt

    You can use the employment income guidance for common employment situations to determine if the employment income is tax-exempt or partially tax-exempt.

    1. Determine if guideline 2 applies

      Your First Nations employee lives on a reserve and you (as the employer) are a resident on a reserve

      Generally, the income is exempt from income tax if all of the following apply:

      • First Nations employee lives on a reserve
      • Employer is a resident on a reserve

      Examples where exempt (guideline 2)

      Examples where not exempt (guideline 2)

      • If your First Nations employee does not live on a reserve or you (as the employer) are not a resident on a reserve, continue to Step 3b - Determine if guideline 4 applies.

    2. Determine if guideline 4 applies

      You (as the employer) are resident on a reserve and are a First Nations band or organization and your First Nations employee’s duties of employment are in connection with your non-commercial activities carried on exclusively for the benefit of First Nations individuals who for the most part live on reserves

      Generally, the income is exempt from income tax if you (as the employer) all of the following:

      • Resident on a reserve
        • A First Nations band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing one or more First Nations bands that have reserves
        • A First Nations organization controlled by a First Nations band which has a reserve, or a tribal council representing one or more First Nations bands that have reserves, and the organization is dedicated exclusively to the social, cultural, educational, or economic development of First Nations individuals who for the most part live on reserves
      • Employee's duties of employment are in connection with the employer's non-commercial activities carried on exclusively for the benefit of First Nations individuals who for the most part live on reserves

      Examples where exempt (guideline 4)

      Examples where not exempt (guideline 4)

      • If you (as the employer) are resident on a reserve and you meet all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations employee and income all exempt.
      • If the situation does not apply, continue to Step 3c - Determine the percentage of duties related to employment on a reserve.

    3. Determine the percentage of duties related to employment on a reserve

      To determine if guidelines 1 or 3 apply to the employment income, you need to determine the portion of employment duties performed on a reserve.

      The following 2 factors are generally taken into account when determining the portion of employment duties performed on a reserve:

      • Whether an actual "duty of employment" was performed (the First Nations employee was required to perform the particular task and was compensated by the employer for performing that particular task)
      • The extent to which the duty of employment was performed on a reserve
      How to calculate the percentage of duties related to employment performed on and off a reserve
      • Total working days on a reserve (do not include vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays)

      An employer requires their First Nations employee to perform duties related to their employment both on and off reserve. The employee had a total of 220 working days in the year, 44 of which were on reserve. These days spent working on the reserve did not include either vacation or sick leave.

      The percentage of the First Nations employee’s duties related to employment performed on a reserve is calculated as follows:

      • 44 total working days on a reserve (not including vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays)
      • divide by 220 total working days
      • equals 0.2 is the result
      • multiply by 100
      • equals 20% is the percentage of duties of employment performed on reserve (paid leave such as vacation days, sick days and statutory holidays is also exempt)

    4. Determine if guideline 3 applies

      First Nations employee lives on a reserve or you (as the employer) are resident on a reserve

      Generally, the income is exempt from income tax if all of the following apply:

        • First Nations employee lives on a reserve
        • Employer is a resident on a reserve
      • First Nations employee performs more than 50% of their employment duties on a reserve

      Example where exempt (guideline 3)

      Example where not exempt (guideline 3)

      • If your employee performs more than 50% of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations employee and income all exempt.
      • If your employee performs 50% or less of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 3e - Determine if guideline 1 applies.

    5. Determine if guideline 1 applies

      First Nations employee performs at least 90% of their employment duties on a reserve

      Generally, the income is exempt from income tax if the First Nations employee performs at least 90% of the duties related to their employment on a reserve.

      Examples where exempt (guideline 1)

      Example where not exempt (guideline 1)

      • If your First Nations employee performs at least 90% of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations employee and income all exempt.
      • If your First Nations employee performs certain duties of their employment duties on a reserve, continue to Step 3f - Calculate the proration of the employment income (guideline 1 - proration rule).

    6. Calculate the proration of the employment income (guideline 1 - proration rule)

      Your First Nations employee performs less than 90% of their employment duties on a reserve

      Generally, the income is partly-exempt from income tax if your First Nations employee performs less than 90% of the duties related to their employment on a reserve and the income is not exempt in other guidelines.

      The exemption is prorated to the portion of the income related to the duties the First Nations employee performed on the reserve.

      Example where not exempt (guideline 1 - proration rule)


      Continue to Step 4 - Withhold deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations employee and income partly-exempt.

  4. Withhold deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations employee

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations employee depending if the income is:

    • All exempt

      • Income tax - Do not withhold
      • EI - Withhold on all income
      • CPP - Withhold only if you filed Form CPT124

    • Partly-exempt

      • Income tax - Withhold only on taxable income
      • EI - Withhold on all income
      • CPP - Withhold only on taxable income or on all income if you filed Form CPT124

    CPP is only withheld on the portion of the income that is taxable. Where the income is not taxable, CPP is not withheld, except if the employer has filed Form CPT124 - Application to Cover the Employment of an Indian in Canada under the Canada Pension Plan whose Income is Exempt under the Income Tax Act.

    If the employer chooses not to apply for coverage, a First Nations employee can elect to pay CPP on tax-exempt earnings on Form CPT20, Election to Pay Canada Pension Plan Contributions when filing their personal income tax return.

  5. Report the payment on a T4 slip

    Generally, you must report on a T4 slip the employment income paid to your First Nations employee depending if the income is:

    • All exempt

      Income and source deductions
      • Code 71 - Report tax-exempt employment income
      • Boxes 16 and 26 - Report CPP contributions and pensionable earnings only if you filed Form CPT124
      • Boxes 18 and 24 - Report EI premiums and insurable earnings on all income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 20 - Do not report if you withheld RPP contributions (you must provide proof to your employee of RPP contributions for all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)
      • Code 69 - Report tax-exempt non-eligible retiring allowances you paid
      Union dues
      • Box 44 - Do not report if you withheld union dues (you must provide proof to your employee of union dues withheld from all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)

    • Partly-exempt

      Income and source deductions
      • Boxes 14 - Report only on taxable employment income
      • Code 71 - Report only tax-exempt employment income
      • Boxes 16 and 26 - Report CPP contributions and pensionable earnings only on taxable income or on all income if you filed Form CPT124
      • Boxes 18 and 24 Report EI premiums and insurable earnings on all income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 20 - Report only on taxable income if you withheld RPP contributions (you must provide proof to your employee of RPP contributions for all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)
      • Code 66 - Report only on taxable income if you paid eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 67 - Report only on taxable income if you paid non-eligible retiring allowances
      • Code 69 - Report only tax-exempt if you paid non-eligible retiring allowances
      Union dues
      • Box 44 - Report only on taxable income if you withheld union dues (you must provide proof to your employee of union dues withheld from all tax-exempt income, your employee will use this amount to fill out Form T90)
      Taxable benefits
      • Codes for taxable benefits (for example, code 40) - Report only on taxable income if you provided employment benefits

    Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted tax from the payment.

Employment-related income

Steps

  1. Determine if the employment-related income is exempt from tax

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    Generally, if the First Nations employee is receiving employment-related income, the employment-related income (for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP) is exempt from income tax when the payment is received as a result of employment income that was exempt from income tax.

    Type of employment-related income

    Example employment-related income

    • If the employment income was not exempt, do not continue to next step.

      You must deduct income tax as you would for your other employees.

    • If the employment income was exempt or partly-exempt, continue to Step 2 - Determine the percentage of employment-related income that was exempt.

  2. Determine the percentage of employment-related income that was exempt

    You need to determine the portion of the employment-related income of the First Nations employee that is related to tax-exempt employment income when the payment was received as a result of employment income.

    If the employment-related income is related to all tax-exempt First Nations employment income, the employment-related income will all be exempt.

    Generally, if the employment-related income is related to partly-exempt First Nations employment income, any employment-related income arising from the exempt share of that income will also be tax-exempt.

  3. Withhold deductions on the employment-related income paid to the First Nations recipient

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions on the employment-related income paid to the First Nations recipient depending if the income is:

    • All exempt

      • Income tax - Do not withhold
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • CPP - Do not withhold

    • Partly-exempt

      • Income tax - Withhold only on taxable income
      • EI - Do not withhold
      • CPP - Do not withhold

    Learn more: How to calculate payroll deductions and contributions

  4. Report the payment on a T4A slip

    Generally, you must report on a T4A slip the employment-related income paid to the First Nations recipient depending if the income is:

    • All exempt

      Other income
      • Code 144 - Report tax-exempt other income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Code 146 - Report tax-exempt pension or superannuation
      • Code 195 - Report tax-exempt PRPP payments
      Lump-sum payments
      • Code 148 - Report tax-exempt lump-sum payments

    • Partly-exempt

      Other income
      • Code 28 - Report taxable other income
      • Code 144 - Report tax-exempt other income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 016 - Report taxable pension or superannuation
      • Code 146 - Report tax-exempt pension or superannuation
      • Code 194 - Report taxable PRPP payments
      • Code 195 - Report tax-exempt PRPP payments
      Lump-sum payments
      • Box 018 - Report taxable lump-sum payments
      • Code 148 - Report tax-exempt lump-sum payments

    Under the CRA administrative policy, a slip must be issued if the total of all payments in the calendar year were more than $500 or you deducted tax from the payment.

Self-employed fishing, barber or hairdresser, driver income

Steps

  1. Determine if the self-employment income is exempt from tax

    The exemption from tax under section 87 of the Indian Act only applies to an individual who is registered or entitled to be registered under the Indian Act.

    The self-employment income earned by the First Nations worker must be situated on a reserve to be exempt from tax.

    To determine if the income is situated on a reserve, you must apply the connecting factors test to the income to meet all conditions for the exemption.

    Examples of business or self-employment income situated on a reserve

    How to apply the factors for self-employed fishing income

    You must apply the factors for self-employed fishing income for the connecting factors test.

    Examples of self-employment fishing income where exempt or not exempt

    • If the self-employment income is not considered to be situated on a reserve, do not continue to next step.

      You can call CRA at 1-800-959-8281 to confirm.

      Your remitting and reporting obligations are the same as other self-employed workers in the same industry.

    • If the self-employment income meets all conditions for the exemption, continue to Step 2 - Determine the percentage of income situated on a reserve.

  2. Determine the percentage of income situated on a reserve

    You need to determine the portion of the income-earning activities of the self-employed First Nations worker that takes place on a reserve.

    Generally, the income is partly-exempt from income tax if part of the self-employed First Nations worker's income-earning activities take place on reserve and part off of a reserve.

    The exemption is prorated to the portion of the income related to the income-earning activities on the reserve.

    If that self-employment income was partly-exempt from income tax, the business expenses arising from the exempt share of that income will also be exempt from income tax, unless another allocation can be shown to be more reasonable.

    • If the self-employed income is all tax-exempt, continue to Step 3 - Withhold and report the payment on a T4 slip and select income all exempt.
    • If the employment-related income is partly-exempt, continue to Step 3 - Withhold and report the payment on a T4 slip and select income is partly-exempt.

  3. Withhold deductions on the employment income paid to your First Nations worker

    Generally, you must withhold the following deductions on the self-employment income paid to the First Nations worker regardless if the income is exempt or partly-exempt:

    • Income tax - Do not withhold
    • EI - Withhold on all income (under special EI regulations, EI is remitted on behalf of the worker on all of their insurable earnings)
    • CPP - Do not withhold

      • Box 20 - Do not report if you withheld RPP contributions (you must provide proof to the self-employed worker of RPP contributions for all tax-exempt income, the self-employed worker will use this amount to fill out Form T90)
      Union dues
      • Box 44 - Do not report if you withheld union dues (you must provide proof to the self-employed worker of union dues withheld from all tax-exempt income, the self-employed worker will use this amount to fill out Form T90)

    • Partly-exempt

      Income and source deductions
      • Boxes 29 - Use the appropriate self-employment code
      • Boxes 18 and 24 - Report EI premiums and insurable earnings on all income
      • Codes 78, 79 and 80 - Report taxable income of self-employed fishers

      • Code 88 - Report tax-exempt self-employment income
      Pension or superannuation
      • Box 20 - Report only on taxable income if you withheld RPP contributions (you must provide proof to to the self-employed worker of RPP contributions for all tax-exempt income, the self-employed worker will use this amount to fill out Form T90)
      Union dues
      • Box 44 - Report only on taxable income if you withheld union dues (you must provide proof to to the self-employed worker of union dues withheld from all tax-exempt income, the self-employed worker will use this amount to fill out Form T90)
