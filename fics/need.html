What is needed from account holders

In order for the CRA to exchange financial account information with other jurisdictions, you may be asked by your financial institution to certify or clarify your tax residence, and to provide identifying information such as a Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) that is relevant to your jurisdiction of residence. Account holders who fail to comply with such a request may have their financial account information reported to the CRA.

Steps

  1. Know the types of account holders

    There are 2 types of account holders:

    Individual account holders
    Any individual who holds an account with a Canadian financial institution
    Entity account holders
    Any corporation, trust, partnership, and other organizations (hereafter referred to as entities) that hold an account with Canadian financial institutions

  2. Self-certify

    A self-certification is a declaration by the account holder that gives their identification details, tax residency, and any other information that may reasonably be required by financial institutions to fulfil its due diligence and reporting obligations.

    A self-certification can be obtained verbally or electronically. It can be provided as a stand-alone document, or it can be part of a more comprehensive document used by a financial institution in connection with the account opening. As long as the document allows the account holder to positively acknowledge by signature or other means that the self-certification is valid, the document can be considered a self-certification.

    Self-certification of individual account holders

    Financial institutions have to know the tax status of the account holders and others they deal with.

    If you are a new client at a financial institution, your financial institution will ask you:

    • To certify your tax residency and whether you are a non-resident of Canada or a U.S. citizen when you open your account
    • To provide documents to support any representation you make

    Your financial institution will confirm the reasonableness of this self-certification based on the information it gets.

    If your situation later changes in a way that suggests you may be a non-resident of Canada or a U.S. citizen (for example, you change your address to an address outside Canada), your financial institution must verify your residence status. This may include following up with you.

    The CRA has developed forms that financial institutions can use to collect the information they need to determine the tax residency of an account holder and of any controlling persons. However, financial institutions are free to collect the information in other ways, as long as they meet their reporting obligations.

    Determining residence status for Canadian tax purposes

    Under Canada's tax system, income tax obligations are based on your tax residence status. You need to know your tax residence status before you can determine your tax responsibilities. Residents of Canada are taxable in Canada on their worldwide income whereas non-residents are taxable in Canada only on their income from Canadian sources.

    Financial institutions have to know the tax residence of their account holders to ensure proper withholding and reporting. Therefore, financial institutions may ask you to certify or clarify your residence status for Canadian tax purposes. Tax residence is determined on a case by case basis. It is based on your situation and all the relevant facts. The relevant facts in determining your tax residence include:

    • Residential ties you have in Canada and abroad
    • The purpose and permanence of your stay in Canada and abroad

    Residential ties include social and economic ties, such as a home, personal belongings, and family.

    If you are an individual who normally lives in Canada, files an income tax and benefit return as a resident of Canada, and pays tax on worldwide income each year to Canada, you can inform your financial institution that you are a resident of Canada for tax purposes. However, even if you are a resident of Canada for tax purposes, if you are a U.S. citizen, you may have to inform your financial institution that you are also a U.S. citizen.

    If you have no significant residential ties with Canada and you have lived outside Canada during the year, you may be considered a non-resident of Canada.

    If you need help in determining your tax residence status, refer to: Determining your residency status

    Account holders with multiple residency

    If you are an individual who is a resident of Canada as well as another jurisdiction that Canada has a tax treaty with, the tie-breaker rules may help you to determine your jurisdiction of residence. You can find the tiebreaker rules under the residence article of the relevant tax treaty.

    For example, you may be a resident of Canada for tax purposes but you may also hold a green card (that is, a permanent resident card for the U.S.), in which case the U.S. considers you to be a U.S. resident. If you are a U.S. citizen, you may have to inform your financial institution that you are a U.S. citizen. However, if you are not a U.S. citizen and are considered a resident of Canada according to the tie-breaker rules under the Canada–U.S. tax treaty, you can tell your financial institution you are a resident of Canada and not a resident of the U.S. If you have relinquished your U.S. citizenship, your financial institution may ask individuals you for supporting documents.

    If you are an individual who is a resident of Canada and of another jurisdiction that Canada does not have a tax treaty with, you must inform your financial institution that you are a resident of both jurisdictions for tax purposes. Refer to: Canada's tax treaties

    Self-certification of entity account holders

    Financial institutions have to know the tax status of account holders and others they deal with.

    If you are a new client at a financial institution, your financial institution will ask you:

    • To certify your tax residency and whether you are a non-resident entity, what type of entity you are and whether you are an entity controlled by one or more non-residents or U.S. citizens when you open the account
    • To provide documents to support any representation you make

    Your financial institution will confirm the reasonableness of your self-certification based on the information provided.

    Your financial institution may follow up with you to verify your resident status or ask for a self-certification if:

    • Your situation later changes in a way that suggests you may be a non-resident of Canada or a specified U.S. person
    • You or a controlling person adds, substitutes, or changes information on your account (a controlling person can be a new administrator, a new major shareholder or a distribution to a discretionary beneficiary)
    • Any change or addition of information to any account associated with your account that affects the status of your account.

    The CRA has developed forms that financial institutions can use to collect the information they need to determine the tax residency of the account holder and of any persons who control the account. However, financial institutions are free to collect the information in other ways, as long as they meet their reporting obligations.

  3. Be aware of information sharing practices

    Designed to improve tax compliance, the two AEOI regimes Part XVIII (FATCA) and Part XIX (CRS) require Canadian financial institutions to collect certain information from their account holders.

    How financial institutions report on individual account holders

    Financial institutions have to report financial information with respect to most bank accounts, mutual funds, brokerage accounts, custodial accounts, annuity contracts (including segregated fund contracts), and some life insurance policies with a cash value that are held by non-residents of Canada or U.S. citizens.

    They do not have to report financial information with respect to certain accounts, including:

    • Registered retirement savings plans (RRSP)
    • Registered retirement income funds (RRIF)
    • Pooled registered pension plans (PRPP)
    • Registered pension plans (RPP)
    • Tax-free savings accounts (TFSA)
    • Registered disability savings plans (RDSP)
    • Registered education savings plans (RESP)
    • Deferred profit-sharing plans (DPSP)

    Due diligence procedures require Canadian financial institutions to identify account holders who are non-residents of Canada, or are U.S. citizens. As an individual account holder, you may be asked to certify or clarify your tax residence to your financial institution. This is done by asking accounts holders:

    • To declare their tax residence and whether they are non-residents of Canada or U.S. citizens
    • To produce documents such as a driver's licence for any representation they make

    If you are a U.S. resident or a U.S. citizen, the financial institution must give your financial account information to the CRA. The CRA then gives that information to the IRS. If you are a resident of a jurisdiction other than the U.S., the financial institution must also give your financial account information to the CRA. The CRA then gives the relevant financial information to CRS partner jurisdictions. In return, the IRS and CRS partner jurisdictions give the CRA similar information on Canadian residents who have financial accounts with financial institutions in their jurisdictions. The agreements are strictly information-sharing and do not involve any new or higher taxes. The sharing of information has no effect on foreign filing or reporting requirements.

    Learn more:

    • Intergovernmental Agreement for the Enhanced Exchange of Financial Account Information under the Canada-U.S. Tax Convention
    • Standard for Automatic Exchange of Financial Account Information in Tax Matters

    Account holders who fail to provide a self-certification or other information upon requests from their financial institution may have their financial account reported to the CRA. Safeguards are in place to ensure that the U.S. and our CRS partners treat the taxpayer information they receive as confidential and use it solely to administer tax laws.

    Information reported to the CRA and shared with the U.S. or CRS partner

    The account information the CRA collects and shares with the U.S. or the CRS partner includes the account holder's:

    • Name
    • Address
    • Jurisdiction of residence
    • Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN)
    • Date of birth (in some circumstances)
    • Account number, account balance or value, and amounts paid or credited to the account

    If a financial institution identifies a joint account as a reportable account, it will report the full value of the account to the CRA. Only information relating to non-residents joint account holders will be exchanged. Information relating to joint account holders who are residents of Canada and are not U.S. citizens will not be exchanged.

    Although a financial institution does not have to notify their account holders that it has reported their financial account information to the CRA, it must be open about their policies and procedures on reporting obligations. The Canadian financial institutions must be prepared to make this information available by allowing account holders to have access to the personal information that has been reported.

    As an account holder, you can also contact the CRA to find out if we have received your information or shared it with the U.S. or your jurisdiction of tax residence.

    How financial institutions report on entity account holders

    Due diligence procedures require financial institutions to identify entity account holders that are specified U.S. persons or reportable persons, controlled by U.S. residents or U.S. citizens or non-residents. This is done by asking for and reviewing information from:

    • Account holders
    • Account records
    • Publicly available sources

    To meet their tax reporting obligations with the CRA, Canadian financial institutions require certain information about entity account holders. Financial institutions have to determine:

    • Where an entity is resident for tax purposes
    • Whether the entity is a U.S. person and, if so, whether it is a specified U.S. person
    • Whether the entity is a non-resident person and, if so, whether it is a reportable person
    • Whether the entity is a financial institution and, if so, what type
    • Whether the entity is a non-financial entity (or a non-financial foreign entity) and, if so, whether it is a passive non-financial entity controlled by non-residents or a passive non-financial foreign entity controlled by U.S. residents or U.S. citizens

    Once the CRA receives this information it is shared with the IRS and partner jurisdictions.

    All of Canada's bilateral tax treaties contain strong safeguards to ensure that the IRS and CRS partner jurisdictions treat the taxpayer information they receive as confidential and use it only to administer tax laws. As well, both the IRS and CRS partner jurisdictions reciprocate by sending the CRA similar information on Canadian entities and entities controlled by Canadian residents holding accounts with financial institutions located in their jurisdictions.

    Account information that is shared includes the entity's:

    • Name
    • Address
    • Jurisdiction of residence
    • Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN)
    • Account number, account balance or value, and amounts paid or credited to the account

    If a non-financial entity or a non-financial foreign entity is identified as having a controlling person that is a non-resident or a specified U.S. person, the information that is shared includes the controlling person's:

    • Name
    • Address
    • Date of Birth (in certain cases)
    • Jurisdiction of residence
    • Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN)
