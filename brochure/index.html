Get a social insurance number (SIN) or tax number

A SIN is a 9-digit number that is personal, confidential, and unique to you.

You need a SIN to:

  • Receive benefits and credits
  • Work in Canada
  • Open most types of bank accounts

Apply for a SIN with Service Canada.

If Service Canada will not issue you a SIN, you may get a temporary tax number (TTN) from CRA

If Service Canada is unable to give you a permanent or temporary SIN, the CRA may give you a temporary tax number (TTN) you may use to get benefits and credits, file taxes, and sign up for CRA's online services.

How to get a TTN:

  • Complete your benefit or credit application or tax return without an account number. Attach a letter explaining why you do not have a SIN or can't get one.

  • To get a number faster, you may also include certified copies of documents that prove your identity, such as:

    • a passport
    • a driver's licence
    • a birth certificate or proof of birth
    • an immigration document showing that your spouse is sponsoring you (if your spouse has a SIN and is sponsoring you)
    • a letter from Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada that says they are reviewing your file

File a tax return to become eligible for payments

To qualify for certain payments, you need to file a tax return, even if you have little or no income, or no tax to pay. If you do not file, you will not receive payments you may otherwise be entitled to.

Situations where you must file: When you must file a tax return

Payments you may qualify for

By filing a tax return, you may be eligible for one or more of the following payments:

GST/HST Credit

Provides four tax-free payments per year. If eligible, single individuals can get up to $496 per year. A couple with two children could get up to $992 per year.

In addition: If you qualify for the GST/HST Credit, you automatically qualify for certain benefits that CRA administers on behalf of the provinces and territories.

Payments from the provinces and territories related to the GST/HST Credit

Based on where you live, you may also get the:

  • BC climate action tax credit
  • New Brunswick harmonized sales tax credit
  • Newfoundland and Labrador income supplement
  • Newfoundland and Labrador disability amount
  • Newfoundland and Labrador seniors' benefit
  • Northwest Territories cost of living offset
  • Nova Scotia affordable living tax credit
  • Nunavut carbon credit
  • Prince Edward Island sales tax credit
  • Saskatchewan low-income tax credit (SLITC)
  • Yukon Government carbon price rebate - Individuals

Canada child benefit (CCB)

Provides monthly tax-free money. Eligible families can get up to $7,437 per year for each child under 6 and up to $6,275 per year, for each child aged 6 to 17.

In addition: If you qualify for CCB, you automatically qualify for certain benefits that CRA administers on behalf of the provinces and territories.

Payments from the provinces and territories related to the CCB

Based on where you live, you may also get the:

  • Alberta child and family benefit
  • BC family benefit
  • New Brunswick child tax benefit
  • Newfoundland and Labrador child benefit (and pre-natal infant nutrition supplement)
  • Northwest Territories child benefit
  • Nova Scotia child benefit
  • Nunavut child benefit
  • Ontario child benefit (related to the Canada child benefit)
  • Yukon child benefit

Canada workers benefit (CWB)

An amount that helps those working and earning a low income to reduce any taxes owed and can result in a tax refund

Climate action incentive payment

A quarterly payment for residents of Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba and Ontario to offset the cost of the federal pollution pricing

Child disability benefit (CDB)

A tax-free monthly payment for families who care for a child under age 18 who has a severe impairment

Canada Dental Benefit

An interim tax-free payment for eligible families with children under 12 who do not have private dental insurance

Ontario trillium benefit (OTB)

A monthly payment that combines the Ontario and property tax credit, the Northern Ontario energy credit and the Ontario sales tax credit

Ontario senior homeowners' property tax grant

A grant to help offset property taxes for seniors who own their own home and have low to moderate incomes

Excise gasoline tax refund

A refund of part of the federal excise tax you pay on gasoline for those individuals who have a permanent mobility impairment and cannot safely use public transportation

Personal tax refunds

A payment for individual taxpayers who have a refund due on their Notice of assessment or reassessment

  • Use the Child and family benefits calculator to determine how much your payments from CRA may be
  • To continue getting payments, be sure to:
    • File your income tax and benefit return on time every year, even if you have no income in the year. If you have a spouse or common-law partner, they also have to file a return every year.
    • Keep your personal information updated with the CRA
    • Keep your supporting documents in case the CRA asks for them as part of the validation process

Doing your taxes

  1. Know what deductions to claim

    You should claim all the deductions you are entitied to. This lowers the amount of income tax you may owe.

    Claim credits and other allowances

    Depending on your situation, you may qualify to claim credits and allowances on your tax return. Often [community] claim the following:

    Disability Tax Credit (DTC)

    Provides a tax credit for people with impairments, or their supporting family member, that lowers the amount of income tax they may owe. Eligible individuals can claim up to $8,870, with an additional $5,174 for those under 18.

    Apply any time during the year.

    In addition: If you think you may be eligible for the DTC, you should apply. DTC eligibility opens the door to other federal, provincial, or territorial programs.

    Federal, provincial, or territorial programs related to the DTC
    • registered disability savings plan
    • Canada workers benefit disability supplement
    • child disability benefit
    • Multigenerational home renovation tax credit

    Home accessibility tax credit

    For eligible renovations to improve the accessibility of your home. A maximum of $20,000 per year in eligible expenses can be claimed.

Learn about Canadian taxes

Many of the benefits people enjoy in Canada are made possible through taxes. If you are an employee, your employer usually deducts taxes and pays them to the CRA for you.

Each year, people complete a tax return (Income Tax and Benefit Return) to find out if they owe tax or if they will get money back.

Understand how the Canadian tax system works: Learn about your taxes

Be aware of tax scams

Be cautious if you are contacted by someone that claims to be from the CRA and requests personal information such as your SIN, credit card number, bank account number, or passport number.

Scams may contact you by phone, email, mail or instant messaging.

We will never:

  • ask you to pay fees using prepaid credit cards, gift cards, or any other similar services
  • use aggressive language
  • threaten to harm, arrest, or deport you

Read more about scams and how to confirm if the CRA contacted you: Scam prevention and the CRA

Know your rights

You have 16 rights when dealing with the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA). These are known as the Taxpayer Bill of Rights. These rights build upon the CRA's corporate values of professionalism, respect, integrity, and collaboration.
